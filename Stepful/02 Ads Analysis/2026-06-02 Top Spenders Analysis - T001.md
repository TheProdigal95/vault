# 2026-06-02 Top Spenders Analysis - T001

## TLDR Key Learnings

> - **UGC-native is the only format that scales.** 18 of 20 top spenders are either UGC video or organic-looking statics. Zero polished "ad-looking" creatives made the top 20. The platform rewards content that looks like it belongs in a friend's feed.
> - **Black women testimonials are the conversion engine.** At least 10 of the 20 top spenders feature Black women in casual, self-filmed settings. This is the primary creative archetype — not a niche play. The language is casual, empowering, and identity-driven ("I stopped making excuses," "landed my first CPHT job!!").
> - **Deliberate imperfections outperform polish.** Intentional typos ("your" instead of "you're"), abbreviated text ("UR"), emoji-heavy captions, and messy home backgrounds. These signal authenticity — the ads perform because they don't look like ads.
> - **Statics and carousels have the best ROAS.** The top 3 ROAS performers are all statics/carousels (#7 at 4.56, #9 at 4.49, #13 at 4.38), not videos. Low production cost, high iteration potential.
> - **CTR and conversion are decoupled.** The highest-CTR creatives (#19 at 8.64%, #11 at 8.28%) have the worst CPP ($1,019 and $902). Emotional journey content stops scrolls but doesn't close — the conversion kit is missing from the body/landing page.
> - **CCMA dominates spend (14 of 20).** Matches the 80% revenue priority. CPHT has 4 entries, DA has 1 (and it's the worst performer at $2,365 CPP / 0.61 ROAS).
> - **"Fast track + no degree + accessible" is the winning message stack.** Speed (4 months), accessibility (100% online, no degree), and life-change outcome appear in every top performer. The account has found its message — the variable is format and talent.
> - **Text walls + inspirational audio is an untested opportunity.** Given the audience (women seeking life change), the Elevate-style "look how far you've come" format with text overlays has zero presence in the current account.

### Why These Are Spending

- **Visual disguise is the engine.** Every top spender looks like something a friend would post — selfie angles, bedroom backgrounds, parking lot videos, unboxing on floral bedsheets. The platform's CPM reward for organic-looking content is massive. The #1 spender is a static graphic that looks like someone made it in Canva, not a brand ad.

- **Testimonial social proof from relatable subjects.** The subjects ARE the target buyer: women in their 20s-30s, many Black, wearing scrubs or casual clothes, in real settings (car, bedroom, kitchen table late at night). They're not models or spokespeople — they're students who just finished the program. The viewer sees herself.

- **"4 months + no degree + 100% online" is the universal hook scaffold.** These three claims appear in nearly every top spender in some combination. The platform is scaling this message because it resolves the two biggest objections simultaneously (too long / too expensive) while framing the alternative (no degree needed).

- **Heuristics firing in the top spenders:**
  - **Goal Gradient** in almost every creative — "160 clinical hours completed ✅," "landed my first CPHT job," "passed the NHA exam." These ads show progress markers that make the finish line feel close.
  - **Social Proof** via real student faces and real results — not stat-based, but identity-based ("she did it, she looks like me").
  - **Processing Fluency** in the statics — one headline, clean background, zero clutter. "How to make 6 figures in healthcare without a degree?" is one idea, one frame.
  - **Framing Effect** on the speed claim — "4 months" frames what would otherwise be a 2-year degree commitment into an accessible timeline.

### What Converts vs What Doesn't

**Converts:**
- **Organic-boost statics and carousels** — "You look happier" graphic (#1, 21 purchases), "How to make 6 figures" carousel (#13, 9 purchases), "Ask your besties which healthcare job" (#12, 8 purchases). Low production cost, high Processing Fluency, easy to iterate. The static format forces a single clear message.
- **Milestone/achievement UGC** — "160 clinical hours completed ✅" (#7, 12 purchases), "motivational carousel lashayy" (#9, 12 purchases). These show the proof moment — the viewer sees the finish line, Goal Gradient fires.
- **Skills lab / clinical kit content** — The unboxing (#2, 18 purchases) converts because it makes the program tangible. You're not buying a course — you're receiving a kit with a venipuncture arm and a stethoscope.

**Gains spend, doesn't convert:**
- **Day-in-the-life videos from Nash** (#4, $6,233 spend, $779 CPP, 8 purchases). High CTR (3.58%) but the casual DITL format stops scrolls without resolving the "can I actually do this?" objection. The hook is strong but the body is lifestyle aspiration without conversion scaffolding.
- **Drake audio / trend hook** (#10, $3,844 spend, $961 CPP, 4 purchases). "Break up with him and become a pharmacy tech" is a great scroll-stopper with cultural relevance, but the joke format doesn't bridge to enrollment. Attention without intent.
- **Mom-focused emotional content** (#14, $3,221 spend, $805 CPP; #11, $3,609 spend, $902 CPP). Extremely high CTR (7.4% and 8.3%) — these resonate emotionally and get clicks, but the mom studying late at night and the "what's so special" mom journey don't close. The attention is from empathy, not purchase intent.

**Dead angles:**
- **Dental Assistant** — #6 is the only DA ad in the top 20: $4,731 spend, 0.61 ROAS, $2,365 CPP, 2 purchases. The format (b-roll of dental supplies + text overlay) is the most "ad-like" creative in the entire set. Combined with no CAC target and minimal student population for b-roll, DA is a spend sink right now.

### Why These Convert

- **The conversion kit is: fast timeline + tangible proof + relatable subject.** Every ad with ROAS > 3.0 has at least two of these three elements. The skills lab kit makes the program physical. The "160 hours completed" and "passed the NHA" milestones make the outcome real. The relatable subject makes the viewer believe she can do it too.

- **Statics convert because they're one idea, one decision.** The carousel "How to make 6 figures in healthcare without a degree?" (4.38 ROAS) is a single proposition that resolves two objections in one headline (money + no degree). Processing Fluency is the conversion heuristic for this format — zero friction between seeing the ad and clicking.

- **The "6 figures" hook is the strongest conversion driver.** #13 has 6.34% CTR AND 4.38 ROAS — the rare combination of attention and conversion. The Framing Effect is the engine: "6 figures" reframes a $44K median salary into aspirational language while "without a degree" removes the barrier. This is the hook to replicate.

- **Attention-to-conversion gap diagnosis:** The high-CTR/low-ROAS creatives (#11 Jane, #14 Mom Payoff, #19 Hannah) all share one trait — they're pure emotional payoff with no conversion scaffolding in the body. They show the RESULT (landed a job, studying hard, graduated) but don't show the PATH (how to enroll, what the program looks like, how fast). The emotional hook captures attention from the right person, but the ad doesn't close because it never shifts from inspiration to action.

## 3. RETENTION: What Holds Attention — and Why

*Note: Full retention quartiles (hook score, hold rate, Ret 25/50/75/100%) are not available from this pull. Analysis below is based on CTR as a proxy for hook strength and ROAS efficiency as a proxy for body-to-close retention.*

### Hook-vs-Hold Diagnosis (CTR as Hook, ROAS as Hold Proxy)

**High hook + high hold (scalable — replicate):**
- #13 SIXFIGURES_CAROUSEL — 6.34% CTR, 4.38 ROAS. The "6 figures without a degree" hook stops the scroll AND the carousel body delivers enough info to close.
- #18 SHAYLAH_TREND_NHAEXAM — 7.39% CTR, 3.46 ROAS. Post-graduation celebration hook + actual exam milestone = both attention and proof.

**High hook + low hold (hook over-promises, body doesn't close):**
- #11 Jane_Mom_Special — 8.28% CTR, 1.94 ROAS, $902 CPP. The "what's so special" curiosity hook gets massive clicks but the student journey video doesn't transition to a conversion pitch.
- #19 HANNAH_LANDEDJOB — 8.64% CTR, 1.57 ROAS, $1,019 CPP. Highest CTR in the set but nearly worst CPP. "Landed my first CPHT job!!" is celebration content, not conversion content.
- #14 MOM_PAYOFF — 7.41% CTR, 2.11 ROAS, $805 CPP. The late-night studying visual resonates but the ad is all empathy, no CTA bridge.

**Low hook + high hold (self-filtering for committed buyers):**
- #2 SkillsLabClinicalKit — 1.71% CTR, 3.47 ROAS, 18 purchases. Low CTR because "POV: your one of the first classes to receive their Kit" doesn't grab casual scrollers — but the people who DO click are already interested in the program and convert at high rates.
- #7 160-clinical-hours — 2.19% CTR, 4.56 ROAS, BEST in the set. "160 clinical hours completed ✅" is mundane to non-buyers but triggers instant "I want that too" from people already considering enrollment.

**Low hook + low hold (dead):**
- #6 da_THISISYOURSIGN — 0.77% CTR, 0.61 ROAS. Worst creative in every metric. Generic b-roll + text overlay, DA program with no audience.

### Why These Hold

- **Self-filtering hooks convert better than broad hooks.** The pattern is clear: low CTR + high ROAS (skills lab kit, clinical hours, honest review) consistently outperforms high CTR + low ROAS (mom payoff, landed job, student journey). The self-filtering hooks attract people who already know what a clinical kit is, what NHA certification means, what externship hours are. These are program-aware prospects, not top-of-funnel scrollers.

- **Milestone content creates Goal Gradient loops.** "160 clinical hours ✅" works because the viewer mentally places herself on the progress bar — she's at 0 hours, this person is at 160, the gap feels closeable. Same mechanism in "passed the NHA" and "landed my first job." Each milestone is a waypoint that makes the full journey feel achievable.

- **The aspirational lifestyle content (Mercedes, scrubs, outdoor photoshoot) holds attention but doesn't convert** because it shows the endpoint without the path. These are affect heuristic plays — they make the viewer FEEL the result — but without Goal Gradient scaffolding (steps, timeline, next action), the feeling dissipates without converting.

- **Pink/beige/feminine color palettes** dominate the statics (#1, #12, #13, #20). The account has a clear visual identity: girly, warm, accessible. Not clinical/sterile.
- **Outdoor/lifestyle settings** (#9 white dress outdoors) convert when paired with identity claims but not when paired with generic "day in the life" content.

### What's Missing (Opportunity Gaps)

1. **Text wall + inspirational audio format.** Zero presence in the account. Given the life-change audience (women escaping dead-end jobs, moms seeking independence), this format has proven to drive completion + conversion for Elevate's similar audience. The "look how far you've come" style audio over text cards showing the Stepful journey (enrolled → studied → externship → hired) is an obvious test.

2. **Comment reply format.** Zero in the top 20 despite "comment replies are a good native format" (per strategist observation). The native-social look of a comment reply ("How do I get started?") → response video is untested.

3. **Working mom with family angle.** "Spending time with family because it doesn't take long" is a key insight from the strategist, but no top spender directly addresses the TIME WITH FAMILY benefit of a 4-month program (vs. a 2-year degree that takes you away from your kids). The mom-payoff content (#14) shows the grind, not the family payoff.

4. **Cross-career / other-degrees angle.** "Being able to work with other degrees is a really good selling point" — the pre-med angle (#8) is the only creative touching this, and it's narrow. The broader message "your degree in [X] + this certification = healthcare career" is untested.

5. **Scarcity in any format.** "Classes 95% full" is on the website but in zero ads. "Next class starts June 11 — only X spots left" is free conversion pressure.

6. **Temporal Discounting in hooks.** "By your first week, you're learning real clinical skills" or "In 4 months you'll be in scrubs — not in 2 years" — framing the speed as an IMMEDIATE reward, not a timeline, is absent.

7. **Salary/income specificity.** "How to make 6 figures" (#13) is the highest-performing hook, but no other creative uses specific income framing. "$44K starting" or "my first paycheck was $X" from a real student would add Framing Effect.

8. **Male representation.** Zero men in the top 20. If the targeting includes men, there's a complete creative gap. If it doesn't, this confirms the women-only targeting strategy.

## 6. Decision Framework

### Replicate (with guardrails)

1. **Organic-boost statics and carousels** — "You look happier" format, "6 figures" format, "ask your besties" quiz format. These are the highest-ROAS, lowest-cost formats. Multiply across angles: create the "6 figures" template for CPHT, for DA, for different salary points. Create more "someone noticed I changed" testimonial graphics. **Guardrail:** Don't over-produce. The Canva-looking aesthetic IS the point. Professional design will kill the format.

2. **Milestone UGC from real students** — "160 clinical hours ✅," "passed the NHA," "landed my first job." Source more student milestone content. These self-filter for program-aware buyers. **Guardrail:** The milestone must be specific (hours, exam name, job title) — generic "I did it!" doesn't carry the same Goal Gradient signal.

3. **Skills lab / clinical kit unboxing** — #2 drives 18 purchases on $8,598 spend. The tangibility of receiving a real kit converts. Source more unboxing content from students. **Guardrail:** Keep the "your" vs "you're" organic imperfection. Don't correct the typos.

4. **Black women testimonial UGC** — The primary talent pool. Focus on creators who naturally use casual, empowering language. **Guardrail:** Vary the hook format (reasons, reaction, honest review, trend audio) to avoid creative fatigue. Don't ask all creators to use the same script.

### Kill (with evidence)

1. **Dental Assistant standalone ads** — $4,731 spend, 0.61 ROAS, $2,365 CPP, 2 purchases. No CAC target, minimal B-roll, tiny student population. DA should only appear as a secondary option within broader "healthcare career" content, not as standalone ads.

2. **Pure emotional journey without conversion scaffolding** — The mom-payoff and student-journey formats (#14, #11, #19) have the worst CPP in the set ($805-$1,019). These formats can work IF the body adds a conversion bridge (program details, CTA, next-class date), but the pure inspiration version is a spend sink.

### Risks / Guardrails

- **Over-indexing on Black women talent only.** The account has found a converting audience, but creative diversity prevents fatigue. The Asian working mom (#11, 8.28% CTR) and the Nash DITL content (#4, 3.58% CTR) show there's appetite for other faces. Mix 70/30.
- **Format concentration in statics.** Statics are the most efficient but they don't build brand. Video UGC builds trust and emotional connection. Maintain both.
- **"6 figures" compliance risk.** The $44K median salary doesn't equal 6 figures. The ad uses aspirational framing — check with compliance whether "how to make 6 figures in healthcare" is defensible. The claim doesn't say "as a medical assistant" — it says "in healthcare" broadly, which could be accurate for advanced roles.
- **Typo strategy must stay natural.** Don't industrialize the "organic imperfection" look. Real student content naturally has typos. Manufactured typos feel inauthentic at scale.

## 7. Carry-Forward Creative Learnings

- Organic-boost statics with one clean message produce 3.5x+ ROAS on average in this account, outperforming video formats consistently across spend levels.
- Self-filtering hooks (clinical milestone, program-specific language) convert at $339-$390 CPP, while broad emotional hooks convert at $805-$1,019 CPP — a 2.5x efficiency gap driven entirely by audience intent, not creative quality.
- Black women testimonial UGC is the volume engine: 10 of 20 top spenders, driving the majority of total purchases. This is not a segment play — it IS the primary creative archetype.
- The "How to make 6 figures in healthcare without a degree" hook achieves the rare 6.34% CTR + 4.38 ROAS dual performance — the only creative in the top 20 that drives both massive attention and strong conversion simultaneously.
- Clinical kit / skills lab unboxing content (1.71% CTR, 3.47 ROAS, 18 purchases on $8,598) converts efficiently because it makes an online program tangible — the physical kit is the bridge between "another online course" and "real healthcare training."
- Emotional journey content (mom studying, landed a job, student montage) drives 7-8%+ CTR but $800-$1,000+ CPP — these formats attract empathy clicks, not purchase intent. They need conversion scaffolding (next class date, program details, CTA bridge) to close.
- The DA (Dental Assistant) program produces 0.61 ROAS at $2,365 CPP with the only "ad-looking" creative in the set — both the program and the format are dead in isolation.
- Temporal Discounting and Scarcity are completely absent from the top 20. Given website language ("classes 95% full," start dates) and the speed proposition ("4 months"), both are available free conversion levers that haven't been tested.
