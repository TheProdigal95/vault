## TLDR Key Learnings

- **Static is the Oral TRT conversion base right now:** Oral TRT statics account for **$21,977 spend / 20 purchases / 0.54 weighted ROAS**, vs. video at **$17,338 / 17 purchases / 0.43 weighted ROAS**.
- **The best converter is simple benefit-point creative, not the highest spender:** “Feel Like Yourself Again” (#9) has **2.81 ROAS** at **$1,213** with **3 purchases**. It is not the most novel idea, but it is the cleanest buyer-readable promise: no injections, physical performance, energy/mood/libido/clarity.
- **The platform is scaling mechanism comparison, but conversion is uneven:** Traditional TRT vs. Nandrogen (#1) gets the most spend (**$7,571**) but only **0.53 ROAS / $1,893 CPP**. The format earns attention through specificity (“80% switching,” natural rhythm), but proof burden is high.
- **To-do / Notes-style native statics are a split test, not a guaranteed format:** V4 (#10) converts at **1.26 ROAS / 2 purchases**; V3 (#4) spent **$3,691 with 0 purchases**. Same broad format, different promise: “fixed after starting TRT” beats “want TRT to fix” because it implies completed transformation.
- **Dr. Jaquish videos get clicks, but only some close:** YouTube HiFi Explainer2 (#2) is the strongest video (**9 purchases / 0.69 ROAS**), while podcast negative hook (#15) gets **6.31% CTR and 0 purchases**. Authority can stop scroll; it does not automatically close skeptical TRT buyers.
- **90-day / timeline education converts only when it stays static and legible:** The 90 Days static (#6) has **5 purchases / $381 CPP / 0.77 ROAS**. The CGI / stick-figure video versions (#11, #18) spend **$1,723 combined with 0 purchases**.
- **Dead/guardrail angles for batch planning:** avoid AI/CGI transformation sequences, long SHBG explanation without proof, “TRT is a scam” negative hook as a primary frame, and broad dashboard/support feature stacks until there is clearer product trust.
- **Big whitespace:** current winners barely use the forum-rich objections: needle fatigue, hematocrit/blood thickening, “is this legit?”, and lab-first proof. Batch should move from generic symptom copy toward credible veteran/critic creative.

### Why These Are Spending

The account is being rewarded for **high-certainty mechanism claims**: “traditional TRT keeps T high 24/7,” “fast acting testosterone mirrors natural rhythm,” “absorbs in 30 minutes,” “no SHBG resistance.” Those phrases are concrete enough to beat generic low-T wellness copy. The problem is that the same concreteness increases proof burden. When the creative looks like a polished claim stack without enough credibility scaffolding, spend does not translate into ROAS.

The spend winners also lean on **processing fluency**: one chart, one list, one big comparison, one to-do note. The strongest statics are easy to parse in under three seconds. The video losers are the opposite: dense biological explanations, CGI sequences, and hard claims that require trust before the viewer has any.

1. **It reduces cognitive load.** #9 and #6 are simple: one promise, three bullets or one timeline. They do not ask the buyer to understand SHBG before they care.
2. **It positions OralOnly as a daily routine, not a miracle.** “Once daily,” “morning,” “Day 1/30/60/90” turns a risky hormone purchase into a managed protocol.
3. **It shows the product as clinical-premium.** The best statics use black pump bottle / RX label / clean white cards. This matters because the brand has credibility vulnerability from compounded status, AI-generated creative, and forum skepticism.

The non-converters usually over-rely on **claim intensity**. “TRT is a scam,” “muscle growing machine,” “90 days transformation,” and CGI physiology all grab attention, but they widen the trust gap for a skeptical medical buyer.

## 5. Cross-Ad Pattern Synthesis

### Format × Performance

Static is currently the safer production lane. It delivers more purchases, better weighted ROAS, and clearer message testing. Video should not be abandoned, but the account's videos need a tighter rule: **short founder/doctor authority + one mechanism + proof close**, not long animated education.

### Visual Choices × Performance

- **Works:** clean black/white/blue clinical statics, product shot, numbered list, note/screenshot native frame, timeline cards.
- **Mixed:** aspirational gym imagery — converts in #9, but may attract scrollers rather than the highest-trust TRT buyer.
- **Weak:** CGI body transformation, AI-storytelling split screen, stick-figure explainer. These formats are visually engaging but worsen credibility risk.

### Heuristic Distribution

- **Processing fluency** appears in almost every converter: 1 big headline, 3-4 bullets, simple icon set.
- **Goal gradient** works in static timeline (#6), not in video timeline (#11/#18).
- **Authority bias** gets clicks in Dr. J videos but needs proof scaffolding to convert.
- **Social proof is underused in winners.** Review/statics appear, but the account still lacks a strong “real labs / real doctors / real patient protocol” proof system.

### Who Is Shown × Performance

The current top creative either shows no person (infographics/product statics) or shows Dr. J / aspirational male fitness. Missing: realistic 35-55 men dealing with injections, bloodwork, fatigue, or credibility concerns. That is the persona gap from the OralOnly research docs.

### What's Missing

- Hematocrit / blood-thickening angle despite clear VoC whitespace.
- Lab-first proof: Quest/Labcorp panel, doctor review, quarterly monitoring, “if you don't qualify” flow.
- Credibility-first creative that acknowledges compounded/not FDA-approved carefully instead of hiding it.
- Real patient protocol content: “I switched from injections because…” with lab-value proof.

## 6. Decision Framework

### Replicate (with guardrails)

- **Benefit-point static:** replicate #9's clarity; make the subject older/more credible and include clinical proof.
- **90-day timeline static:** replicate #6 as a static/listicle, not CGI video. Keep claims conservative and tied to the protocol.
- **Short contrarian authority video:** replicate #2's brevity and mechanism simplicity, but replace risky claims with doctor/lab/protocol proof.
- **Native Notes/to-do format:** use the V4 “fixed after starting” pattern, not the V3 “want to fix” pattern.

### Kill (with evidence)

- **CGI / stick-figure timeline videos:** #11 + #18 = $1,723 spend, 0 purchases.
- **Podcast “TRT is a scam” negative hook as executed:** #15 = $896, 6.31% CTR, 0 purchases.
- **Generic dashboard/support feature stack:** #19 = $667, 0 purchases. Too SaaS-like; not enough product pain.
- **V3 to-do list “Things I want TRT to fix”:** #4 = $3,691, 0 purchases. Too hypothetical.

### Test (gaps and opportunities)

- **Veteran switcher:** “I was tired of pinning every week — but I didn't want a wellness gimmick.”
- **Hematocrit skeptic:** “My hematocrit hit 50+ on injections. I wanted a shorter rhythm.”
- **Credibility critic:** “Not another TRT ad — show me the labs, doctor, and pharmacy.”
- **Lab-first entry:** “Before you buy testosterone, find out if you even qualify.”

### Risks / Guardrails

- Do not write “80% switching” unless source is verified and citeable.
