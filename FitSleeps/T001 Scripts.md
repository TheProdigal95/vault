**Persona:** Heavy Sleeper + Considerate Partner
**Structure:** UGC Podcast Testimonial
**Creator:** AI-generated male
**Close Type:** Emotional

### Concept

The viewer is a heavy sleeper whose alarm problem is affecting his relationship. This ad borrows Ad 02's proven format — a creator in a podcast/interview setting delivering a faux-complaint pattern interrupt, then pivoting to a rave testimonial. The humor hook earns 40 seconds of attention. The body weaves three extreme scenario proof points between the discovery story and the partner payoff. The failed alternatives are mentioned briefly (this is a testimonial, not an alternative parade — V3 handles that). Word-of-mouth discovery through a paramedic buddy anchors credibility to a profession where waking up on time is life-or-death.

## Brief 2 — Creator on Green Screen: Mechanism Education

**Format:** Video — creator (Karra) in corner box on green screen, B-roll fills the frame. Educational explainer on why alarms fail and vibration works.
**Persona:** Considerate Partner (primary)
**Structure:** Creator on Green Screen over B-roll (~30-40s)
**Creator:** Karra (in-house female creator)
**Close Type:** Proof cascade (Trustpilot 4.6 + 175K customers + 100-night guarantee)

### Concept

The viewer's alarm is destroying his partner's sleep, and he blames himself for it. This ad reframes the problem: it's not about being inconsiderate, it's about using the wrong type of alarm. The creator explains the mechanism (why sound-based alarms fail heavy sleepers and wake partners) over B-roll that shows the problem and the failed alternatives. The partner angle is the primary emotional thread: every morning your alarm goes off, her nervous system takes the hit. The mechanism education (sleep spindles block sound, vibration on your wrist uses a different pathway) is the intellectual backbone that makes the product bridge feel earned, not pitched. The creator delivers this like a knowledgeable friend who finally figured out why her boyfriend's alarms never worked, not like a science presenter reading a teleprompter.

**Format:** Video — creator direct to camera, UGC style (phone-quality, bedroom setting). Parades through failed alternatives then reveals FitSleeps.
**Persona:** Heavy Sleeper (young professional)
**Structure:** UGC Talking-Head Direct to Camera
**Creator:** AI-generated
**Close Type:** Ease/simplicity

### Concept

The viewer is a heavy sleeper who has thrown money and effort at every alarm solution on the market. This ad uses the "overwhelm then simplify" format: the creator parades through failed alternatives one by one, naming them and dismissing them, with self-deprecating personal moments woven between each failure. The accumulated pile of solutions that did nothing creates a feeling of exhaustion and hopelessness. Then FitSleeps is introduced as the single thing that actually solved it. The body borrows the alternative-parade structure from the top-performing long-form story ads (Ads 01, 07, 08) but compresses it into a 35-45 second talking-head delivery. The self-deprecating moments are the connective tissue that makes it personal rather than a product comparison. Partner angle included as a single payoff line.

My son was about to fail out of college, and it had nothing to do with effort.

ADHD doesn't just make it hard to focus. It rewires how the brain handles sleep. Eighty percent of adults with ADHD experience what clinicians call "the sleep of the dead." Their circadian rhythm is shifted later than everyone else's. They fall asleep late, and when they finally do, they drop into a sleep so deep that sound cannot reach them. Not one alarm. Not six. Not their mother screaming from the hallway.

Seventy-five percent of people with ADHD have a diagnosable sleep disorder. This isn't a motivation problem. It is neurological. And I spent years not knowing that.

I spent years thinking it was a parenting problem.

Here is everything I bought Tyler before he left for college.

A Sonic Bomb alarm clock. $35. It has a 113-decibel siren and a vibrating disc you put under the mattress. He slept through it by the fourth night. His roommate didn't. His roommate asked to switch rooms.

An alarm app called Alarmy. $7.99 a month. It makes you solve math problems or scan a barcode before the alarm stops. Tyler solved the problems in his sleep. Literally. He'd complete three multiplication problems, dismiss the alarm, and have zero memory of doing it.

A sunrise lamp. $89. It simulates dawn over thirty minutes and is supposed to gently shift you out of deep sleep before the alarm fires. Tyler told me it was "kind of nice as a nightlight." He slept through it every single time.

Three separate phone alarms staggered five minutes apart, volume max, with the phone across the room. He'd get up, walk across the room, turn them off, get back into bed, and wake up three hours later with no memory of any of it. His roommate confirmed this. Said he watched Tyler do it with his eyes closed.

Sound-based alarms were never going to work for Tyler. Not because he wasn't trying. Not because he was irresponsible. Because the ADHD brain filters sound during sleep more aggressively than a neurotypical brain. The thalamus, which is the part of the brain that decides what sensory information gets through while you sleep, blocks repetitive audio. Every alarm Tyler ever heard became background noise his brain learned to suppress. Louder didn't help. More alarms didn't help. Novelty wore off within a week.

"Mom, I don't think you understand. I woke up BEFORE my alarm today. That's never happened. Not once in my life."
