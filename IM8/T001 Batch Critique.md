### Issue 2: Redundancy Between Hooks and Body

**The problem:** When a hook uses a phrase that the body then repeats, the viewer hears the same line twice in the first 10 seconds. Every line in a 48-second script has to earn its place. The body should advance from where the hook left off, not echo it.

**Every instance found:**

**Script #1 — Hook C vs. Body:** Hook C VO says "From the first sip, your joints start to change" and the caption says `FROM FIRST SIP TO DAY 90`. Body opens at 0:04 with "From your first sip, 27 raw superfoods..." The phrase "from your/the first sip" appears twice in the first 8 seconds.

**Script #4 — Hook A vs. Body:** Hook A VO says "...when you replace your morning coffee with one scoop of IM8." Body opens at 0:04 with "From your first sip, B12 at 8,333 percent..." The "first sip" framing repeats the hook's "one scoop" coffee replacement framing. Less severe than #1 but the same structural issue: hook sets up a "what happens when," body restarts with the same beat.

**Script #5 — Hook C vs. Body:** Hook C VO says "caffeine doesn't create energy. It just borrows it." Body at 0:28 says "Real fuel for your mitochondria, not another caffeine crutch." The caffeine-as-fake-energy concept appears in both. Less exact repetition, but the same thesis stated twice.

**Script #7 — Hook A caption vs. Body:** Hook A caption says `WHAT HAPPENS FROM YOUR VERY FIRST NIGHT`. Body opens with "From your first night, magnesium bisglycinate may help calm your nervous system." Direct repetition of "first night."

**Script #11 — Hook C vs. Body:** Hook C says "The weight loss was everything you wanted. The hair loss is everything you feared." Body at 0:04 says "Research suggests up to 40 percent of the weight you lose may be muscle, not fat, and hair thinning, low energy, and gut problems often follow." The hook names hair loss, the body immediately names hair thinning. The emotional punch of the hook gets diluted by restating the same fear clinically.

**Script #14 — Hook A vs. Body:** Hook A says "This is what happens inside your gut when you take one scoop of IM8 every day for 90 days." This is an exact structural copy of Ad 01's hook, which is fine. But the body then says "By Day 14, digestive enzymes have cleared out undigested food and toxins." The gap from the hook's "90 days" promise to starting at Day 14 is fine, no redundancy here. (Cleared.)

**What good looks like:** Script #13's hooks never overlap with the body. Hook A says "Dr. James spent 15 years researching why your probiotics never worked." The body opens with "Taking a probiotic alone is like sending one soldier into a war zone." Different angle, different image, advances the story. Ad 04 (top spender) does this too: the hook says "most unusual athlete endorsement," the body opens with the coach discovering IM8 on his own. The hook opens a loop; the body enters through a different door.

**Fix:** For each flagged script, rewrite the body's opening line to advance past the hook's framing. If Hook C says "from the first sip," the body should NOT restate "from your first sip." It should jump to what happens: "27 raw superfoods like turmeric and tart cherry may help ease joint discomfort within hours."

### Issue 3: Closer Phrasing Not Natural Speech

**The problem:** Stats in close sections sound like data readouts, not a person talking. "95% felt more energy in a clinical trial" is missing the subject. A real person would say "95% of users felt more energy in a clinical trial." The words "of users" or "of people" make the stat sound spoken, not written.

**Every instance found:**

**Script #1 close VO:** "95% felt more energy in a clinical trial." Missing "of users" or "of people."

**Script #4 close VO:** "95% felt more energy in a clinical trial." Same issue, same exact wording as Script #1.

**Script #9 close VO:** "80% felt they slept better. 85% felt better digestion." Missing "of women" or "of users" in both.

**Script #13 close VO:** "85% felt better digestion in a clinical trial." Missing "of users."

**Script #15 close VO:** "85% felt better digestion. 80% felt they slept better." Missing "of users" in both.

**Scripts #3, #5, #7, #8 close VO:** These use different phrasing. #3 says "Formulated with guidance from Dr. James DiNicolantonio" (authority close, no stat issue). #5 says "Developed alongside Dr. James DiNicolantonio" (same). #7 says "IM8 makes it easy to support your sleep, your energy, and your mood" (ease close). #8 says "Developed alongside researchers who spent 15 years studying gut health and sleep" (authority close). These are fine.

**Script #2 close VO:** "If the world's number one is willing to bet her career on it..." (story close, fine).

**Script #6 close VO:** "IM8 makes it easy to get everything in one place..." (ease close, fine, but ends with "I'm never going back to the pill pile" which is strong).

**Script #10 close VO:** "IM8 makes it easy to support your gut, energy, and overall health, so your GLP-1 journey feels smoother." (Ease close, fine.)

**Script #11 close VO:** "IM8 makes it easy to protect your hair, your energy, and your gut while you lose weight..." (Ease close, fine.)

**Script #12 close VO:** "My story is my proof. I lost the weight, kept my hair, and got my energy back." (Story close, excellent.)

**Script #14 close VO:** "IM8 makes it easy to rebuild your gut from the ground up, so bloating and discomfort stop running your life." (Ease close, fine.)

**Total affected:** 5 scripts (#1, #4, #9, #13, #15) use bare percentage stats without a human subject.

**What good looks like:** Ad 02 (top spender) says "85% of people reported better digestion and less bloating." "Of people" makes it sound like someone telling you about a study. Our scripts should match: "95% of users felt more energy in a clinical trial."

**Fix:** Add "of users," "of people," or "of women" (for menopause scripts) to every bare percentage stat in VO. Captions can stay abbreviated (captions are display text, not speech).

### Issue 4: Visual Direction — Flag Only Truly Meaningless Phrases

**The problem:** Visual direction should communicate the general vibe, editing approach, and emotional register of the ad. It does NOT need to be a shot-by-shot cut list — editors have creative freedom with footage selection, transitions, and pacing. The issue is only with phrases so vague they communicate nothing at all: "strong and competent, not intimidating" gives an editor no direction because every editor's interpretation is different. But most of our visual direction is already functional: tone descriptions like "warm, empathetic, validating" combined with concrete anchors like "bedroom scenes," "warm lighting," or "no harsh overhead" give an editor enough to work with.

**Truly vague phrases that need rewriting:**

**Script #1:** "Gym footage is strong and competent, not intimidating. Viewer sees someone they want to be." The second sentence is useful (aspirational, not intimidating). The first sentence is subjective without context. Rewrite to: "Gym footage is aspirational — controlled form, clean reps. The lifter is fit but relatable, not a bodybuilder."

**Script #12:** "The story needs breathing room." This is a feeling, not direction. Rewrite to: "Slow edit — hold creator shots for 4-5 seconds without cutting. This is the slowest-paced edit in the batch."

**Visual direction that is already adequate (no changes needed):**

- Script #3: "Creator sounds like a smart training partner, not a professor." This is casting/performance direction — useful for the creator brief. The B-roll guidance elsewhere in the section is sufficient.
- Script #5: "Educational revelation" energy — this sets the tone. Combined with its CGI and color direction, it works.
- Script #7: "This woman has been dismissed — the ad sees her and takes her seriously." Combined with its warm lighting and lifestyle B-roll guidance, this gives the editor the emotional register.
- Script #9: "Each woman films in her own setting — kitchen, living room, garden. Real, not studio." This IS actionable. Three distinct environments are specified.
- Script #2, #6, #8, #13, #14: Already clear and actionable.

**What good looks like:** Visual direction gives the editor a general approach and emotional register, plus a few concrete anchors (timing rules, lighting notes, subject descriptions). It does NOT prescribe every shot. Script #13's "Never more than 7 seconds in either before switching" is an example of a useful concrete anchor within broader editorial direction. Specific shot suggestions are fine as inspiration, but the editor has freedom to select footage that serves the concept.

### Issue 5: CGI Overuse

**The problem:** CGI should be a pattern interrupt or a mechanism visualization, used sparingly. One, maybe two cutaways per script. The top spenders use CGI briefly at transitions (Ad 01 uses 2-3 second animation clips between milestone stages), not as continuous visual content. When every body section calls for CGI, the editor is either building a medical animation reel (expensive, slow) or reusing the same stock CGI clips across all 15 scripts (repetitive to viewers who see multiple ads).

**Scripts that overuse CGI:**

**Script #3:** 4 body sections, CGI called for in 3 of them (0:12-0:20 adaptogens CGI, 0:20-0:28 amino acids CGI, plus CGI hooks A and B). The body alone has 2 sustained CGI sequences. Add the hook and that's 3 CGI moments in 42 seconds.

**Script #5:** 4 body sections, CGI in 2 (0:04-0:12 mitochondria CGI, 0:12-0:20 B12/CoQ10 CGI), plus CGI in hooks A and B. Visual Direction says "Heavy in the first half" which explicitly acknowledges the overuse.

**Script #11:** 4 body sections, CGI in 3 (0:04-0:12 nutrient depletion CGI, 0:12-0:20 nutrients flooding back CGI, 0:20-0:28 gut bacteria CGI), plus CGI in hooks A and B. Visual Direction says "Heavy in first half."

**Script #14:** 4 body sections, all 4 call for CGI inside milestone cards. The Visual Direction says "CGI: Inside every milestone card, the gut IS the visual content of the milestones." This means CGI runs continuously for the entire 32-second body. This is the worst offender.

**Script #15:** 3 body sections, all 3 call for CGI cutaways (serotonin flow, nutrient absorption, serotonin-to-melatonin). Visual Direction says "Brief cutaway per benefit (3-4 seconds each)" which is more measured, but still 3 CGI moments plus any CGI in hook B.

**Scripts that use CGI correctly:**
- **Script #13 (model):** CGI in hooks (pattern interrupt), then 4 CGI steps in body. This is on the line, but the Visual Direction says each step gets distinct animation and creator alternates with CGI (full-frame vs corner-box). The CGI serves the 4-step mechanism which IS the educational content. Acceptable because the mechanism needs visualization.
- **Script #4:** "One brief cutaway (mitochondria/CoQ10, ~4 seconds). Not sustained." Correct usage.
- **Script #7:** "One brief cutaway (gut-serotonin-melatonin pathway, ~4 seconds)." Correct usage.
- **Script #8:** CGI is on one side of a split screen with the whiteboard. The whiteboard carries the education; CGI illustrates it alongside. This is CGI as supporting visual, not leading visual. Correct.
- **Script #6:** No CGI. "This is a demonstration ad. Real bottles, real sachet, real person." Correct.
- **Script #9:** No CGI. "This is all real women, real settings, real language." Correct.
- **Script #12:** No CGI. Creator to camera + before/after photos. Correct.

**What good looks like:** Ad 01 (top spender, $67K) uses CGI animations at 00:23-00:26 (T-cells/knee joint, 3 seconds) and 00:33-00:36 (mushrooms + cortisol, 3 seconds). Two brief CGI moments in a 1:43 ad. The rest is real footage: gym B-roll, product shots, lifestyle, UGC clips. The ratio is roughly 6 seconds CGI to 97 seconds real footage.

**Fix:** Scripts #3, #5, #11 should each cut one CGI body section and replace with lifestyle B-roll or product shots. Script #14 should alternate: CGI inside milestone cards for milestones 1 and 3, lifestyle B-roll inside cards for milestones 2 and 4 (or vice versa). Script #15 should cut one CGI cutaway, probably #2 (nutrient absorption) which is the least visually distinctive.

### Issue 6: Missing Names in HOOK Captions for Muted Viewing

**The problem:** Hook captions are the most critical for muted viewing — they're the first text a scrolling viewer reads. If a hook caption says "she" or unnamed references, the muted viewer has no curiosity anchor and no reason to stop. Names in hooks are mandatory for any person whose identity matters to the argument. Body captions don't need names repeated — by then, the viewer knows who the ad is about. For widely recognized people like Sabalenka, last name only ("Sabalenka") is enough — that's what people call her.

**Hook captions that need names:**

**Script #2 Hook A caption:** `SHE INVESTED HER OWN MONEY IN A SUPPLEMENT` — "She" is unnamed. Muted viewer sees Sabalenka footage but the caption doesn't name her. Should be: `SABALENKA INVESTED HER OWN MONEY IN A SUPPLEMENT`.

**Script #2 Hook B caption:** `THE SUPPLEMENT SHE BET HER CAREER ON` — Same issue. Should be: `THE SUPPLEMENT SABALENKA BET HER CAREER ON`.

**Script #2 Hook C caption:** `THE ONLY SUPPLEMENT DEAL SHE ACCEPTED` — Same. Should be: `THE ONLY SUPPLEMENT DEAL SABALENKA ACCEPTED`.

**Captions that are fine as-is:**

**Script #3 close caption:** `TRUSTED BY THE WORLD #1` — This is a close caption, not a hook. By this point the viewer has watched 34 seconds of the ad. "The world #1" is fine in context.

**Script #9 Hook C caption:** `SHE FOUND IT THROUGH A WOMAN JUST LIKE HER` — Deliberately anonymous/everywoman. The "she" is not a named figure — it's the viewer. Creative choice, acceptable.

**Script #13 Hook B caption:** `THIS DOCTOR IS BEGGING YOU TO STOP TAKING PROBIOTICS ALONE` — The visual shows Dr. James's photo with a name tag. "THIS DOCTOR" paired with a photo is sufficient for muted viewing and creates curiosity. No change needed.

**Script #5 close caption:** `DR. JAMES DINICOLANTONIO · 300+ PAPERS · YOUR CRASH CAN END TODAY` — Names him. Correct.

**Script #1 close caption:** Beckham is named. Good.

**What good looks like:** Ad 04's hook captions name Sabalenka immediately: "SO ARYNA SABALENKA IS THE WORLD #1 TENNIS PLAYER." The muted viewer knows who the ad is about from the first frame. Body captions in Ad 04 then use shorter references because the viewer is already hooked.

**Fix:** Replace unnamed "she" references in Script #2's three hook captions with "Sabalenka." No other hook captions need changes.

### Issue 7: Edit Structure vs. Top Spenders

**The problem:** The top spenders' actual edit patterns (visible in the appendix transcripts) show specific rhythms. Ad 01 cuts every 1-2 seconds during B-roll, uses 1-second ingredient close-ups (hand picks up turmeric capsule, hand squeezes tart cherry), and holds milestone title cards for only 1-2 seconds before cutting to the next lifestyle shot. Ad 02 holds CGI sequences for 2-3 seconds but intercuts with real footage (doctor shots, stock of people in discomfort) every other shot. Ad 04 keeps the narrator in a corner box for 80% of the runtime with athlete footage filling the frame, and the narrator comes full-frame only for the hook and close. Ad 08 uses product photography (scooping, mixing, pouring) as the connective tissue between information beats, with each product shot lasting 2-3 seconds.

Our scripts impose structures that diverge from these patterns in specific ways:

**Scripts #1, #4, #7 (timeline structure):** The milestone cards hold for "2-3 seconds" per Visual Direction. But looking at Ad 01's actual edit, the milestone title cards (red screen with "After 9 days") appear for only 1-2 seconds, then immediately cut to B-roll. The card is a beat marker, not a sustained visual. Our scripts describe milestone cards that contain both the milestone text AND ingredient information, which means they need to stay on screen longer (3+ seconds to read "FROM YOUR FIRST SIP - 27 Raw Superfoods"). This creates a more static, text-heavy edit than Ad 01's rapid visual flow.
- **Fix:** Split the information: milestone marker on the card (hold 1-2 seconds), ingredient info as a lower-third overlay on the B-roll that follows. This matches Ad 01's rhythm: card > B-roll with text overlay > next card.

**Script #14 (CGI inside milestone cards):** This creates a completely different visual language from Ad 01. Ad 01's cards are simple text on red backgrounds, used as pacing beats between lifestyle footage. Script #14 puts CGI animations inside the cards, making each card a 4-6 second visual sequence. This is closer to Ad 02's sustained medical animation approach than Ad 01's milestone structure. The script claims to borrow from Ad 01 but the edit rhythm is actually Ad 02's.
- **Fix:** Either rename the visual approach (drop the "milestone card" language, call it what it is: a CGI mechanism sequence) or restructure to match Ad 01's actual rhythm with simple text cards between CGI shots.

**Scripts #3, #5, #11 (educational mechanism with heavy CGI):** These scripts describe sustained CGI sequences of 8+ seconds where the animation carries the education. Ad 02 does use CGI heavily, but intercuts with real footage every 2-3 seconds: the CGI shows the gut lining, cuts to a stock shot of someone clutching their stomach, back to CGI showing bacteria, cuts to a doctor in a lab. Our scripts describe the CGI as continuous for each body section with lifestyle cuts only between sections, not within them.
- **Fix:** Add intercut direction within each body section: "CGI for 2-3 seconds, cut to relatable stock footage for 1-2 seconds, back to CGI." Match Ad 02's actual pacing.

**Scripts #6, #9 (UGC style):** These are well-matched to the top spender patterns. #6 matches Ad 08's product-photography-as-backbone approach. #9 is a testimonial compilation which has no direct top spender comparison but the intercut direction (added in the previous critique) would give it the right rhythm.

**Scripts #2, #12 (narrative/story):** These match Ad 04's approach well. Creator carries the story, B-roll fills the frame, creator in corner box. Good structural alignment.

**Script #8 (whiteboard):** Matches Ad 03's format directly. Lo-fi, single-take feel, whiteboard is the visual. Good alignment.

**Script #13 (model):** Creator alternating full-frame/corner-box with CGI. Matches the Ad 04 + Ad 02 hybrid it's designed as. Good alignment.
