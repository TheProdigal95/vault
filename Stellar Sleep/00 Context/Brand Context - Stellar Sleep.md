## Mission + Story

Stellar Sleep was founded in 2021 as a venture at Harvard Innovation Labs by two co-founders who personally suffered from chronic insomnia. One co-founder averaged less than 5 hours of sleep per night for months straight; the other woke at 3–4am nightly, "frustrated, anxious, and angry." Both had tried everything — cutting coffee, blue-light blocking, sleeping pills — with no lasting results.

> "We tried everything to treat our insomnia from cutting coffee, blocking blue light, to taking sleeping pills, but couldn't find anything that worked. Our insomnia was turning us into literal waking zombies. Our performance at work suffered, our personal relationships suffered, our own happiness suffered."
> — stellarsleep.com/mission

After consulting sleep specialists and medical literature, the founders identified that most mainstream insomnia advice is calibrated for mild sleep problems — not chronic insomnia (defined as trouble sleeping 3+ nights per week for 3+ months). They concluded that chronic insomnia is more often a manifestation of unresolved psychological issues (anxiety, burnout, stress) and partnered with sleep therapists and researchers to build a CBT-I based app.

Legal entity: **Twenty Nights, Inc.** (named in copyright footer across all pages). Original brand name: **Slumber One** (still visible in some App Store reviews and early on-site testimonials).

**Timeline:**
- 2021 — Launched at Harvard Innovation Labs
- 2022 — 1st Place, Digital Health Innovation Awards
- Ongoing — Clinical study in partnership with Brigham and Women's Hospital (Harvard Medical School teaching hospital), funded by Massachusetts eHealth Institute
- 2024 — MedTech Breakthrough Awards: Best Overall SleepTech Solution

**Source:** https://stellarsleep.com/mission, https://stellarsleep.com/learn/why-stellar-sleep

## ICP — Who the Brand Says It's For

Stellar Sleep targets adults with **chronic insomnia** who have already exhausted standard sleep hygiene advice and are looking for a root-cause treatment. The brand explicitly distinguishes itself from apps addressing mild sleep difficulty.

**Stated target segments (from site copy):**

1. **Individual consumers** — primary. Adults with chronic insomnia who cannot or choose not to access in-person CBT-I therapy (cost, waitlists, geography). Funnel starts with a free sleep quiz.

2. **Employers** — secondary B2B channel. Pitched as an employee benefit delivering $3,700/employee in cost savings over 2 years (lower health spend, productivity lift). ROI calculator available. Target: HR/benefits decision-makers.

3. **Clinicians** — referral channel. Clinicians refer patients to Stellar Sleep as a scalable digital adjunct to in-person sleep therapy. Clinician analytics platform provided. Target: sleep therapists, GPs, and psychiatrists.

**Sources:** https://stellarsleep.com (homepage), https://stellarsleep.com/employers, https://stellarsleep.com/for-clinicians

## Positioning and Moats

**Core positioning:** CBT-I delivered via app — "the most effective known treatment for insomnia" — made accessible, personalized, and affordable. Not generic wellness. Not meditation. Root-cause psychology.

**Key positioning claims (verbatim):**
- "The CBT-I app to help you sleep better with psychology. Start seeing results in 2 weeks."
- "Solve your sleep problems at the root"
- "Insomnia is often a symptom of deeper challenges like anxiety, stress, or other things going on in your life. That's why things like cutting coffee, and meditation aren't enough."
- "No more 'yo-yo'-ing between good nights and bad nights"
- "Solves problems at the root, not just surface-level 'quick fixes'"
- "#1 mobile app for helping you fall asleep faster and stay asleep longer"

**Confirmed resonant phrase (client-verified):** "Founded and developed at Harvard" — client confirms this specific phrasing is actively used in marketing and resonates with customers. Use verbatim in copy where proof is needed; do not paraphrase to "Harvard-backed" or "Harvard-inspired."

**Differentiators vs. competition:**

1. **Harvard/clinical credibility** — curriculum developed with Harvard Medical School and Brigham and Women's Hospital. Ongoing funded clinical study. Awards. Multiple mentions of "proven" and "evidence-based." Confirmed resonant phrase: "founded and developed at Harvard."

2. **Personalization** — quiz-first onboarding creates a personalized program. Intelligent algorithm adjusts bedtime window based on sleep data. Positioned as "automated therapy" not generic content.

3. **Results benchmarks** — specific clinical outcome data (over 80% improve sleep, 74 min increase in total sleep time, 53 min less time to fall asleep, 52% fewer awakenings). Cited consistently across homepage, science page, and Why Stellar Sleep article.

4. **Comprehensive program** — CBT-I core plus relaxation library (soundscapes, sleep music, bedtime stories, meditations). Full program includes SRT (Sleep Restriction Therapy), Paradoxical Intentions, Cognitive Restructuring. Not a one-trick app.

5. **FSA/HSA eligible** — as of 2024, making it potentially reimbursable for US consumers with health savings accounts.

## Known Vulnerabilities

**1. Pricing opacity and billing friction.** The largest source of 1★ reviews. Pricing is not disclosed upfront — users complete a sleep quiz before seeing the cost ($188 USD per 3 months as of FAQ). Trial-to-subscription conversion has triggered widespread complaints of unauthorized charges and difficult cancellation. The FAQ acknowledges a financial assistance program exists (for those who cannot afford it), but the pricing architecture creates a significant trust problem.

**2. Sleep restriction therapy backlash.** SRT (the core clinical mechanism) is effective but requires restricting sleep to a narrow window (sometimes as low as 5.5 hours), which many users find "brutal." Several negative reviews mention the program worsens insomnia in the short term before improving it — and that this is not sufficiently disclosed before purchase.

**3. Content depth drop after first month.** Multiple reviews note that educational content becomes sparse after the initial program period (~26–30 days), yet the subscription continues billing quarterly. Reviewers describe paying $188/quarter for an essentially empty app.

**4. App technical issues.** Bug reports: sleep log freezing, 30-day challenge counter not progressing, cross-device sync failure. Not widespread but mentioned across multiple review periods.

**5. No human interaction option.** Several power users and complex cases express frustration at the fully-automated nature. Requests for Zoom sessions or peer forums appear in detailed 4★ reviews.

## Seasonal Trends (Client-Supplied)

- **Peak:** Q1, especially January — highest traffic and sales
- **Stable:** Rest of year is relatively flat
- **Trough:** Mid-November through mid-December

**Promotional insensitivity:** Stellar Sleep has tested promos and offers extensively. None have increased sales. Do not lead ad angles with discount, promo, or limited-time offer framing — the brand does not convert on price pressure.

## Client SWOT (Verbatim from Intake)

**Strengths:** Based on CBT-I (established treatment, high success rate); convenient/easy to access; program designed for sequence, pacing, and consistency.

**Weaknesses:** Not well known (early-stage brand); CBT-I itself is not well known; within the customer's mind, little distinction between insomnia apps.

**Opportunities:** Increased general awareness of sleep importance; relatively uncrowded field.

**Threats:** Customers may not be open to treating a clinical issue via an app; larger brands (e.g., Calm) moving into sleep.

**Creative implication of key weakness:** "Little distinction between insomnia apps" means creative must lead with mechanism differentiation — CBT-I vs. meditation/content apps is the wedge, not features or UX. Customers don't know the difference until you show them.
