## Patterns

### Format Distribution
- **5 Images** (Ads 01, 03, 04, 07, 08) — image ads dominate this account's top spenders
- **5 Videos** (Ads 02, 05, 06, 09, 10) — even split, but the formats serve different purposes
- Images carry the story through primary text (the image is a scroll-stopper, not the message)
- Videos are short (13-51 seconds) and deliver the pitch through visuals + text overlays

### The Two Ad Architectures

**Architecture 1: Native Image + Long-Form Story (Ads 01, 03, 07, 08)**
This is the dominant format — 4 of the top 10 spenders. The image looks like a real phone photo (parking lot, alarm screen, messy hotel room, phone on bed). The story lives in the primary text — 1,500-2,200 words of first-person narrative. The headline is the hook. The image is the scroll-stopper. The primary text is the ad. These are whitelisted (published from creator accounts, not the brand).

**Architecture 2: Short-Form Video (Ads 02, 05, 06, 09, 10)**
Videos are 13-51 seconds. Three sub-types:
1. **Humor skit → product showcase** (Ads 05, 06, 10) — 5-second comedic hook, then features/benefits
2. **Podcast-style testimonial** (Ad 02) — single speaker, intercut with B-roll
3. **B-roll listicle** (Ad 09) — no voiceover, text overlays only

**Architecture 3: BOF Promo (Ad 04)**
One conversion ad with product image, discount, and features. Serves the bottom of funnel.

### Hooks That Stop the Scroll

| Hook Type | Ads | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Alarm count + consequence** | 01, 07, 08 | "I set 6 alarms every night. I still woke up at 10:43" |
| **Humor / pattern interrupt** | 02, 05, 10 | "FitSleeps, I want my money back!" |

The "alarm count + consequence" pattern dominates the image ads. Every headline contains: a number of alarms + a specific failure. This formula works because the specificity signals authenticity and the failure creates a curiosity gap.

### Close / CTA Patterns

Every ad uses at least one of these:
1. **100-day / 100-night guarantee** — mentioned in 8 of 10 ads
2. **Sold-out scarcity** — mentioned in 3 of 10 ads (01, 02, 07)
3. **Trustpilot rating** — 4.6 stars, 175K customers (in longer stories)
4. **Discount** — only in the BOF ad (Ad 04: $98 → $44) and humor retargeting (Ad 10: 38% off)
5. **Mechanism explanation** — vibration vs sound (in 4 of the long-form stories)

### Recurring Story Template (Image Ads)

All 4 long-form story ads (01, 03, 07, 08) follow nearly identical beats:

1. **High-stakes opening** — almost lost job, missed flight, ruining partner's sleep
2. **Identity establishment** — age, job, relationship, years of the problem
3. **Alarm system description** — exact number of alarms, cascading times, backup systems
4. **Partner as human alarm clock** — always present, always eventually burns out
5. **Failed alternative parade** — sunrise lamp, sleep cycle app, dual-bell clock, phone under pillow (each with price and exact failure details)
6. **Away-from-home vulnerability** — hotels, conferences, travel (worse without home systems)
7. **Word-of-mouth discovery** — always through a colleague/friend named Marcus or similar
8. **First morning beat-by-beat** — time, wrist buzz, eyes open, room still dark, partner still sleeping
9. **Partner's reaction** — "You were up before me" / "Why didn't we find this four years ago?"
10. **Week-by-week transformation** — week 1-4, each with a milestone
11. **Price comparison** — $49 vs total spent on failed solutions
12. **Trust signals** — Trustpilot rating, guarantee, wake-up guarantee, sold-out scarcity
13. **Closing emotional beat** — "Turns out it was $49" / "I was $49 from fixed"

### What's NOT in These Ads

- No CGI / 3D animations
- No professional authority figures (no doctors, no scientists)
- No complex mechanism explanations beyond "vibration bypasses sound habituation"
- No ingredient lists or technical specifications
- No temporal discounting / milestone timelines (the product works immediately)
- No voiceover-heavy educational content
- No celebrity endorsements
- No multiple product comparison (Standard vs Pro barely mentioned)

### Key Patterns for Creative Strategy

1. **The image IS the scroll-stopper, the story IS the ad.** FitSleeps' winning formula is the opposite of most DTC — the creative (image) is intentionally low-effort and native. The selling happens in the primary text.

2. **Humor is the #1 hook mechanism for video.** 3 of 5 videos use humor in the first 5 seconds. The humor is always relatable exaggeration (cooking pot, whiskey binge, alarm waking everyone but you).

3. **"Not waking your partner" is the emotional payoff, not "waking up on time."** Even heavy sleeper ads include the partner beat. The moment the narrator says "she was still asleep" is the emotional climax of every story.

4. **Every story positions against the same 4-5 failed alternatives:** phone alarms (multiple), sunrise lamp, sleep cycle app, dual-bell alarm clock, partner shaking them. These alternatives appear in every single long-form ad. They're almost formulaic.

5. **Price is spoken openly.** $49 is mentioned explicitly in stories. This is unusual for DTC — most brands hide the price. FitSleeps uses it as a selling point because it's cheap relative to the alternatives and the consequences.

6. **Scarcity is a consistent CTA driver.** "Sold out" or "out of stock" appears in multiple ads. This creates urgency without discount dependency.

7. **Videos are SHORT.** The longest video is 51 seconds. Most are under 30. This is dramatically shorter than IM8's 50s-2:13 range. FitSleeps' product is simple — it doesn't need 90 seconds of mechanism education.

8. **The product's singular mechanism is its greatest creative advantage.** "Vibration on your wrist" is explainable in one sentence. This means every ad can spend 95% of its time on the problem and the emotional transformation, not on explaining how the product works.
