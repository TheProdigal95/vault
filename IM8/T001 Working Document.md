## Script Criteria

Permanent rules for writing scripts. These compound across batches. Derived from top spender analysis, headline criteria, creative direction, and mistakes corrected during T001.

### Copywriting Rules

**Voice and Flow:**
- No em-dashes in VO text. Scripts are spoken word. Em-dashes create choppy delivery. Use commas, periods, or natural sentence flow.
- Lines must flow like natural speech. Use connectors: "but," "then," "and finally," "because." Choppy standalone statements feel scripted.
- Every mechanism claim needs a "so that..." or "because..." connector explaining WHY the viewer should care. "Postbiotics seal your gut lining" means nothing alone. "...so nutrients stay in and toxins stay out" gives the reason.
- Word choice matters. "Amazing" over "good." Small word, big difference in desire.
- One scientific term max per concept, explained immediately in plain language. But don't overcorrect. If the script has established the context (e.g., talking about energy throughout), a slightly technical term like "cellular fuel" reinforces credibility without confusing. Only flag jargon that would be confusing WITHOUT the context the script has built.

**Product Introduction:**
- Must bridge from the mechanism naturally. "You can get all of that in one scoop of IM8" works because "all of that" references what was just explained. Never jump abruptly from education to product.
- Each script's product bridge must be unique to that concept. Reference the specific mechanism or benefits just explained. Do NOT copy the model script's bridge verbatim.
- Use "nutrients" not "ingredients" when talking about what the body needs or what medications deplete.

**Taste Beat:**
- Not mandatory. Must earn its place. If timing is tight or the concept doesn't naturally lead to a sip moment, cut it.
- When included, vary the delivery: sometimes a spoken line ("and it actually tastes amazing"), sometimes a reaction shot, sometimes earlier during a mixing moment.

**On-Screen Captions:**
- Captions and VO are two separate hooks on two channels (sound on vs sound off). Caption creates its own curiosity gap, never repeats VO verbatim.
- Captions must work on mute as standalone headlines. Apply the Headline Types & Criteria doc. "STILL BLOATED?" is too short (no loop). "THE REAL REASON YOUR PROBIOTIC FAILED" works (names problem, opens loop).
- Curiosity gaps must be contrarian and non-generic. See Qualitative Copy Standards for the full rule with examples.
- Hook captions must pass the cold viewer test. See Qualitative Copy Standards for examples of what fails and what passes.
- Use the person's actual name for recognized people, not pronouns. "SABALENKA INVESTED" not "SHE INVESTED." "THE SUPPLEMENT SABALENKA BET HER CAREER ON" not "THE SUPPLEMENT SHE BET HER CAREER ON." For unrecognized people, title is enough: "THIS DOCTOR" with a photo works.
- For persona-specific ads, establish the persona in the hook caption. A menopause ad's hook must mention menopause. A GLP-1 ad must mention GLP-1. Don't assume the viewer knows which ad they're watching.
- Caption cells must contain ONLY the text that appears on screen. No formatting instructions, no markdown, no labels.
- Use dashes (-) not middle dots (·) or special characters. Write captions like someone would type on their phone.
- Percentage stats: include "of users" or "of people." "95% OF USERS FELT MORE ENERGY" not "95% FELT MORE ENERGY."

**Redundancy:**
- Never repeat the same phrasing between a hook variation and the body. If Hook C says "from first sip," the body must use different wording for the same idea (e.g., "within hours"). Every line earns its place.
- Don't echo the hook's closing phrase at the body's opening. "Here's how to avoid it" (hook) → "Here's what happens" (body) sounds redundant. The body should flow directly into the mechanism without another "here's" opener.

**Close Sections:**
- Each script's close must be unique. Do NOT copy the model script's close structure verbatim.
- Use percentage stats naturally: "95% of users felt more energy in a clinical trial" not "95% felt more energy in a clinical trial."

**Qualitative Copy Standards:**
- Cleverness is fine when it's also clear. "CAFFEINE BORROWS ENERGY. THIS BUILDS IT." is clever AND immediately understandable. "Your gut isn't broken, it's starving" is clever but too vague for a cold viewer who has no context. The test: would someone scrolling with zero context understand the problem AND feel curiosity? If the cleverness creates confusion, cut it. If it creates clarity faster than a literal statement would, keep it.
- Don't add attribution layers when you can state the thing directly. "The women say it actually tastes amazing" adds a hearsay layer ("the women say") that makes it sound like you're quoting instead of stating. "And it actually tastes amazing" is direct. Same information, no attribution filter. This applies any time you're tempted to write "people are saying" or "users report" in the VO — just state the claim.
- Dollar amounts spoken aloud should sound like speech. "$2.61 a day" sounds choppy when spoken. "It comes down to about two dollars and sixty-one cents a day" is how a person actually talks. This applies to dollar amounts and casual numbers only — dosages and percentages ("B12 at 8,333 percent," "1,500 milligrams") are fine spoken as numbers because they're credibility signals, not conversational.
- When a script targets a specific persona, establish the persona's condition somewhere in EVERY hook — either in the VO or in the caption. A menopause ad must mention "menopause" in the hook. A GLP-1 ad must mention "GLP-1." The viewer doesn't know which ad they're watching. This doesn't mean the condition must be the first word — it means it must appear before the hook ends, in at least one of the two channels (VO or caption).
- Temporal discounting phrasing should vary across scripts: "from the first sip," "from your first night," "from the first day," "within hours." Don't default to one formula for every timeline concept.
- Hook captions must name something the viewer FEELS, not just something technically true. "IT'S NOT AGE. IT'S CORTISOL." is technically a reframe but the viewer doesn't feel "cortisol." "YOUR SLOW RECOVERY ISN'T AGE. IT'S CORTISOL EATING YOUR MUSCLE." works because "slow recovery" is something they've experienced and "eating your muscle" is visceral. The caption should make the viewer think "that's me" or "wait, what?"

### Structural Rules

**Hooks (0:00-0:05):**
- Must land in under 5 seconds.
- 3 variations per script:
  - **Variation A:** Curiosity Gap (proven, dominates top spenders)
  - **Variation B:** Untested type from headline criteria (Provocation, Reframe, Alternative Callout, Conditional). Pick what fits the concept.
  - **Variation C:** Must use a different visual style from A/B (mandatory). If A/B use CGI, C uses UGC or lifestyle. If A/B use product, C uses empathy B-roll.
- Product CAN appear in the hook (minority of concepts). When it does, pair with curiosity gap + aspirational visual filtering (Ad 01 model).

**Time to Benefit (from top spender analysis):**
- Progression timelines: 5-10 seconds
- Numbered reasons: 3 seconds (each reason IS a benefit)
- Authority/story: 15-20 seconds (story earns the delay)
- Educational mechanism: by 0:30 for our format

**Body (0:05-0:45):**
- Mechanism over claims. Show HOW, not just WHAT.
- Specificity always. Name ingredients, cite dosages, exact numbers. Never "powerful nutrients" or "premium blend."
- Analogies for complex mechanisms (soldier vs army, cheat sheet, cleanup crew). Compress to one beat, don't expand.
- Sophisticated market. Lean into mechanism and pain. Audience knows supplements.
- CGI is sparse. Max 2 cutaways per script body as pattern interrupts or mechanism visualization. Not every milestone or mechanism step needs CGI. Fill the rest with lifestyle B-roll, product shots, or stock footage. Learn from the top spenders: Ad 01 uses roughly 6 seconds of CGI in a 103-second ad.

**Close (final 7-10 seconds):**
Use one of 4 close types. Distribute across a batch so no type dominates:
1. **Proof cascade** (Ad 01): stat + NSF + celebrity. Direct and numbers-heavy.
2. **Authority close** (Ad 02): name a specific doctor/institution, then emotional pivot ("Your [problem] can end today").
3. **Story close** (Ad 04): the story IS the proof. No stats needed. ("If the world's #1 is willing to bet her career on it, that's quite a statement.")
4. **Ease close** (Ad 08): soft, reassuring. ("IM8 makes it easy to support your [benefit], so your [journey] feels smoother.")

Offer elements (welcome kit, guarantee) go in the platform end card, not the script.

**Timing & Pacing:**
- Plan for 55 seconds max at comfortable pace. Hard cap at 60. End card is platform-added, not part of our timing.
- Measure pacing in SYLLABLES PER MINUTE, not words per minute. Short words ("gut," "sleep," "one") take half the time of long words ("mitochondria," "bisglycinate"). Word count alone is misleading.
- **250 spm = comfortable conversational pace.** This is the target. Every script must fit under 55 seconds at 250 spm.
- **280 spm = brisk, energetic.** Acceptable for hooks but not the target for body/close.
- To calculate: count total syllables (longest hook + body + close), divide by 250, multiply by 60 = seconds.
- Verify pacing per-line, not just total. A single dense line will sound rushed even if the overall average is fine.
- Timestamps in the script must reflect real delivery time. If a line has 30 syllables, it needs at least 7 seconds at 250 spm — don't assign it 4 seconds.
- When trimming scripts for time, cut from the BODY, not the hooks. Hooks are tested as-is. Body has more room for condensing.
- Good trims: cut throat-clearing phrases ("Here's what's actually happening:"), redundant summaries (listing ingredients that the body already established), and wordy connectors. Bad trims: cutting mechanism connectors ("so that," "because"), dropping ingredient specificity, or removing proof points.

**Visual Direction:**
- Give the general vibe and editing approach. Editors have creative freedom with transitions, pacing, and footage selection.
- Specific shots are suggestions, not rigid requirements. "Use warm lighting, empathetic tone, lifestyle B-roll of women 50+" is good. Shot-by-shot cut lists are unnecessary.
- Only flag vague phrases that are truly meaningless and unactionable. "Aspirational and energetic" is fine. "Strong and competent, not intimidating" is meaningless.

**Script Delivery Format:**
Each script in the scripts document must include, in this order:
1. Brief name (Agency_Brand_Angle_##_BatchID format, e.g., "Reach Digital_IM8_Gut Health Doctor Authority_13_T001")
2. Editor Notes (standard block: follow hooks closely, use judgment for body, visual descriptions are suggestions)
3. Footage links (Google Drive folders for brand assets and B-roll, plus any script-specific footage like athlete pages)
4. Visual Direction (general vibe, CGI notes, caption style, color, edit pace)
5. References (Motion links to the top spender ads this script borrows from, with brief note on what's borrowed)
6. Hook Variations A, B, C (4-column tables: Time | Visual | Voiceover | On-Screen Captions)
7. Body (4-column table, same format)
8. Close (4-column table, same format)
- No quotation marks around VO text. Scripts are spoken word, not quoted dialogue.
- Triple line dividers (---) between each script for visual separation.

**Footage Requests:**
- One request per script that needs creator filming.
- Each request includes: task title (Brand_Description format), instructions, tone, and a full script table with Time | Voiceover | On Camera columns.
- Time column shows approximate duration per line so the creator can pace delivery.
- Include a note that delivery must roughly match the timestamps because the platform has a time limit.
- "Green screen" means the footage will be edited as green screen — the creator doesn't need an actual green screen, just a non-busy background.

**Model Script Usage:**
- When writing a batch, write one model script first with the creative director. Iterate on it until the quality bar is set.
- Sub-agents then follow the model's FORMAT (section structure, table format, level of detail) but NOT its exact WORDING. Every product bridge, close, and body line must be unique to each concept.
- The model script teaches the structure. It does not provide copy to paste.

### Per-Structure Rules

**Progression Timelines:**
- "From your first sip/night/day" opens the timeline with immediate benefit
- Each milestone names the ingredient responsible
- 3-4 milestones max for 30-60s format
- Honest starting points: "first sip" for energy/joints, "first night" for sleep, "Day 14" (not "first sip") for gut (adjustment period days 1-5)
- Use days not weeks for milestones beyond month 1 (Day 14, Day 28, Day 56, Day 90). Days are mentally easier to follow.

**Talking-Head Creator:**
- Alternate full-frame and corner-box every 5-8 seconds
- Creator references authority (doctors, research) but doesn't claim to be one. Name the doctor.
- CGI as pattern interrupt in hooks, creator enters within 5 seconds
- Whiteboard concepts: disqualify alternatives first (cross out with red marker), then reveal real pathway (circle)
- B-roll behind green screen can include: supplement facts labels, nutrient tables, product shots, athlete footage, NSF documents (reference Ad 04)

**Numbered Reasons:**
- Mini-PAS per reason: problem → how IM8 solves it
- Benefits start at ~3 seconds (Ad 08 pacing)
- Close with ease/simplicity ("the one thing you need to do")
- Product reveal can be late (after benefits established)

**Authority/Story:**
- Narrative arc with tension: skepticism, risk, proof. Not just endorsement.
- Real details create credibility (coach's name, NSF report document, specific moments)
- Benefit can wait 15-20 seconds. Story earns the delay.
- Don't cram too many transformation claims into one beat. Max 2 claims per 8-second section.

**UGC/Story with Narrative Thread:**
- Must have an overarching narrative connecting testimonials. NOT a quote montage.
- Each clip serves the arc: dismissed → discovered → results.
- Intercut 2-3 seconds of lifestyle B-roll between clips. Narrative VO bridges between people.
- If we can't film multiple real customers: one creator narrates and reacts to real reviews displayed on screen. She's the guide, not pretending to be them. Reviews appear as text overlays.
- If the story is about a specific customer: creator narrates in third person ("He lost 20 pounds on his GLP-1") rather than pretending it's her own story. Can switch between third person ("his hairdresser asked") and direct address to viewer ("here's how you can avoid it").
- All hook variations must transition smoothly into the same body. If one hook is story-first and another is educational-first, the body opening must work for both — usually by going straight into the mechanism ("When you eat less on a GLP-1, your body gets fewer nutrients") rather than continuing a story thread that only one hook started.
- **The most common transition failure:** A hook ends by naming the PROBLEM (reframe, provocation, conditional) without pointing toward a solution, then the body opens with the solution. The viewer goes from "here's what's wrong with you" to "B12 at 8,333 percent..." with no bridge. The fix: every problem-first hook must end with a phrase that hints a solution is coming. "And one scoop a day can fix it." "One supplement can change that." "Here's why people are switching to just this one." This bridge phrase connects the hook's problem to the body's solution.

### Authority Figure Rules

- If the body references an authority figure ("what he discovered"), every hook variation must establish who "he" is: name in VO, photo + name tag in visual, or title in caption.
- Authority figures not on camera: show their photo with name + short qualifier (3-4 words). Example: "Dr. James DiNicolantonio · 300+ Published Papers."

### Compliance

- "Supports," "helps support," "may support" are all acceptable for structure/function claims. Don't over-hedge. "Supports" is confident. "May help" is cautious. Use judgment. Only use "may" when the claim could be interpreted as treating/curing.
- No disease treatment claims.
- Approved stats: "85% of users felt better digestion," "95% of users felt more energy," "80% of users felt they slept better" (from clinical trials). Always include "of users" or "of participants" — never abbreviate to just "85% better digestion" (implies 85% improvement, not 85% of people).
- Beckham = "co-founder." Sabalenka = "shareholder and ambassador."
- NSF Certified for Sport, "Replaces 16+," "90 clinically dosed ingredients" — all approved.
- Do NOT: claim cures/treats/prevents disease; say "works like Ozempic"; cite specific weight loss numbers; name competitor brands (use solution categories: "probiotics," "greens powders," "single-ingredient supplements").
- Use terms from the PDP/landing page only.

### Quality Checklist (run on every script before finalizing)

1. Total syllables fit under 55 seconds at 250 spm (longest hook + body + close)
2. Timestamps match real delivery time per-line (no 30-syllable lines crammed into 4 seconds)
3. Hook captions ≠ VO verbatim (two separate hooks on two channels)
4. Hook captions work as standalone headlines on mute
5. Hook C uses a different visual style from A/B
6. No em-dashes in VO or caption text
7. No formatting labels in caption cells (no "**Milestone card:**")
8. No special characters in captions (use dashes, not middle dots)
9. No quotation marks wrapping VO text
10. Recognized people named in hook captions (e.g., "SABALENKA" not "SHE")
11. Every mechanism claim has a "so that" or "because" connector
12. Product bridge is unique to this concept (not copied from model)
13. Close type is assigned and varied across the batch
14. No competitor brand names
15. Compliance hedging where needed (structure/function claims are fine without "may")
16. No redundancy between any hook variation and the body
17. Percentage stats include "of users" or "of people" in VO, captions, AND visual stat graphics
18. All hook variations transition smoothly into the same body opening
19. Ingredient count matches client-confirmed number (currently 90, not 92)
20. Stats in visual/caption columns match approved phrasing exactly — "X% OF USERS FELT [benefit]" not "X% [benefit]"

## Process Log — T001 Examples

The Script Criteria above contains the rules. This section shows what actually went wrong during T001 and how it was fixed. Read these to develop judgment about edge cases the rules don't cover.

**Caption that looked right but failed:**
- Before: `IT'S NOT AGE — IT'S CORTISOL` (Script #3 Hook B caption)
- Problem: It's technically a reframe, but a cold viewer scrolling has no idea what this means. "Cortisol" doesn't trigger identification. Nobody thinks "my problem is cortisol."
- After: `YOUR SLOW RECOVERY ISN'T AGE. IT'S CORTISOL EATING YOUR MUSCLE.`
- Why it works now: "slow recovery" is something the viewer has experienced. "Eating your muscle" is visceral and creates urgency.

**Product bridge copied verbatim across all scripts:**
- Before: Every script said "You can get all of that in one scoop of IM8, and it actually tastes amazing." (copied from model script #13)
- Problem: By the 6th script, the batch feels template-driven. The model teaches structure, not words.
- After: Each script references its own mechanism. #6: "All 12 bottles in one scoop. It comes down to about two dollars and sixty-one cents a day." #12: "One scoop of IM8 with 90 ingredients, including the nutrients my GLP-1 was depleting."
- Lesson: When giving agents a model script, explicitly state that they follow the FORMAT, not the WORDING.

**Hook that didn't mention the persona:**
- Before: Script #9 Hook B said "Three women were told by their doctors..." (no mention of menopause)
- Problem: A menopause ad that doesn't say "menopause" in the hook fails to identify its audience. The viewer doesn't know what ad they're watching.
- After: "Three women with menopause were told by their doctors..."
- Lesson: The persona's condition must appear in every hook, in either the VO or the caption.

**Hooks that didn't transition into the body:**
- Before: Script #12 Hook B ended with "here's how to avoid it" and the body opened with "Here's what happens."
- Problem: "Here's how" → "Here's what" is redundant and sounds like two separate intros.
- After: Body opens with "When you eat less on a GLP-1, your body gets fewer nutrients." Goes straight into the mechanism without another "here's" opener.
- Lesson: All three hook variations must flow into the same body opening. Write the body first, then check each hook's last sentence against the body's first sentence.

**Problem-first hooks that don't bridge to the solution body:**
- Before: Script #4 Hook B: "Your 2pm crash isn't a coffee problem. It's a nutrient problem. And caffeine is making it worse." Body opens: "Within hours, B12 at 8,333 percent..."
- Problem: The hook ends on the problem ("caffeine is making it worse") and the body jumps straight into the solution (B12 dosages). No bridge. The viewer goes from "what's wrong" to "here's a specific ingredient" with nothing connecting them.
- After: "Your 2pm crash isn't a coffee problem. It's a nutrient problem. And one scoop a day can fix it." Now the hook ends pointing at a solution, and the body delivers on "one scoop."
- Lesson: Every problem-first hook (reframe, provocation, conditional) must end with a phrase hinting at the solution. The body then delivers on that hint. Test by reading the hook's last sentence and the body's first sentence back-to-back — if there's a jarring shift from problem to solution, add a bridge phrase to the hook's ending.

**Pacing that looked right by word count but was too fast:**
- Before: Scripts measured at ~150 wpm seemed fine. But "mitochondria" takes 5x longer to say than "gut."
- After: Switched to syllable-based measurement at 250 syllables per minute. Several scripts that "fit" at 150 wpm were actually 10+ seconds over at comfortable pace.
- Lesson: Always measure in syllables, not words. Short-word scripts (gut, sleep, mood) are faster than their word count suggests. Long-word scripts (mitochondria, bisglycinate, adenosine) are slower.

**Creator filming instructions that assumed equipment:**
- Before: "Film on green screen" implied the creator needs a green screen setup.
- After: Added note: "You don't need an actual green screen. Just find a background that's not too busy so we can key it out in post."
- Lesson: Never assume what equipment or setup a creator has. Describe the result you want, not the equipment you assume.

**Ingredient count sourced from homepage, not the product page:**
- Before: Every script said "92 ingredients" because the Product Context file said "92 nutrient-rich ingredients." That number came from the im8health.com homepage and athlete pages. But the Essentials Pro product page (get.im8health.com) already said "90+ ingredients replacing 16 daily supplements." The Landing Page Analysis flagged this discrepancy but we defaulted to "92" from the homepage.
- After: Client confirmed the correct number is 90 (April 2026). Fixed across all scripts, context docs, and Working Document.
- Lesson: For product specs (ingredient counts, dosages, claims), use the product page as the source of truth, not the homepage or brand-level marketing pages. Homepages round up or use legacy numbers. The product page reflects what's actually in the product.

**Stat graphics said "85% BETTER DIGESTION" — client flagged as inaccurate:**
- Before: Visual descriptions and captions used abbreviated stats like "95% MORE ENERGY" and "85% BETTER DIGESTION." These imply the metric improved by that percentage.
- After: Client corrected: the actual claim is that 85% of participants reported better digestion — not an 85% improvement. Changed all visual/caption stats to match guardrails-approved phrasing: "95% OF USERS FELT MORE ENERGY," "85% OF USERS FELT BETTER DIGESTION," "80% OF USERS SLEPT BETTER."
- Lesson: Stats in captions and visual descriptions must match the approved phrasing exactly, not abbreviate it. "X% [benefit]" is a different claim than "X% of users felt [benefit]." The abbreviation isn't just shorter — it's a different (and potentially non-compliant) claim. Apply to VO, captions, AND visual stat graphics.
