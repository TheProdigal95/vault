# Universal Copy Rules

Rules that apply to every format and every brand. All other criteria docs in this folder build on top of these.

Derived from IM8 T001, FitSleeps T001, and Lifeforce T002 — the strongest version of each rule kept.

---

## Voice & Flow

- Em-dashes are banned across ALL user-facing copy channels — VO text, primary text, on-screen captions, text-wall overlays, and headlines. Use commas, periods, or colons instead. The ban applies equally to text meant to be spoken and text meant to be read; em-dashes always get rewritten to a different mark.
- Lines must flow like natural speech. Use connectors: "but," "then," "and finally," "because," "so," "that means," "that's why." Choppy standalone statements feel scripted.
- **No noun-phrase fragment stacking.** No VO line may string together standalone noun phrases without a subject and verb to bridge them. This is the most common speakability failure and it turns a VO into a PowerPoint bullet list.
	- ❌ "Those mood swings. Saffron at 30 milligrams. Anything less barely supports your serotonin."
	- ✅ "Number one, mood swings. You need saffron at 30 milligrams, because anything less barely supports your serotonin."
	- ❌ "Better digestion. More energy. Sharper thinking."
	- ✅ "Your digestion is better, your energy is back, and your thinking is sharper."
	- ❌ "Saffron for mood. Magnesium for sleep. B12 at levels her single bottles never matched."
	- ✅ "Saffron for her mood, magnesium for her sleep, and B12 at levels her single bottles never matched."
	- **Test:** read the line out loud. If you wouldn't say it that way in conversation, add subject + verb + connector.
- VO must sound spoken, not written. If a line sounds like copy or a tagline, rewrite it as something a person would actually say. "Same problem, different packaging" is copy. "They're all just different versions of the same thing" is speech.
- Close sections must be conversational, not listing. "175,000 customers. 4.6 stars on Trustpilot." reads like a data dump. "It has 175,000 customers and a 4.6 on Trustpilot" sounds like a person talking.
- Every mechanism claim needs a "so that" or "because" connector explaining WHY the viewer should care. "Postbiotics seal your gut lining" means nothing alone. "...so nutrients stay in and toxins stay out" gives the reason.
- One scientific term max per concept, explained immediately in plain language. But don't overcorrect — if the script has established the context, a slightly technical term reinforces credibility without confusing. Only flag jargon that would be confusing WITHOUT the context the script has built.
- Vary sentence length. Short punchy lines for impact. Longer sentences for storytelling. Never a wall of uniform-length sentences.

## Product Introduction

- Must bridge from the mechanism/problem naturally. "You can get all of that in one scoop" works because "all of that" references what was just explained. Never jump abruptly from education to product.
- Each script or ad's product bridge must be unique to that concept. Reference the specific mechanism or benefits just explained. Do NOT copy the model script's bridge verbatim across the batch.
- Use "nutrients" not "ingredients" when talking about what the body needs or what medications deplete (supplement brands).

---

## Redundancy

- Never repeat the same phrasing between a hook variation and the body. If Hook C says "from first sip," the body must use different wording for the same idea (e.g., "within hours"). Every line earns its place.
- Don't echo the hook's closing phrase at the body's opening. "Here's how to avoid it" (hook) → "Here's what happens" (body) sounds redundant. The body should flow directly into the mechanism without another "here's" opener.
- When a hook already names the product or explains the mechanism, the body should skip the reintroduction and go straight to benefits.
- No two scripts/ads in the same batch should have near-identical close sections. Each close must be structurally and verbally distinct.
- **No overextended / restated beats within a single VO delivery.** If a beat says the same idea twice in different words inside one continuous VO line, cut to the load-bearing version. Classic pattern: a credentialing claim repeated with different institutional pairings in one breath ("founded at X, developed with X Medical School and Y Hospital") — pick one pairing, drop the elaboration. Each sentence in a VO beat must carry a distinct unit of meaning; elaborations that only restate get cut.

## Qualitative Copy Standards

- **Cleverness test:** Cleverness is fine when it's also clear. "CAFFEINE BORROWS ENERGY. THIS BUILDS IT." is clever AND immediately understandable. "Your gut isn't broken, it's starving" is clever but too vague for a cold viewer. The test: would someone scrolling with zero context understand the problem AND feel curiosity? If the cleverness creates confusion, cut it. If it creates clarity faster than a literal statement would, keep it.
- **No attribution layers.** Don't add "the women say" or "people are saying" or "users report" when you can state the thing directly. "And it actually tastes amazing" is direct. "The women say it actually tastes amazing" adds a hearsay filter. Same information, no attribution needed.
- **Hook captions/headlines must name something the viewer FEELS,** not just something technically true. "IT'S NOT AGE. IT'S CORTISOL." is technically a reframe but the viewer doesn't feel "cortisol." "YOUR SLOW RECOVERY ISN'T AGE. IT'S CORTISOL EATING YOUR MUSCLE." works because "slow recovery" is something they've experienced and "eating your muscle" is visceral. The caption should make the viewer think "that's me" or "wait, what?"
- **Persona in every hook.** For persona-specific ads, establish the persona's condition somewhere in EVERY hook — either in the VO or in the caption. A menopause ad must mention "menopause." A GLP-1 ad must mention "GLP-1." A heavy sleeper ad must mention "heavy sleeper." The viewer doesn't know which ad they're watching. The condition doesn't need to be the first word — it must appear before the hook ends, in at least one of the two channels.

## Banned Constructions

These are AI tells or lazy patterns. They apply everywhere — video, image, primary text.

- **No false dichotomies.** "That's not medicine. That's a subscription." reads as AI-generated. The "That's not X. That's Y." construction is banned entirely. Instead: give a reason why, add a specific detail, or create a curiosity gap.
- **No "It's not X. It's not Y. It's not Z." lists.** "It's not cold plunges. It's not peptides. It's not some exotic supplement stack." is an immediate AI tell. Rephrase as natural speech: "I'm not talking about cold plunges or peptides or any of that."
- **No swearing, even censored.** No "sh*t," "f*ck," "bullsh*t." The target demos respond to sharp, direct language, not edginess.
- **No writerly metaphors or stock transformation beats.** "Like someone had turned down the brightness on his entire existence" is novelistic. "Playing basketball with his son again" is a stock health-ad beat. Replace with specific, ugly details that feel real: "felt like he'd aged 15 years in 3," "his son said 'Dad, you seem happy' and he realized the kid hadn't said that in two years."
- **Max one analogy per script or primary text.** If there's a crafted metaphor, it should be the only one. Let specific details (numbers, dollar amounts, timelines) do the rest of the persuasion.
- **No fourth-wall breaks.** "That's not a sales pitch," "I know this sounds like an ad" — banned.

## Dollar Amounts & Numbers

- **Spoken VO:** Dollar amounts should sound like speech. "$2.61 a day" sounds choppy. "It comes down to about two dollars and sixty-one cents a day" is how a person talks. This applies to casual amounts only — dosages and percentages ("B12 at 8,333 percent," "1,500 milligrams") are fine as numbers because they're credibility signals.
- **Written text (primary text, captions):** Dollar amounts always as numerals ($49, $149/month, $599). Never written out as words.
- **Percentage stats:** Include "of users" or "of people." "95% of users felt more energy" not "95% felt more energy." The abbreviation isn't just shorter — it's a different (and potentially non-compliant) claim. Apply to VO, captions, AND visual stat graphics.

---

## Pacing (Syllable-Based)

- Measure pacing in SYLLABLES PER MINUTE, not words per minute. Short words ("gut," "sleep," "one") take half the time of long words ("mitochondria," "bisglycinate"). Word count alone is misleading.
- **250 spm = comfortable conversational pace.** This is the target for body and close.
- **280 spm = brisk, energetic.** Acceptable for hooks and short punchy lists but not sustained delivery.
- To calculate: count total syllables (longest hook + body + close), divide by 250, multiply by 60 = seconds.
- Verify pacing per-line, not just total. A single dense line will sound rushed even if the overall average is fine.
- Timestamps must reflect real delivery time. If a line has 30 syllables, it needs at least 7 seconds at 250 spm — don't assign it 4 seconds.

---

## Specificity

- Specificity always. Name ingredients, cite dosages, exact numbers. Never "powerful nutrients" or "premium blend."
- When using personal story framing, include at least one specific number: age, testosterone level, number of doctors visited, months of symptoms, pounds lost.
- Stats must be research-backed. Don't invent stats.
- No absolute claims about what doctors do or don't do. "Most doctors don't test for this" is too close to untrue. Use "many doctors don't" or "your doctor may not have tested this."

---

## Model Script Usage

- When writing a batch, write one model script first with the creative director. Iterate on it until the quality bar is set.
- Sub-agents then follow the model's FORMAT (section structure, table format, level of detail) but NOT its exact WORDING. Every product bridge, close, and body line must be unique to each concept.
- The model script teaches the structure. It does not provide copy to paste.

---

## Cross-Batch Differentiation

- No two ads in a batch should open the same way. If #1 opens with a stat, #2 opens with a story moment, #3 opens with a question.
- Failed alternatives must vary across the batch. Don't use "I went to 3 doctors" in every ad.
- Discovery mechanisms must vary. Not every ad can use the same referral source.
- Temporal discounting phrasing should vary: "from the first sip," "from your first night," "from the first day," "within hours." Don't default to one formula.
