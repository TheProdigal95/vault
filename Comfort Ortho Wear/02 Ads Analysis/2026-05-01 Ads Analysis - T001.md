# Ads Analysis — T001

Working document: [[T001 Working Document]]

---

## TLDR — Key Learnings

**Winning format:** Ranked comparison ("Top 5 shoes for nurses → ComfortWear wins #1") accounts for ~$70K of the top spend. It works because it reframes brand claims as objective test results and weaponizes competitive price anchoring — ComfortWear at $60 vs. Hoka at $165, Nike at $120, and $300 podiatrist orthotics.

**Strongest persona:** Nurses, by a wide margin. Every top-performing cold video targets them with hospital-specific context (concrete floors, 12-hour shifts, surgery, patient rooms, scrubs). Travel persona tested in Ad 2 — comparable spend but slightly lower ROAS, suggesting lower daily pain intensity = weaker purchase urgency.

**Best pain point for hooks:** Plantar fasciitis. Ad 10 opens with PF and gets the highest hook score (66) and best cold video ROAS (1.91). PF is the sharpest, most specific pain — it names the condition the audience already Googles.

**Price anchor that converts:** "$60 vs. $300 podiatrist orthotics" appears in the two highest-ROAS cold ads (3 and 10). Anchoring against medical-grade alternatives frames ComfortWear as a smart shortcut, not a cheap substitute.

**Static persona-label ads punch above their weight.** "12-HOUR SHIFT SHOES" + 6-bullet benefit list drives 1.62–1.67 ROAS with zero video production. Whitelisted nurse pages ("Nurse Finds") lift both ROAS and AOV over identical brand-page placements — a media buying insight as much as creative.

**Hook vs. watch score tension:** Hook and watch are inversely correlated in this account. The most curiosity-driven hooks (Ad 10: 66 hook, 49 watch) stop scrollers but lose them in the body. The ranked comparison format (Ads 4–5: 60–61 hook, 65 watch) balances both because the "who wins?" structure gives viewers a reason to stay. The implication: high-hook educational formats need tighter bodies, and ranked formats should keep testing stronger hooks without sacrificing the competition arc.

**ROAS hierarchy (cold only):** PF-first survey hook (1.91) > static persona label (1.62–1.67) > ranked comparison (1.17–1.29) > origin story (0.98). The origin story format has a strong concept but breaks at length — 2 minutes of history before introducing the product is too long.

**RTG converts hard:** Social proof static (3.30 ROAS) and UGC nurse testimonial (2.76 ROAS) dominate warm audiences. The Facebook comment format and "$60 vs. $300 orthotics" price anchor from these RTG ads are cold-testable elements.

**Untested opportunities for T001:**
1. Pure first-person PF pain hook in cold video (not survey-framed)
2. Short-form UGC (<60 sec) adapting the RTG testimonial for cold traffic
3. B-roll + caption format (zero production, native-feeling — Reference 1)
4. "Cute and comfortable" / gifting angle for non-nurse audiences
5. Deep mechanism explainer — WHY the arch support works differently, not just THAT it exists
6. Social proof video (comment/testimonial format adapted from static to video for cold)

**Analysis**

Ranked comparison is the highest-spend format in this account — it works by reframing brand claims as objective test results rather than self-promotion. The price anchor is the emotional core: ComfortWear at $60 beats Hoka at $165 and Nike at $120, and the "cheapest option beats shoes that cost three times more" line at ~01:29 is the peak. Hospital-specific b-roll (scrubs, wet floors, concrete) and named conditions (plantar fasciitis, wide toe box, grip) signal authentic nurse understanding. The 45-day vs. 30-day guarantee comparison is used as a competitive differentiator, not just a CTA. Hook score of 54 is the lowest in the video set — the 3-panel split open may be losing viewers before the format registers; a single-frame hook with the nurse persona stated immediately could lift this.

### Patterns Across Top Spenders

**Structures that dominate:**

Ranked comparison (Top 5 → our product wins #1) accounts for the largest share of spend — Ads 1–5 (~$70K combined) all use it. The format works because it reframes brand claims as objective test results and uses competitive price anchoring to make ComfortWear the logical winner. Second dominant structure: direct-benefit static with persona label ("12-HOUR SHIFT SHOES"), which drives solid ROAS at a fraction of the production cost. RTG-specific formats (social proof static, UGC testimonial) show the highest ROAS but require warm audiences to function.

**Hook patterns that work:**

Three proven structures: (1) Mission statement — "We looked at 5 popular shoes nurses wear to see which one actually helps with foot pain." (2) Results-first curiosity gap — "The most expensive came in SECOND PLACE. You won't believe it." — higher CTR than mission statement. (3) Data/survey open — "We asked 500 nurses with plantar fasciitis one simple question" — highest hook score (66) of any video tested. The UGC first-person cold open ("I spent $60 on these shoes for my nursing job") has the best RTG ROAS but hasn't been tested cold.

**Visual / creative differentiators:**

Hospital-specific context is the #1 signal that this creative is for nurses — scrubs, patient rooms, concrete floors, surgery references, and 12-hour shift language. Competitors appear by name in VO but logos are blurred in footage (aggressive competitive framing without legal risk). Animated foot anatomy overlays (X-ray arch support, wide toe box visualization) appear across multiple ads and explain mechanism without being clinical. "Limping to her car after a shift" is the most consistent pain visualization moment across all video ads.

**Caption and editing patterns:**

Fast cuts, 2–3 seconds per shot across all videos. Ranked comparison ads mirror VO closely in captions — verbatim reinforcement. Orange/yellow highlight used for score callouts ("9.2 OUT OF 10", "40% OFF"). End card is standardized: brand logo + shoe lineup (3–4 colorways) + badge stack (guarantee, free shipping, doctor-recommended). Social proof hierarchy: (1) volume — "thousands of reviews from nurses"; (2) peer referral — "three other nurses on my floor ordered them"; (3) screenshot comments — unedited Facebook comments (Ad 9); (4) customer count badge — "500,000+ customers" on end cards.

**Best hook scores — and what's driving them:**
- **Ad 10 at 66 (PF survey)** — "We asked 500 nurses with plantar fasciitis one simple question" is the strongest hook because it stacks three scroll-stop triggers: a specific number (500), a named condition the audience self-identifies with (plantar fasciitis), and an implied surprise ("one simple question" creates a curiosity gap). It's the only hook that opens with the pain rather than the product category.
- **Ad 2 at 64 (travel ranked)** — "Think sneakers are the most comfortable shoes for travel?" opens with a direct challenge to a belief. The question format forces the viewer to answer internally — that micro-engagement buys the next 3 seconds. Stronger than Ad 1's mission-statement open.
- **Ad 8 at 62 (origin story)** — The nostalgia hook ("Remember when nurses wore those crisp white uniforms?") works because it triggers a strong emotional anchor and a "what changed?" curiosity loop. Despite the high hook, the ad can't convert it — the history section runs too long before the product appears.
- **Ads 4–5 at 60–61 (Spanish ranked)** — Same ranked format as Ad 1 but slight edge from the Hook 2 results-first variant ("the most expensive came in SECOND PLACE").
- **Ad 1 at 54 (nurse ranked, English)** — Lowest video hook score. The three-panel split-screen open asks the viewer to process too much visual information before registering what the ad is about. A single-frame hook with the nurse persona stated immediately would likely lift this.

**Best watch scores — and why people stay:**
- **Ads 4 and 5 tie at 65 (Spanish ranked)** — The ranked comparison structure has a built-in retention engine: viewers want to see who wins. Each competitor reveal is a mini-payoff that keeps them watching. Spanish audience may also have fewer competing ads, reducing drop-off pressure.
- **Ad 1 at 60 (English nurse ranked)** — Same retention engine as Ads 4–5. The slightly lower watch score may reflect the English-language audience being more ad-saturated and more likely to skip once they register the ad's intent.
- **Ad 2 at 59 (travel ranked)** — Nearly identical to Ad 1. The travel persona holds attention equally well through the competition arc, confirming this is a structural effect (the format retains), not a persona effect.
- **Ad 8 at 54 (origin story)** — The nostalgia/history format creates initial engagement but doesn't deliver product information fast enough. By the time ComfortWear appears at ~1:43, many viewers have already dropped. The watch score reflects people who stayed for the story but doesn't translate to ROAS (0.98).
- **Ad 3 (RTG) at 52** — Lower watch score is expected for RTG — warm audiences already know the product and are deciding whether to buy, not whether to keep watching.
- **Ad 10 at 49 (PF survey)** — The sharpest hook-to-watch drop in the account. 66 hook → 49 watch. The educational walkthrough (Skechers → Hokas → podiatrist → ComfortWear) is valuable content but each shoe review is a natural exit point. Viewers who get the concept from the hook — "ComfortWear will probably win" — leave before the reveal. Hypothesis: cutting the competitor section from 4 shoes to 2 (the strongest price anchors only) would tighten the body without losing the comparison frame.

**The core retention pattern: structure vs. information.**
Two retention strategies emerge. Ranked comparisons (Ads 1, 2, 4, 5) retain through *structural anticipation* — the format itself gives you a reason to stay (who wins?). Educational/survey formats (Ads 8, 10) retain through *information density* — each fact is interesting but doesn't build toward a climax. The structural approach produces better watch scores (59–65 vs. 49–54). The implication for T001: educational formats need a structural backbone — give the audience a reason to stay beyond "this is interesting," like a countdown, a competition, or an explicit promise that pays off at the end.

**ROAS patterns — what converts:**
- **RTG dominates on ROAS** (3.30, 2.76) — but this is audience quality, not creative innovation. The creative takeaway from RTG is which *elements* convert warm audiences: social proof screenshots, the $300 orthotics anchor, and first-person nurse testimonials.
- **Best cold ROAS: Ad 10 at 1.91** — The PF-first hook filters for high-intent viewers from the first second. People who stop for "500 nurses with plantar fasciitis" are people who HAVE plantar fasciitis. The high hook score (66) + high ROAS combo means it's both stopping the right people AND converting them.
- **Static ads at 1.62–1.67** — Highest cold ROAS per production dollar. "12-HOUR SHIFT SHOES" as a headline is a persona filter that eliminates low-intent viewers before they even click. These likely capture audiences already warmed by video exposure elsewhere in the funnel.
- **Ranked comparison at 1.17–1.29** — Moderate ROAS but by far the highest absolute spend (~$70K combined). The format is efficient at scale — it doesn't convert as sharply as PF-first hooks but it works across personas (nurses, travelers) and sustains spend without fatiguing.
- **Origin story at 0.98** — The concept has legs but the execution doesn't convert. History sections need to be 10–15 seconds max before introducing the product.

**Gaps and opportunities for T001:**

1. PF as primary cold hook — Ad 10 gets closest but leads with a survey frame; a pure first-person PF pain hook in cold video is untested
2. Short-form UGC (<60 sec) adapting Ad 3's RTG testimonial structure for cold — the $300 orthotics anchor and "6 months still brand new" claims are cold-friendly
3. B-roll + caption format (Reference 1 structure) — zero production cost, native-feeling, untested for ComfortWear
4. "Cute and comfortable" / gifting angle (Reference 2 structure) — opens non-nurse audiences and holiday gifting frames
5. Deep mechanism explainer — no ad goes beyond feature lists into WHY the ORTHO-WEAR tech works differently at a structural level
6. Social proof video (not just static) — the comment/testimonial format of Ad 9 has never been adapted to video cold
