## 1. Regulatory Framework

### FDA Classification
IM8 products are classified as **dietary supplements, NOT drugs**. This distinction governs everything we can and cannot say.

### Three Types of Claims (FDA)

1. **Structure/Function Claims** (ALLOWED)
   - Describe how a supplement supports normal body functions
   - Example: "Supports immune system function"
   - **MUST include disclaimer:** "This statement has not been evaluated by the FDA. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

2. **Nutrient Content Claims** (ALLOWED)
   - Describe the level of a nutrient in the supplement
   - Example: "High in Vitamin C"
   - Must follow FDA definitions of nutrient levels

3. **Disease Claims** (PROHIBITED)
   - Cannot claim to treat, cure, or prevent a specific disease
   - Example: "Cures cancer" or "Prevents heart disease"

### FTC Requirements
All advertising claims must be:
- **Truthful:** Accurately reflect the product's capabilities
- **Non-misleading:** Not deceive consumers through exaggeration or omission
- **Substantiated:** Supported by reliable scientific evidence (typically human clinical studies)

**Critical:** The FTC and FDA evaluate claims based on **"net impression"** -- what a reasonable consumer would understand from the overall message, not just literal wording. Even implied disease prevention is prohibited.

### Additional Prohibited Terms & Phrases (Source: Marketing Framework & SOP)

**FDA-Regulated Medical Claims (NEVER USE):**
- "Anti-aging" -- FDA considers aging a natural process, not a disease. Use instead: "healthy aging," "longevity support," "aging gracefully"
- "Cure," "Treat," "Prevent," "Diagnose" -- drug claims requiring FDA approval. Use instead: "support," "promote," "maintain," "help with"
- "Reverse aging" -- implies medical intervention. Use instead: "support cellular renewal," "promote healthy aging processes"
- "Fountain of youth," "Turn back the clock," "Age backwards" -- unrealistic. Use instead: "support vitality," "promote healthy aging"

**Disease-Related Claims (NEVER USE):**
- Specific disease names: Cancer, Alzheimer's, diabetes, heart disease, etc.
- "Boost immune system" -- can be interpreted as medical claim. Use instead: "support immune function," "maintain immune health"
- "Detox" or "Cleanse" -- implies medical intervention. Use instead: "support natural cellular processes," "promote cellular renewal"

**Exaggerated Performance Claims (AVOID):**
- "Miracle," "Breakthrough cure," "Revolutionary treatment"
- "Guaranteed results," "Works for everyone," "100% effective"
- "Better than prescription drugs"
- "No side effects," "Completely safe for everyone," "No need to consult a doctor," "Safe for all medical conditions"

**Pricing/Value (AVOID):**
- "Cheapest longevity supplement"
- "Best deal on the market"
- "Unbeatable price"
- "Too good to be true pricing"

**Diagnostic Language:**
- "5 Signs You Have [Disease]" -- diagnostic framing is prohibited
- Any before/after progression showing someone becoming "disease-free"

## 6. Competitive Claims Guidelines

### DO Say:
- "More comprehensive than other supplements"
- "Higher ingredient capacity than capsules"
- "More complete formula"
- "Advanced formulation approach"

### DO NOT Say:
- "Better than [specific competitor name]" (e.g., "Better than AG1")
- "Competitor X doesn't work"
- "Only effective longevity supplement"
- "Replaces all other supplements" (too absolute)

**Note:** Head-to-head comparison content is allowed on the IM8 website (comparison pages exist for AG1). But in ads, show don't tell -- let the comparison speak through ingredient/feature specifics rather than naming competitors.

## 8. Creative Advertising Guardrails (Source: SOP)

### Prohibited Content Categories

| Category | Rationale | Specific Examples to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Crude & Bathroom Humor** | Low-brow, cheapens the brand, misaligned with premium positioning. Embarrassing for a brand associated with David Beckham. | Any mention of "farts," "poop," or crude bodily function terms. Headlines like "It's time to fix those silent but deadly farts!" or "Poop Smarter, Not Harder." **Workaround:** If discussing digestion, replacing "poop" with "Number 2" is acceptable. |
| **Graphic or Unpleasant Imagery** | Shock value and gross-out tactics are off-brand. Creates negative product associations. | Animated/real depictions of vomiting, regurgitation. Overly graphic illustrations of internal organs or digestive processes. Images designed to be disgusting or unsettling. |
| **Insensitive & Stereotypical Content** | Using identity as a marketing hook is unacceptable. Carries significant brand risk. | Creatives that lead with phrases like "My gay friend..." or tokenize any group. Crude/reductive discussions of gender identity, sexual orientation, LGBTQ+ topics. Using someone's identity as a punchline or hook. |
| **Political Content & Divisive Topics** | IM8 serves a diverse, global audience. Political messaging alienates customers. | References to political figures, parties, or current events. Taking stances on divisive issues. Content perceived as aligning with any political ideology. |
| **Spammy, Clickbait & Low-Quality Aesthetics** | Erodes premium brand quality. | Low-resolution images, poor animations, generic stock photos. Anything that looks like a "get rich quick" scheme or dubious internet ad. **Note: We can be attention-grabbing, but classy about it.** |
| **Disease Claims & Prohibited Medical Statements** | Illegal under FDA/FTC regulations. | See Section 3 above for full list. |

### Tone & Voice
- **Premium, sophisticated, confident** -- not clinical or cold, but elevated
- **Respectful and inclusive** -- no tokenizing, no stereotypes
- **Science-forward but accessible** -- use data and mechanism, but explain it simply
- **Attention-grabbing but classy** -- bold hooks are fine, crude ones are not
- **Empathetic** -- acknowledge the frustration of the target audience without being condescending

### Visual Guidelines
- High-quality imagery only -- no low-res, no poor animations, no generic stock
- Clean, premium aesthetic
- **Color Psychology (Longevity):** Orange (energy/vitality), Deep Blue (trust/science), Clean White (purity/premium), Accent Gold (premium positioning)
- Diverse, aspirational demographics in lifestyle photography
- Natural, energetic environments
- Clean product photography
- Scientific visualization graphics where appropriate

## 10. Platform-Specific Considerations

While the source documents don't specify per-platform rules beyond general FTC/FDA compliance, the following principles apply across all paid media channels:

### General (All Platforms)
- All claims must use approved structure/function language
- Required disclaimers must be present (FDA statement)
- No disease claims regardless of platform
- "Net impression" standard applies -- even implied health claims can trigger issues

### Meta (Facebook/Instagram)
- Meta's ad policies layer on top of FDA/FTC rules
- Health and wellness ads face stricter review
- Avoid "personal attributes" targeting language in ad copy (e.g., "Are you suffering from...")
- Before/after claims require careful framing -- show lifestyle improvement, not disease resolution
- Supplement ads frequently flagged -- keep claims within structure/function territory

### TikTok
- TikTok restricts health claims in ads similarly to Meta
- UGC-style content still must comply with FDA/FTC guidelines
- Creator partnerships must disclose material connections
- Avoid diagnostic language even in trend-following content

### Google/YouTube
- Google restricts health supplement advertising
- Claims must be clearly substantiated
- Landing pages must include required disclaimers
- Avoid restricted health terminology in ad copy

### Email/Owned Channels
- Same FDA/FTC rules apply to email marketing
- Required disclaimers must appear
- More flexibility in educational content, but claims still must be structure/function only

## 13. Case Studies: Companies That Got Fined (Source: Client Claims Bible)

These serve as cautionary examples of what happens when supplement companies make prohibited claims:

| Company | Year | Violation | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GOLO, LLC** | 2021 | Advertised supplements could treat diabetes (disease claims not FDA approved) | $2.5 million fine, cease unsubstantiated claims |
| **Prevagen** | 2019 | Marketed as "clinically proven" to improve memory (clinical trials were inconclusive) | Sued by FTC and NY State, required to stop misleading claims |
| **Lumosity** | 2016 | Claimed brain games could prevent cognitive decline and reduce Alzheimer's risk | $2 million FTC fine, required customer notification |
| **Airborne Health** | 2008 | Claimed supplement could prevent colds and boost immunity (disease prevention) | $23.3 million FTC settlement |
| **POM Wonderful** | 2013 | Claimed pomegranate juice reduces heart disease, prostate cancer risk (insufficient studies) | $35 million fine, cease unsupported claims |

**Key Lesson:** Even "clinically proven" language can be a violation if the clinical evidence doesn't fully support the claim as stated. Our 12-week trial data must be referenced precisely.
