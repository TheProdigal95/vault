## The Skeptical Psychology Convert — Journey Map

### Trigger
- **Signal from reviews:** "I tried various sleep solutions, from noise machines to aromatic oils and even prescription aids. Sadly, each fell short. They either served as distractions or left me with an undesirable mental fog." — `appstore-us-most-10193721293`
- **Signal strength:** ~22 reviews reference the moment of turning to a psychological approach after physical solutions failed / total Skeptical Psychology Convert reviews: 50
- **Pattern:** The trigger is not the beginning of insomnia — it's the exhaustion of the physical-fix category. Something prompts a reframe: "maybe this is in my head" or "maybe pills aren't the answer." A friend recommendation, a late-night Google search, or a doctor's offhand mention of CBT-I creates the first opening.

### Awareness to Consideration
- **Query language (from review content):** "CBT-I," "cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia," "psychology for insomnia," "app for insomnia that's not meditation" — reviewers describe being frustrated by generic wellness apps and searching specifically for a clinical protocol
- **Competitors they compare against:** Generic meditation apps (Calm, Headspace mentioned by category, not always by name); VA's free CBT-I Coach app (mentioned by 2 reviewers as a comparison point — free but less guided, requires more self-motivation); Sleep Reset (mentioned once, negatively, as a comparable-priced alternative that also failed)
- **Information gaps:** "I wish I had known earlier that CBT-I exists"; "I didn't know chronic insomnia was different from normal sleep difficulty"; "I didn't realize SRT would be this difficult before I signed up"

### Evaluation
- **Deciding factors cited in reviews:** Harvard credibility signal ("They're from Harvard. Maybe it'll work.") — `appstore-us-most-8550819579`; Clinical outcome data (80% see improvement, specific minute-level sleep gains); Word-of-mouth recommendation from a trusted person ("My friend recommended Stellar Sleep to me this January") — `appstore-us-most-10209334086`; FSA/HSA eligibility (not yet appearing in reviews but present as a potential factor)
- **Anxieties that almost stopped purchase:** Price opacity — discovering $188 after the quiz; SRT described as "brutal" in reviews; Worry that an app can't replicate in-person therapy; "I don't want to get caught in a subscription I can't cancel"
- **Decisive moment / tipping point:** Either the Harvard credential ("If Harvard can't help me, nothing can") — `appstore-us-most-8550819579` — or desperation: "I had already spent hundreds of dollars buying things so why not try something else."

### Purchase
- **Preferred path:** 7-day trial (sometimes $0.99, sometimes $0) → subscription. Trial is the entry; the quiz-first funnel creates investment before price reveal.
- **Offer sensitivity:** Several reviews mention discovering significant discounts by attempting to cancel ("Select 'too expensive' as the reason and receive a massive discount") — `appstore-au-most-11980299847`. The standard $188/quarter price triggers sticker shock in a significant minority.

### Use / Post-purchase

- **Ritual language:** "I usually spend about 15–30 minutes a day in the app (I like poking around the different charts)" — `appstore-us-most-10209334086`; "Every morning" check-ins, sleep diary completed as a daily ritual
- **Positive surprises:** The 2-week inflection point arrives earlier than expected; "Some of the stuff they tell you to do just seems completely illogical, but somehow, through their sequence of instructions, I can sleep again" — `appstore-us-most-8550819579`
- **Negative surprises:** Content drops after ~30 days; "I wish more human interaction was available — let me talk to someone face to face (on Zoom) or build a forum" — `appstore-us-most-10193721293`

### Advocacy / Churn

- **What they tell others:** "My friend recommended Stellar Sleep to me this January, and I've been telling her that she probably saved my life." — `appstore-us-most-10209334086`; "I recommend it highly" language appears in many reviews; specific recommendation language: "Stop your worrying and get this app!" — `appstore-nz-most-8581548236`

## The Chronic Everything-Failer — Journey Map

### Trigger
- **Signal from reviews:** "The program in this app ended a decade long stretch of insomnia. Before finding this I tried everything from supplements (magnesium, melatonin) to prescription medication to lifestyle changes (no caffeine, exercise more, etc) and nothing really worked." — `appstore-us-most-10567740942`
- **Signal strength:** ~22 reviews reference the pattern of years-long or decades-long failed attempts / total Chronic Everything-Failer reviews: 30
- **Pattern:** The trigger is usually a new crisis moment — a sleep tracker confirming weeks of zero sleep, a health scare from sleep deprivation, a major life event that worsened existing insomnia. After years of sub-threshold coping, something tips them into active problem-solving again.

### Awareness to Consideration
- **Query language (from review content):** "app for chronic insomnia," "CBT-I app," "something that actually works for insomnia"; reviews show people who had already researched CBT-I and found the VA free app but found it too unguided
- **Competitors they compare against:** VA CBT-I Coach app (free but more self-directed — noted as inferior in guidance); generic supplements (melatonin, magnesium — referenced as category failures); prescription medication (Ambien, Temazepam — effective but dependency-forming); lifestyle interventions (exercise, caffeine restriction — "I've tried all these things" language)
- **Information gaps:** "Nobody told me that CBT-I is the first-line treatment" — the American College of Physicians recommendation cited in blog content is largely unknown to these users before finding the app

### Evaluation
- **Deciding factors:** Desperation as the primary driver ("I had already spent hundreds of dollars buying things so why not try something else") — `appstore-us-most-8550819579`; Harvard credibility provides the final permission to try; the quiz-based personalization framing sets expectation of a customized solution (not another generic program)
- **Anxieties that almost stopped purchase:** "This is probably just another thing that won't work"; price opacity ($188 after 20-minute quiz investment); "What if SRT makes me worse before better"
- **Decisive moment / tipping point:** Usually reaching a physical or psychological breaking point — Fitbit showing 0 hours, heart palpitations from sleep deprivation, "I was becoming a shell of myself" — `appstore-us-most-12662727925`

### Purchase
- **Preferred path:** Quiz → trial → subscription. This persona often describes buying "on impulse" after research exhaustion — the urgency of desperation overcomes normal evaluation friction.
- **Offer sensitivity:** Less price-sensitive than other personas once they've committed (several describe $188 as "worth triple the price" after results) but the undisclosed price before quiz completion still creates friction at the funnel entry.

### Use / Post-purchase
- **Month 1 vs. month 3+ language:**
  - Month 1: "The first week using Slumber One I didn't think much was changing. More money down the drain." — `appstore-us-most-8550819579`. Initial weeks often worse due to SRT.
  - Month 3+: Long-form gratitude reviews. "This app has transformed and improved my sleep in just one week. I am grateful to have found it." — `appstore-us-most-12802874672`
- **Ritual language:** Sleep diary as daily anchor; progress tracking (sleep efficiency percentage, Fitbit confirmation); accountability coaches designated from personal network — `appstore-us-most-10209334086`
- **Positive surprises:** Speed of results: "transformed and improved my sleep in just one week" — `appstore-us-most-12802874672`; the logical illogicality of SRT suddenly making sense
- **Negative surprises:** Content cliff after month 1; SRT severity before the benefit arrives; some find the quiz-generated "personalized" program feels generic — `appstore-us-most-11260868632`

### Advocacy / Churn
- **Why they stay:** After decades of failure, successful treatment creates extreme loyalty. These are the reviewers who write 400-word essays. Several describe telling family members.
- **Why they leave:** Billing issues; program content depletion after ~30 days; the few who don't see results by week 4 typically churn (SRT didn't work for their specific sleep-maintenance insomnia pattern)
- **What they tell others:** "My friend recommended Stellar Sleep to me this January, and I've been telling her that she probably saved my life." — `appstore-us-most-10209334086`; "I told my [partner/family]" language common in 5★ long-form reviews

## The Sleep Anxiety Spiraler — Journey Map

### Trigger
- **Signal from reviews:** "It became so bad I would panic at bedtime, knowing I wouldn't get adequate rest. I knew it had become mental at this point as well and I needed help." — `appstore-us-most-12224394054`
- **Signal strength:** ~12 reviews describe the moment of recognizing the anxiety-sleep feedback loop / total Sleep Anxiety Spiraler reviews: 20
- **Pattern:** Often triggered by a life transition — new baby, perimenopause, high-stress job, bereavement — that creates the first bad nights. The initial sleep problem then spawns sleep anxiety, which becomes the primary driver of insomnia. The trigger is often the moment of recognizing "my fear of not sleeping is the thing keeping me awake."

### Awareness to Consideration
- **Query language (from review content):** "CBT-I for anxiety," "sleep anxiety app," "bedtime anxiety treatment"; one reviewer was "in regular therapy to address my problems with anxiety" and arrived via the therapy channel — `appstore-us-most-11277848613`

- **Information gaps:** "I didn't know most sleep issues are related to anxiety" — `appstore-us-most-11277848613`; "I didn't know CBT-I had specific tools for the anxiety component"

### Evaluation
- **Deciding factors:** The cognitive/skills-based framing of Stellar Sleep's positioning resonates strongly: "emphasis on building skills that I can use to tackle my specific sleep issues" — `appstore-us-most-9687450171`; the "root cause" language is specific to this persona's self-understanding (they already know their problem is mental)

### Use / Post-purchase

## The Pill Escape Seeker — Journey Map

### Trigger
- **Signal from reviews:** "I finally went to my new doctor... was prescribed Temazepam for sleep, which usually worked, but I didn't want to get addicted." — `appstore-us-most-11316861661`
- **Signal strength:** ~8 reviews reference the medication-dependency fear or desire to stop pills / total Pill Escape Seeker reviews: 11
- **Pattern:** Two sub-triggers: (1) Doctor prescribes sleep medication, person fills it, then worries about dependency. (2) Person has been on OTC sleep aids (melatonin, NyQuil, Benadryl) and finds they stop working. The trigger is a moment of recognizing the dependency pattern.

### Awareness to Consideration
- **Query language (from review content):** "how to stop sleeping pills," "CBT-I as alternative to Ambien," "insomnia without medication"; this persona typically searches on the medication exit path, not the insomnia entry path
- **Competitors they compare against:** The medication category itself (melatonin, THC, prescription sleep aids); in-person CBT-I therapists (waitlist, cost); VA CBT-I Coach app (free but less guided)
- **Information gaps:** "I didn't know CBT-I produces permanent results after the treatment ends"; "I didn't know there was an app alternative to in-person sleep therapy"

### Evaluation
- **Deciding factors:** The medication-independent mechanism of CBT-I is the primary draw; Harvard clinical credibility; review testimonials specifically mentioning getting off medication; the 30-day money-back guarantee reduces financial risk
- **Anxieties that almost stopped purchase:** "Will this produce enough sleep change for me to stop taking my prescription?"; the $188 price vs. ongoing medication cost comparison
- **Decisive moment / tipping point:** Doctor reference, friend recommendation, or a review describing successful medication cessation — `appstore-nz-most-11472026575`

### Purchase
- **Preferred path:** Quiz → trial → subscription. Often co-presenting with Chronic Everything-Failer characteristics — this persona has usually tried many things. The healthcare-adjacent framing (FSA/HSA eligible, clinical protocol) is a purchase accelerator.
- **Offer sensitivity:** Moderate. Will pay for clinical effectiveness but price comparison to a therapist's per-session rate is relevant.

### Use / Post-purchase
- **Month 1 vs. month 3+ language:**
  - Month 1: "By the middle of the second week I stopped taking melatonin and about 10 other supplements." — `appstore-us-most-11123687667` — early medication reduction
  - Month 3+: "3 months later I'm off my sleeping pills and no longer have crushing anxiety about going to bed." — `appstore-nz-most-11472026575`
- **Ritual language:** "I no longer need coffee to kickstart my day" — `appstore-us-most-10241843854`; the cessation of the morning-compensating ritual (coffee, naps) signals program success
- **Positive surprises:** Speed of medication reduction; the relief of falling asleep without anything is described as fundamental — "I can sleep on my own without sleep meds for about 6 and a half hours" — `appstore-us-most-10460900572`
- **Negative surprises:** Some find the program too generic for their specific medication-tapering context; "I feel it's generic. Not custom to me personally." — `appstore-us-most-11260868632`

### Advocacy / Churn
- **Why they stay:** Medication cessation is an ongoing goal — the app's maintenance content supports sustained independence. High retention language: "stuck with the program" — `appstore-ca-most-11609113463`
- **Why they leave:** Program content depletion after month 1; those who don't achieve medication cessation by month 3 may churn feeling the investment was incomplete
- **What they tell others:** No review signal — inferred from adjacent Chronic Everything-Failer and Skeptical Psychology Convert advocacy patterns.

- **Pattern:** This persona's "journey" is a commercial failure arc, not a sleep improvement arc. The trigger is the discovery of a charge — either on completing the quiz and discovering the price, or after cancellation, finding charges continued.

### Awareness to Consideration
- **Query language:** No signal — this persona arrives via the same channels as other personas (paid search, word of mouth, App Store browsing). The billing problem is discovered post-purchase, not pre-purchase.
- **Competitors they compare against:** No signal — inferred that post-billing-burn, this persona researches alternatives and discovers CBT-I alternatives (VA app, psychologists) — `appstore-au-most-12941920673`
- **Information gaps:** "I did not know the price before completing the quiz"; "I did not know cancelling the app does not cancel the PayPal subscription"

### Evaluation
- **No review signal specific to pre-purchase evaluation for this persona.** They entered through standard channels; the billing problem was post-purchase.

### Purchase
- **Path:** Quiz → $0.99 or $0 trial → subscription → surprise $188 charge. Multiple reviewers specifically describe the trial pricing as misleading ("getting into the app it suggests a $9/week cost") — `appstore-us-most-10129453768`
- **Offer sensitivity:** Very high — this persona is sensitized to pricing architecture by the discovery of hidden costs.

### Use / Post-purchase

- **What they tell others:** "READ THE REVIEWS about fraudulent billing." — `appstore-us-most-12409714849`. Active warning-spreading. These are the reviews that future prospects read before buying.
- **Advocacy (negative):** Several reviewers explicitly describe warning others. This persona is the primary source of reputation damage in App Store listings.
