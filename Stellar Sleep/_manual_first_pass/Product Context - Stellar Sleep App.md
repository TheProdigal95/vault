## Core Therapeutic Framework

### What Is CBT-I?

**CBT-I = Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia.**

It is not a relaxation app. It is not sleep hygiene tips. It is the most clinically validated treatment for chronic insomnia that exists — a structured behavioral and cognitive program that targets the root causes of insomnia rather than masking the symptoms.

**Recommended by:**
- American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) — designated as first-line treatment
- American College of Physicians
- NIH: "CBT-I produces clinically significant effects that last up to a year after therapy"

**Why it works (mechanism for copywriters):**
Chronic insomnia is a learned problem. Over time, through stress, poor sleep associations, and anxiety about sleep itself, the brain learns to associate the bed with wakefulness and dread rather than rest. CBT-I systematically unlearns those associations and rebuilds healthy ones through behavioral change, thought restructuring, and sleep schedule calibration. The changes are lasting because they rewire behavior — not because they suppress symptoms.

**The key contrast vs. pills:** Sleeping pills mask the symptom (wakefulness) without changing the underlying brain patterns. When you stop taking them, the insomnia returns. CBT-I changes the pattern itself — which is why efficacy persists for a year or more post-treatment.

**The app also incorporates:**
- ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) — for reducing the struggle against unwanted thoughts
- Motivational interviewing — for building commitment to behavioral change
- Neuroplasticity-based behavioral retraining — the brain can change; the app creates the conditions for it to do so

## The 4 Core Techniques

### 1. Sleep Restriction Therapy (SRT)

**What it is:** A strategic adjustment of the sleep-wake schedule that deliberately reduces total time in bed initially — then gradually extends it as sleep efficiency improves.

**Why it works:** SRT builds "sleep pressure" (adenosine accumulation) by consolidating fragmented sleep into a shorter, deeper window. This re-establishes the brain's drive to sleep at the right time. Over 2-4 weeks, sleep consolidates into a longer, more reliable block.

**The catch (and the honest angle):** SRT is the most effective CBT-I technique — and the most challenging. The first 1-2 weeks can feel worse before they feel better. Users frequently describe it as "brutal." This is important creative context: ads that acknowledge the difficulty and position it as the price of a permanent fix convert better with this audience than ads that promise easy results. Chronic insomniacs have been failed by "easy" solutions before.

**Long-term outcome:** Retrains the brain-to-bed association from wakefulness to sleep. Most effective technique in the CBT-I toolkit.

**Copy angles:**
- "The counterintuitive protocol that finally fixed my insomnia"
- "It felt harder before it got easier — and that's exactly how it works"
- "The technique sleep doctors use that nobody's talking about"

---

### 2. Paradoxical Intention

**What it is:** A technique where the patient is instructed to try to stay awake while lying in bed — the opposite of what they're trying to do.

**Why it works:** Insomnia is frequently maintained by "sleep performance anxiety" — the harder you try to sleep, the more aroused and alert your nervous system becomes. Paradoxical intention removes the pressure to perform, which dissolves the anxiety feedback loop and allows sleep to occur naturally.

**Copy angles:**
- "Stop trying to fall asleep. That's exactly what's keeping you awake."
- "The anxiety about sleep is making the sleep worse. There's a technique for that."
- "What if trying harder is the problem?"

---

### 3. Cognitive Restructuring

**What it is:** A structured process for identifying, challenging, and reframing unhelpful beliefs and thought patterns about sleep.

**Why it works:** Chronic insomnia is perpetuated by catastrophic thinking — "I'll never sleep again," "I need 8 hours or I can't function," "My body is broken." These thoughts trigger the stress response (cortisol spike), which physiologically prevents sleep. Cognitive restructuring replaces these automatic patterns with more accurate, functional beliefs, reducing the emotional charge that feeds the anxiety cycle.

**Copy angles:**
- "The thought that's keeping you up at 3am — and how to break it"
- "Your brain isn't broken. It just learned the wrong pattern."
- "What 10 years of insomnia actually taught your brain (and how to unteach it)"

---

### 4. Sleep Anxiety Management

**What it is:** A targeted set of tools for managing the anxiety-specific component of insomnia — racing thoughts, pre-sleep arousal, hypervigilance in bed.

**Why it works:** A significant proportion of chronic insomnia is anxiety-driven. Standard sleep hygiene (consistent schedule, dark room, no screens) doesn't address anxiety that's already activated. Sleep anxiety management provides behavioral and cognitive tools to bring the nervous system out of high-alert mode before and during the sleep window.

**Tools in this module include:** breathing exercises, progressive muscle relaxation, guided meditations calibrated for pre-sleep use.

**Copy angles:**
- "Your racing thoughts at 2am aren't random — they're a pattern. And patterns can be broken."
- "Melatonin doesn't fix anxiety. This does."
- "The anxiety is the insomnia."

## Who It's For / Who It's Not For

### Who It's For

- **Chronic insomniacs** — clinically defined: difficulty sleeping 3+ nights per week, for 3+ months
- **The already-tried crowd** — people who've failed: melatonin, sleep hygiene articles, meditation apps, OTC sleep aids, prescription sleep medications. This is not a first-resort product; it's for people who know standard approaches don't work for them
- **Anxiety-driven insomniacs** — racing thoughts, pre-sleep dread, wake-at-3am-can't-turn-off-brain profiles
- **Access-limited CBT-I candidates** — people who would benefit from in-person CBT-I therapy but face 6-month waitlists, high cost, or geographic limitations
- **Motivated behavioral changers** — people willing to commit 10 minutes/day and follow behavioral protocols, even when it's uncomfortable
- **Data-oriented users** — people who track sleep (Oura, Fitbit, Apple Health) and want to see measurable progress
- **Adults aged approximately 28-55+** — based on testimonial demographics and anxiety-insomnia profile overlap

### Who It's Not For

- People seeking passive, quick fixes (the app requires active daily engagement)
- People with maintenance insomnia only (stays asleep, can't fall back asleep) — the program may underserve this specific subtype
- Non-tech-savvy users (fully app-based, no human support component)
- Those with highly unpredictable schedules — CBT-I requires schedule consistency to work; shift workers and frequent travelers may struggle
- Skeptics or non-compliers — if someone won't follow behavioral protocols (especially SRT), results will be limited
- Budget-constrained users — $60+/month is significantly higher than free alternatives; the value prop requires buying into the clinical rationale

## Neuroplasticity Angle

This is the core brand narrative — the "why it's possible" story that makes behavioral change feel credible and hopeful to a skeptical, long-suffering audience.

### The Frame
Chronic insomnia is not a biological defect. It is a learned pattern — a set of associations and conditioned responses the brain has built over time. The brain learned to associate the bed with stress, wakefulness, and anxiety. That association can be unlearned. The brain is capable of change at any age.

### Key Lines (pull directly into copy)
- "If your brain learned to associate your bed with stress, it can learn to associate it with rest."
- "Your brain hasn't forgotten how to sleep. It just needs the right conditions."
- "Your brain is capable of change."
- "Your body remembers how to sleep naturally."
- "CBT-I teaches you skills you can use for your lifetime."

### Why This Works Creatively
Chronic insomniacs are often in a place of deep helplessness — "nothing works," "my brain is broken," "I've given up on ever sleeping normally." The neuroplasticity frame is the reframe. It:
1. Validates the suffering ("your brain did learn something real")
2. Removes blame and shame ("this isn't a personal failure")
3. Opens possibility ("the same way it learned, it can unlearn")
4. Establishes mechanism ("here's how CBT-I creates that unlearning")

This is the emotional unlock before the rational proof. The best performing ads in this category tend to hit the helplessness validation before introducing the solution — not the other way around.

### Narrative Arc for Scripts
**Hook:** The feeling of lying awake, knowing you're about to face another day destroyed. (Validates the lived experience.)
**Shift:** "What if your brain isn't broken — it just learned something wrong?" (The neuroplasticity reframe.)
**Mechanism:** CBT-I unlearns the pattern — and here's why that's different from everything you've tried. (Rational proof.)
**Proof:** Real user numbers. Harvard study. 4.7 stars. 10,000 users. (Credibility stack.)
**Close:** 7-day free trial. 10 minutes a day. (Low-commitment entry.)

## Pricing Strategy & Conversion Mechanics

### The Pricing Architecture

The pricing structure is designed to create trial accessibility while monetizing on a 3-month commitment basis — matching the program's clinical timeline.

| Stage | Mechanic | Strategic Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 7-day free trial | No upfront cost; experience the assessment and first modules | Removes risk barrier for skeptics who've been failed before |
| ~$60-63/month | Subscription price | Premium positioning vs. Calm ($70/yr) and Headspace ($70/yr) — but priced against therapy ($100-250/session), not apps |
| 3-month minimum | Auto-renewal cycle | Aligns with the 5-week clinical timeline; prevents churn before results kick in |
| 30-day money-back | Full refund if requested within first month of full subscription | De-risks purchase decision; reduces objection at checkout |
| Promotional pricing | Podcast/influencer-specific offers (e.g., 50% off for life) | Acquisition tool for high-trust referral channels |

### Copy Implications

**The trial is the hook.** Every cold-traffic ad should have a clear path to "start your free trial." The 7-day free trial is the conversion event — not the subscription decision.

**Anchor against therapy, not apps.** "$63/month" sounds expensive anchored to Calm or Headspace. It sounds like a bargain anchored against "6-month waitlist + $200/session for a CBT-I therapist." Always anchor high.

**The 3-month minimum is a feature, not a bug — when framed correctly.** "The program is designed to change your brain over 5 weeks. The 3-month commitment is there because results compound — not because we're locking you in." This framing converts better than burying the commitment.

**The 30-day guarantee is the safety net.** Frame it as: "If it doesn't work in the first month, you don't pay." This reduces risk perception for the high-hesitancy chronic insomniac audience.

**Price sensitivity exists.** $60+/month is a real barrier for a segment of the audience. Counter by:
- Anchoring against the cost of what they're already doing (OTC meds, Rx co-pays, productivity loss from poor sleep)
- The "permanent fix" framing (monthly cost × 6 months vs. lifetime result)
- Promotional codes in high-trust channels (podcast, creator partnerships)
