# Persona Context - Stellar Sleep

Primary creative reference document for all Stellar Sleep briefs and scripts. All five personas below are drawn from App Store reviews, Google Play reviews, founder accounts, clinical literature on CBT-I, and publicly documented customer testimonials. No claims are invented.

---

## Framework Glossary

**TEEP Stages:** Trigger → Exploration → Evaluation → Purchase. Maps where the persona is in their journey to buying.

**Three Selves:**
- **Ought Self** — who they feel they should be (functional, rested, performing)
- **Actual Self** — who they are right now (exhausted, anxious, compromised)
- **Ideal Self** — who they want to become (sleeping deeply, waking refreshed, back to normal)

**Emotional Zones (valence × intensity):**
- High-negative intensity: panic, despair, dread
- Low-negative intensity: resignation, flat affect, numbness
- High-positive intensity: hope, relief, excitement
- Low-positive intensity: cautious optimism, quiet satisfaction

## 1. The Anxious High-Achiever

### Part 1: How Stellar Sleep Connects to The Anxious High-Achiever

This persona's insomnia is performance-driven. The mind that made them successful — fast-processing, always-on, problem-solving — becomes the mechanism of their sleep disorder. Stellar Sleep addresses this directly through CBT-I's core cognitive restructuring techniques, which target the anxious thought loops that prevent sleep, not just the symptoms.

| Mechanism | What Stellar Sleep Does | Why It Matters to This Persona |
|---|---|---|
| Cognitive Restructuring | Teaches the persona to identify and challenge catastrophic sleep thoughts ("If I don't sleep, tomorrow is ruined") | High-achievers operate on logic — showing them that their thoughts are distorting reality and giving them a tool to counter it fits their problem-solving identity |
| Sleep Restriction Therapy | Temporarily narrows the sleep window to rebuild sleep drive and break the cycle of lying awake in bed | This is a science-backed protocol, not a passive relaxation technique. The rigor appeals to someone who wants a real fix, not generic advice |
| Stimulus Control | Re-associates the bedroom with sleep rather than anxious wakefulness | Addresses the conditioned dread that high-achievers develop — they've brought work anxiety into the bedroom, literally and psychologically |
| Sleep Efficiency Training | Measures and improves the ratio of time asleep to time in bed | Gives this persona a metric to track, which satisfies the optimization instinct without feeding the counterproductive obsession with hours |
| 10 Min/Day Format | The program works in short daily sessions | Removes the "I don't have time for another commitment" objection for people who already feel time-poor |
| 2-Week Initial Results | Most users experience measurable improvement in 2 weeks | Fast enough for someone who needs proof before they'll commit to a longer process |

### Part 2: Deep Customer Profile — The High-Performer Whose Brain Won't Clock Out

This person has built their career on being sharp. They may be a product manager at a tech company, a consultant at a Big Four firm, a physician, a startup founder, or a senior finance professional. They are 28 to 45. They are used to solving problems — including health problems. When their knee hurt, they went to a physio and did the exercises. When they felt anxious at work, they read books on it or went to therapy. When their diet was off, they tracked macros or hired a nutritionist.

Sleep is the one problem they cannot engineer their way out of. And that fact alone is maddening.

They lie down and their brain launches into its evening debrief: emails they should have sent, the conversation that went wrong, the deliverable due Friday, the colleague they need to manage, the career decision they keep avoiding. By the time they notice they're thinking too hard, an hour has passed and the anxiety about not sleeping has compounded the original anxiety about work.

They've downloaded Calm. Tried the sleep stories. They feel slightly patronizing. They've bought blue-light-blocking glasses. They've tried melatonin — it does nothing for a mind this wired. They've read the sleep hygiene articles and implemented every tip. Still awake at 1am. Still waking at 5am with their heart beating too fast.

They are not yet at prescription medication. They don't want to go there. They want something they can understand mechanically, something that addresses the actual problem, something that treats them as intelligent.

- **Alcohol before bed:** Initially seems to help sleep onset, but worsens sleep quality and causes earlier waking. Many tried this phase and abandoned it.

### Part 5: Objection Handling

**Objection 1:** "Is this just another meditation app?"
- **Root:** They've been burned by Calm and Headspace. They assume any sleep app = relaxation content. Meditation feels passive and ineffective for a racing mind.
- **Counter:** CBT-I is the opposite of relaxation content. It's a structured behavioral protocol — the same one sleep medicine clinics use — that works by changing how your brain relates to sleep, not by trying to calm you down. Stellar Sleep teaches cognitive restructuring, sleep restriction, and stimulus control. You won't find guided breathing exercises here.
- **Tone:** Direct and confident. Don't over-explain. Match their skepticism with specificity.

**Objection 2:** "I've already tried apps. Why would this be different?"
- **Root:** Rational skepticism based on past failed purchases. They don't want to waste $60 on something that doesn't address the root cause.
- **Counter:** The apps you've tried treat symptoms (relaxation before bed). CBT-I treats the cause — the cognitive patterns and behavioral habits that sustain insomnia. It's the same difference as painkillers vs. physiotherapy for a chronic injury. One manages the pain; the other fixes the underlying problem.
- **Tone:** Validate the frustration, then make the mechanism distinction clearly. Avoid overselling.

**Objection 3:** "I don't have time for another program."
- **Root:** Time scarcity is real for this persona. They're already time-poor and skeptical of adding another commitment.
- **Counter:** 10 minutes a day. That's the full program. Less than a coffee break. And the payoff is getting those 1–2 hours back every night that you're currently spending lying awake.
- **Tone:** Matter-of-fact. Frame it as a ROI calculation, not a lifestyle pitch.

**Objection 4:** "Maybe if I just manage my stress better, the sleep will fix itself."
- **Root:** They believe the problem is upstream (stress) and that addressing sleep directly is treating a symptom. This keeps them from taking action.
- **Counter:** Stress triggers insomnia — but insomnia sustains itself through its own cognitive and behavioral patterns independent of the original stressor. Research shows that even when the stress resolves, the sleep disorder persists because the brain has learned to associate the bedroom with wakefulness. That learned pattern requires a specific intervention to undo.
- **Tone:** Respectful of their intelligence. Give them the mechanism. This is the same objection Edrei (co-founder) had before he discovered CBT-I.

## 3. The Middle-of-Night Waker (Maintenance Insomniac)

### Part 1: How Stellar Sleep Connects to The Middle-of-Night Waker

This persona's insomnia is maintenance insomnia — they fall asleep, then wake at 2am, 3am, 4am and lie awake for 1–3 hours before either falling back to sleep just before the alarm, or not at all. This is functionally different from sleep onset insomnia, and most sleep advice (and many sleep apps) doesn't speak to it specifically. This represents both a challenge and an opportunity for Stellar Sleep.

| Mechanism | What Stellar Sleep Does | Why It Matters to This Persona |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep Architecture Education | Explains that waking during the night is normal — REM cycles mean brief arousals are natural — but anxiety and hyperarousal in the waking window is what causes the problem to sustain | Reframes the 3am waking as not the problem itself, but as the trigger for a secondary anxiety loop that then sustains wakefulness |
| Cognitive Techniques for Middle-of-Night | Addresses the catastrophic thinking that activates when they wake ("How many hours left?" "I have to be up at 6") | The 3am thought spiral is a distinct cognitive pattern — clock-watching, calculating lost sleep, dreading the day ahead |
| Sleep Restriction | Rebuilds sleep drive by consolidating sleep, which gradually reduces fragmentation | Addresses maintenance insomnia directly — compresses the window to build deeper, less fragmented sleep |
| Stimulus Control for the Waking Window | If awake for 20+ minutes, leave the bed — re-associate the bed with sleep, not with the experience of lying awake mid-night | Specific technique for the maintenance insomniac: what to do when they wake up |

### Part 5: Objection Handling

**Objection 1:** "Maybe I should just wait it out — this might resolve on its own."
- **Root:** Underestimation of how quickly situational insomnia becomes structural. They believe time heals it.
- **Counter:** Insomnia that persists more than 3 months after the original trigger has typically stopped being about the trigger and started being about the behavioral and cognitive patterns that have formed around it. The brain has started to associate the bedroom with wakefulness, and the anxiety about sleep has become its own sustaining loop. Waiting allows that pattern to calcify. Intervening now — when the pattern is 3–6 months old rather than 3–6 years old — is significantly more effective.
- **Tone:** Informative urgency. Not fear-mongering. Give them the mechanism.

**Objection 2:** "I don't think my insomnia is severe enough to need a clinical program."
- **Root:** Minimization. They compare themselves to people with "real" chronic insomnia and feel they don't qualify.
- **Counter:** CBT-I is not only for severe chronic insomnia. It's the recommended intervention for any persistent insomnia — including the early-stage disruption that hasn't yet become long-term. Treating it now, before the patterns become entrenched, is significantly easier than treating it after years of conditioning.
- **Tone:** Normalize the intervention. You don't wait until you have a broken bone to get it set — same principle.

**Objection 3:** "Isn't this just stress? If I deal with the stress, the sleep will come back."
- **Root:** Correct understanding of the trigger but incorrect understanding of why the problem persists after the trigger resolves.
- **Counter:** Stress can trigger insomnia — but insomnia that has persisted for months after the stressful event has ended is no longer about the stress. The brain has learned to associate the bedroom with wakefulness, and the anxiety about sleep has become self-sustaining independent of the original cause. Managing stress is valuable — but it won't reverse a learned sleep pattern that now runs on its own.
- **Tone:** Empathic and educational. Meet them where they are — they're not wrong that stress was the cause; they just haven't heard the mechanism for why it persists.

**Objection 4:** "I've been using Calm / Headspace — isn't that enough?"
- **Root:** They've already invested in a solution and don't want to believe it's insufficient. Or they've seen improvement and don't realize it's not addressing the root pattern.
- **Counter:** Relaxation apps help with the symptoms of bedtime anxiety, which can be genuinely helpful. But they don't address the behavioral patterns (going to bed too early, lying in bed awake, daytime napping to compensate) or the cognitive patterns (catastrophic thoughts about sleep, clock-watching, hyperarousal) that sustain insomnia. CBT-I addresses those specifically. Think of it as the difference between taking ibuprofen for a sprained ankle and doing the physiotherapy.
- **Tone:** Validate the partial value of what they're already doing, then make the distinction.
