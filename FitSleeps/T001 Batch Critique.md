# T001 Batch Critique

> Critique of all 7 scripts (V1, V2, V3, V4, I5, I6, I7) in the FitSleeps T001 batch. Organized by issue type with every instance across all scripts. Read against V1 as quality bar, Working Document as criteria reference, Sleep Science Research as claims reference.

---

## Issue 1: Pacing — Lines Over 320 SPM

**The problem:** The Working Document says creators should speak at a "conversational pace, not rush." At 250 spm, a normal conversational pace feels natural. At 300 spm, it's brisk but workable for short energetic lines. Above 320 spm, the line will either sound rushed when spoken at the intended pace, or the creator will run over the timestamp and compress the next segment. V1 sits comfortably at 180-270 spm across all segments. V2 has the worst pacing in the batch.

**Every instance found:**

**V2 Hook B (5s):** "If you're a heavy sleeper, regular alarms were never going to work on you. Here's what actually does." ~27 syllables in 5 seconds = ~324 spm.

**V2 Hook C (4s):** "Your alarm is waking her up every single morning, and she's probably not telling you." ~24 syllables in 4 seconds = ~360 spm. This is the fastest line in the batch.

**V2 Body 0:04-0:12 (8s):** "Here's what's happening. When you're deep asleep, your brain produces something called sleep spindles. They block sound before it can wake you. So your alarm goes off, and your brain just filters it out." ~47 syllables in 8 seconds = ~352 spm. The longest segment in V2 and physically impossible to deliver naturally.

**V2 Body 0:12-0:19 (7s):** "That's why a smartwatch buzz is too weak, a sunrise lamp lights up the whole room, and a phone under the pillow still rattles. Same problem, different packaging." ~38 syllables in 7 seconds = ~326 spm.

**V2 Body 0:19-0:27 (8s):** "That's why I switched to this. It's called FitSleeps. It vibrates on your wrist instead of making sound, so it reaches your brain through a completely different pathway. One your brain hasn't learned to block." ~50 syllables in 8 seconds = ~375 spm. The most overpacked line in the entire batch.

**V3 Body 0:28-0:36 (8s):** "That's when I found FitSleeps. It's just a band on your wrist. No sound, no light. It vibrates and gets stronger until you're up, so your brain can't learn to ignore it the way it does with sound." ~44 syllables in 8 seconds = ~330 spm.

**V3 Body 0:40-0:46 (6s):** "They give you a hundred days to try it. 175,000 people already have. And it sells out, so if it's in stock, grab it." ~33 syllables in 6 seconds = ~330 spm.

**V1, V4:** All lines land at or below 320 spm. V1 is the best-paced script in the batch (180-270 spm range). V4 is tight at 317 spm on one body segment but within tolerance.

**Total count:** 7 lines above 320 spm. 5 of them are in V2, which makes V2 the script most in need of a pacing pass.

**Fix:** V2 needs a full retime. Either extend timestamps by 1-2 seconds per segment (pushing total duration from 41s to ~47s), or cut 20-30% of the VO text. The mechanism explanation tries to pack too many concepts into too little time. The "sleep spindles" segment at 0:04-0:12 should be split across a longer window or simplified. V3's two flagged lines can be fixed by extending each by 1 second.

## Issue 2: Product Bridges — Repetition Across Scripts

**The problem:** The Working Document says "Each script's product bridge must be unique to that concept." A product bridge is the transition from problem/mechanism to product introduction. If multiple scripts use the same bridge language, a viewer who sees more than one ad hears the same pitch twice.

**Every instance found:**

**V1:** "Then my buddy who's a paramedic showed me this band. It's called FitSleeps." Word-of-mouth bridge. Unique.

**V2:** "That's why I switched to this. It's called FitSleeps." Direct switch bridge. Unique phrasing, though "that's why" + "it's called FitSleeps" is a structural match to V3.

**V3:** "That's when I found FitSleeps. It's just a band on your wrist." Discovery bridge. The "It's called FitSleeps" / "That's when I found FitSleeps" construction is structurally similar to V2's "It's called FitSleeps."

**V4:** "It's called FitSleeps. No sound, no app." Direct naming. Third instance of "It's called FitSleeps."

**I5:** Cassandra holds up her wrist, narrative discovery. No "it's called FitSleeps" — the name appears later when she reads the clasp. Unique.

**I6:** Mike found it from a shift worker forum. Narrative discovery. Unique.

**I7:** ADHD parent support group friend told her about it. Occupational therapist mentioned it. Unique.

**The phrase "It's called FitSleeps" appears in V2, V3, and V4.** While the surrounding context differs, the identical naming construction creates a sameness across three of four video scripts. V1 avoids this by weaving the name into the word-of-mouth story. The image ads avoid it through narrative discovery.

**Fix:** V4 should drop "It's called FitSleeps" since the product was already named in Hook B (S tier) and Hook C ("how FitSleeps wakes me up"). The body can go straight to "FitSleeps. No sound, no app." V3 could shift to something like "That's when someone showed me FitSleeps" to match the discovery-after-exhaustion concept. V2's "It's called FitSleeps" makes sense for the educational format and can stay.

## Issue 3: Close Structure Variety

**The problem:** The Working Document specifies 4 different close types for the 4 video scripts and story closes for the 3 image ads. The distribution table assigns: V1 = Emotional, V2 = Proof cascade, V3 = Ease/simplicity, V4 = Urgency/offer, I5/I6/I7 = Story.

**Checking actual closes:**

**V1:** "I finally feel in control of my mornings. And at this stage in life, that's everything. Thanks, FitSleeps." **Emotional.** Matches assignment. Clean.

**V2:** "175,000 customers. 4.6 stars on Trustpilot. And you get 100 days to try it risk-free. She's going to notice the difference before you do." **Proof cascade.** Matches assignment. The last sentence adds an emotional partner beat, which is good.

**V3:** "One band. One purpose. And it actually works. FitSleeps. Try it for a hundred nights." **Ease/simplicity.** Matches assignment. Clean. But note: "Try it for a hundred nights" also invokes the guarantee/offer, which blurs into V4's territory.

**V4:** "They give you a hundred days to try it. 175,000 people already have. If it's in stock right now, don't wait." **Urgency/offer.** Matches assignment. But note: "175,000 people already have" is proof cascade language, overlapping with V2.

**V3 Body 0:40-0:46** (which is functionally part of the close flow): "They give you a hundred days to try it. 175,000 people already have. And it sells out, so if it's in stock, grab it." This is almost identical to V4's close. Same structure, same proof point, same scarcity language.

**I5:** "Turns out I was one band away from keeping my career." Story close. Strong. Unique.

**I6:** "She came back to our bed on a Tuesday. That's all I needed to know." Story close. Strong. Unique.

**I7:** "He made it to every class this semester. First time since middle school." Story close. Strong. Unique.

**The real issue:** V3's last body segment (0:40-0:46) is functionally a second close that duplicates V4's actual close almost word for word. Both say "They give you a hundred days to try it. 175,000 people already have." V3 then says "if it's in stock, grab it." V4 says "If it's in stock right now, don't wait." These are the same close.

**Fix:** V3's last body segment should be rolled into the close or removed. The ease/simplicity close ("One band. One purpose. And it actually works.") is the assigned close type and should stand alone without a preceding guarantee/scarcity segment that belongs to V4's territory. Move the 100-day guarantee mention to a caption overlay or cut it from V3 entirely — V3's close should feel like relief after the overwhelm, not a sales pitch.

## Issue 4: Hook/Body Redundancy

**The problem:** When a hook uses a phrase that the body repeats, the viewer hears the same concept twice in the first 10 seconds. The Working Document explicitly flags this: "Never repeat the same phrasing between a hook variation and the body."

**Every instance found:**

**V3 Hook A / Body:** Hook A says "I'm a heavy sleeper. I've tried every alarm solution out there. They all failed. Except this one." The body opens at 0:05 with "I bought a dual-bell alarm clock..." which begins the parade of failed solutions. This is NOT redundancy — the hook promises a story, the body delivers it. The hook opens a loop. Cleared.

**V4 Hook B / Body:** Hook B introduces FitSleeps inside the hook as "S tier. Only thing that actually works." The body then says "It's called FitSleeps. No sound, no app." The product is named twice — once as S tier in the hook, then reintroduced formally in the body. For Hook B specifically, the body should skip the naming and go straight to benefits: "No sound, no app. Just a vibration on your wrist..."

**V4 Hook C / Body:** Hook C says "a quiet buzz on my wrist and I'm just up." The body says "Just a vibration on your wrist that gets stronger until you're up." Both describe vibration on the wrist as the mechanism. The body re-explains what the hook already demonstrated.

**V2 Hook A / Body:** Hook A says "The real reason you sleep through alarms has nothing to do with how tired you are." Body opens at 0:04 with "Here's what's happening. When you're deep asleep, your brain produces something called sleep spindles." This is a good loop-and-deliver pattern. No redundancy. Cleared.

**V1 Hook C / Body:** Hook C says "I'm suing FitSleeps." Body opens with "I'm a heavy sleeper." Clean pivot, no redundancy. Cleared.

**Total count:** 2 instances (both in V4). V4's body needs to account for the fact that Hook B already names the product and Hook C already explains the mechanism.

**Fix:** V4 body segment 1 should have two versions: one for Hook A (where the product hasn't been named yet) that keeps "It's called FitSleeps," and a trimmed version for Hooks B and C that skips the naming and goes straight to "No sound, no app. Just a vibration on your wrist..." Or, since this adds editorial complexity, simply drop "It's called FitSleeps" from the body since all three hooks have already introduced the product or concept by name.

## Issue 5: Partner Angle Coverage

**The problem:** The Working Document says "Even heavy sleeper ads should include a partner beat somewhere." The partner angle appears in 7 of 10 FitSleeps top spenders.

**Checking every script:**

| Script | Persona | Partner Beat | Line |
|---|---|---|---|
| V1 | Heavy Sleeper + Partner | Yes (expanded) | "My wife sleeps right through it. She has no idea my alarm even went off." |
| V2 | Considerate Partner | Yes (primary thread) | "She stays asleep. She doesn't even know your alarm went off." |
| V3 | Heavy Sleeper | Yes (single line) | "My girlfriend has no idea it even went off." |
| V4 | Heavy Sleeper | Yes (single line) | "Your partner won't hear a thing." |
| I5 | Shift Worker | Yes (secondary) | Priya (roommate) hears the alarms, doesn't hear the band. Also Priya ordering one for her brother who's a paramedic. |
| I6 | Considerate Partner | Yes (primary) | Entire narrative is the partner story. |
| I7 | Parent for ADHD Teen | No (roommate replaces partner) | "Roommate still sleeping" is the only mention. No romantic partner. |

**I5 note:** Priya is a roommate, not a romantic partner. The Working Document says "partner," and the top spenders all feature a romantic partner (wife, girlfriend). I5 technically satisfies "someone else in the house isn't disturbed" but misses the emotional weight of the romantic partner angle. The shift worker persona is single (broke up with Javi). This is a defensible creative choice — forcing a partner into a single shift worker's story would feel fake.

**I7 note:** No partner angle at all. This is also defensible — the persona is a parent buying for a teenage son in a dorm. There's no romantic partner in this scenario. The roommate serves the same functional role (someone else who isn't disturbed).

**Verdict:** All heavy sleeper scripts (V1, V3, V4) include the partner beat as required. I5 and I7 have reasonable creative justifications for the absence of a romantic partner. No fix needed.

## Issue 9: Mechanism Claims vs. Sleep Science Research

**The problem:** The Sleep Science Research doc lists specific "Claims to Avoid" that are oversimplified or unsupported. Scripts must not make these claims.

**Claims to Avoid (from the doc):**
1. "Your brain can't habituate to vibration." (FALSE)
2. "Vibration bypasses the thalamus." (FALSE)
3. "Sound alarms don't work for heavy sleepers." (Too absolute)
4. "ADHD causes you to sleep through alarms." (Oversimplified)
5. "Vibration always wakes you up." (No 100% success)
6. "Louder alarms don't help." (Oversimplified)

**Checking every script:**

**V2 Body 0:04-0:12:** "your brain produces something called sleep spindles. They block sound before it can wake you." This is a correct, defensible claim per the research doc. Clean.

**V2 Body 0:19-0:27:** "it reaches your brain through a completely different pathway. One your brain hasn't learned to block." The second sentence is close to "can't habituate" (Claim #1 to avoid). "Hasn't learned to block" implies the brain CAN'T learn to block it, which is the oversimplified version. The accurate claim is that vibration uses a different pathway and, when varied in pattern, is harder to ignore. This line needs softening.

**V3 Body 0:28-0:36:** "so your brain can't learn to ignore it the way it does with sound." Same issue as V2. "Can't learn to ignore it" is the oversimplified habituation claim. The brain CAN habituate to sustained vibration. The accurate version: FitSleeps uses an escalating pattern that makes it harder to sleep through.

**V4 Body 0:07-0:14:** "so your brain can't ignore it like a phone alarm." Same pattern. "Can't ignore it" oversimplifies.

**I5:** "Sound travels through air and your brain learns to filter it... Vibration on the wrist uses a different pathway. Direct skin contact. Your nervous system can't ignore it the way your ears can tune out sound." "Can't ignore it" — same issue, but the comparative phrasing ("the way your ears can tune out sound") is closer to defensible. Still slightly oversimplified.

**I6:** "The vibration doesn't travel through the mattress because it's not a smartwatch motor tapping against a hard surface. It's pressed directly against skin." This is a physical claim about vibration transfer, not a habituation claim. Defensible.

**I7:** "Sound-based alarms were never going to work for Tyler." This is close to Claim #3 to avoid ("Sound alarms don't work for heavy sleepers" — too absolute). However, it's framed as personal ("for Tyler") rather than universal, and it's preceded by a detailed explanation of ADHD-specific neurological factors. Defensible in context.

**I7:** "His brain couldn't gate it out the way it gates out sound." Comparative framing. Better than "can't ignore it" but still implies an absolute.

**I7:** "ADHD doesn't just make it hard to focus. It rewires how the brain handles sleep." Accurate framing per the research doc.

**Total count:** 4 instances of the "brain can't ignore/block vibration" claim (V2, V3, V4, I5). All four use variations of the oversimplified habituation claim that the Sleep Science Research doc explicitly flags as false.

**Fix:** Replace "can't" language with comparative or escalation language:
- V2: "One your brain hasn't adapted to yet" or "One that's much harder to sleep through."
- V3: "so it's much harder for your brain to tune out" instead of "can't learn to ignore."
- V4: "so your brain can't just tune it out the way it does a phone alarm" — actually, this comparative version is borderline acceptable. Consider "so it's harder for your brain to filter out than a phone alarm."
- I5: "Your nervous system responds to it differently than your ears respond to sound" instead of "can't ignore."

## Issue 10: VO Naturalness

**The problem:** VO lines should sound spoken, not written. Any line that sounds like copy rather than conversation needs rewriting.

**Lines that sound written rather than spoken:**

**V2 Body 0:12-0:19:** "Same problem, different packaging." This is a tagline, not something a person says in conversation. It's punchy as copy but would sound rehearsed spoken aloud. A more natural version: "They're all just different versions of the same thing your brain already knows how to ignore."

**V2 Close:** "She's going to notice the difference before you do." This is a clever copywriter line. A real person would say something like "Honestly, she'll notice before you do."

**V3 Close:** "One band. One purpose. And it actually works." This is advertising copy. Three-word fragments followed by a tagline. A real person would say "It's just one thing and it actually works" or similar. This is the assigned close type (ease/simplicity) so the structure is intentional, but it needs to feel less polished.

**V4 Body 0:07-0:14:** "so your brain can't ignore it like a phone alarm." This is fine for narrated VO (V4 is narrated, not testimonial), where a slightly more produced tone is acceptable.

**I5 closing paragraph:** "If you work shifts and you sleep through alarms, it is not a character flaw. Your brain filters sound. That's what it's built to do. But your wrist doesn't have that filter." The last sentence — "But your wrist doesn't have that filter" — is excellent copy but slightly too tidy for a nurse writing her own story. The rest of I5 is remarkably natural, so this stands out.

**I7:** "That's not a sales pitch. That's the neuroscience of why my son missed fourteen classes." This line explicitly calls out the sales pitch concern, which is a meta-awareness that breaks the fourth wall of the story format. A parent wouldn't say "that's not a sales pitch" in an organic story. This feels like the writer anticipating objections rather than the character speaking.

**Total count:** 6 lines flagged. Most are minor. The V3 close and I7 "not a sales pitch" line are the most noticeable.

**Fix:**
- V2 0:12-0:19: Replace "Same problem, different packaging" with a natural observation.
- V2 Close: "She's going to notice the difference before you do" can stay — it's the last line and the slight polish works as a closing beat.
- V3 Close: "One band. One purpose. And it actually works." — consider "Honestly? One band. And it just works." to make it feel less composed.
- I7: Cut "That's not a sales pitch." The sentence that follows ("That's the neuroscience of why my son missed fourteen classes.") is powerful enough on its own.
