## Round 2 Review Plan (2026-04-14)

_After reviewing the compliance-rewritten batch, Marce flagged several places where the rewrite was too strict on compliance, losing punchiness unnecessarily. Plan below reconciles compliance requirements with edge preservation._

### Compliance reconsideration

**What's actually banned vs what I over-policed:**

Truly banned (keep these rules):
- Drug names (Ozempic/Wegovy/Mounjaro/Zepbound) outside disclaimer
- "GLP-1" as product name
- Specific weight numbers, percentages, timeframes as results claims
- "Same active ingredient as [brand]"
- "Safe," "effective," "well-tolerated," "zero side effects," "clinically proven"
- Florida Rule: rapid/dramatic/effortless/magical weight loss claims
- Drug imagery (vials, syringes, packaging)

NOT compliance violations (my previous edits were overreach):
- Strong commentary on dieting psychology ("why willpower always fails" — it's about dieting, not product efficacy)
- Feature lists in subheadings (just factual descriptions of what's included)
- Sharp competitive framing against med spas
- Narrative emotional language that doesn't quantify outcomes
- Absolute claims ABOUT dieting patterns (different from absolute claims about results)

### Cartoon "swirling" clarification

When the image direction says "food icons swirling around the head," the reference is: Looney Tunes / classic cartoons where birds or stars spin in a ring around a character's head after they get hit or are dizzy. Food icons orbit in a ring AROUND the head, not inside the head. Represents the external mental pressure of constant food thoughts. Alternative phrasing for designer brief: "food icons orbiting in a ring around her head, like cartoon thought-stress symbols."

### Editorial disclaimer question (Marce's call)

Compliance docs require the brand-ownership disclaimer on any ad discussing weight loss medications. Editorial format still sells a medication-based program, so it likely still needs the disclaimer. **But:** prominent legal copy breaks the editorial illusion.

**Recommendation:** Keep disclaimer required on editorial briefs (#7, #8, #9) but specify in Designer Note that it should be treated MORE discreetly than on other formats — smaller font, lower contrast, very bottom edge. Preserves editorial feel while maintaining compliance. Removing entirely = real compliance risk. **Awaiting Marce's call.**
