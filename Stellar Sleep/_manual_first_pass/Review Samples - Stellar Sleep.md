> **Purpose:** Raw material for ad copywriters -- verbatim reviews organized by persona/use-case and sentiment. All quotes are exact wording from reviewers. Pull directly from these for ad scripts, hooks, and social proof.

---

## How to Use This Document

1. **For ad hooks/headlines:** Scan the category that matches your target persona. The strongest lines name a specific duration of suffering AND a specific outcome or transformation.
2. **For objection handling:** The Negative Reviews section contains the real failure modes and structural objections. Understanding them lets you preempt in copy -- especially SRT disclosure and maintenance insomnia gaps.
3. **For story-driven ads:** Look for reviews with a before/after arc -- reviewer describes months or years of insomnia, then describes improvement week by week. These are your narrative ad scripts.
4. **Note on coverage:** Review volume is limited. Stellar Sleep has very few formal reviews relative to its user base. App Store ratings (2,700) significantly outnumber extracted reviews (4 App Store, 1 Google Play). Treat all quotes with that context -- they are directionally useful but not statistically representative.

## 7. Negative Reviews

### Critical Objections

---

> After three weeks, I experienced decreased sleep. The app focuses on those with trouble falling asleep -- only brief mention of issues waking up in the middle of the night, making it ineffective for that problem. Sleep restriction therapy was absolutely brutal and not adequately disclosed before subscription charges applied.

**Muttly14, 1 star, Apple App Store, Jul 2024**
*What this tells us: Two distinct objections. (1) Product-fit gap: sleep onset insomnia vs. maintenance insomnia (waking in the middle of the night). If Stellar Sleep's program is primarily designed for sleep onset, it is failing users whose core problem is waking at 2am. (2) SRT disclosure: sleep restriction therapy is legitimately grueling -- you are temporarily sleep-deprived to reset the sleep drive. If users hit this wall before understanding why it's happening, they quit and leave 1-star reviews. This is the most important negative review in the dataset. Both issues need to be addressed in onboarding, landing copy, and ad targeting.*

---

### Structural Criticisms (Qualitative / HN Thread)

> After paying I had to fill out a nearly identical questionnaire...the pricing was buried inside a collapsed FAQ. It felt like sunk-cost manipulation rather than transparent value proposition.

**tomcam, HN (paraphrased)**
*What this tells us: Onboarding UX and pricing transparency are objections at the consideration stage. Prospects who feel manipulated before subscribing will not convert -- or will reverse the charge immediately. If running paid acquisition, any friction in the pre-subscription flow bleeds CAC.*

---

> CBT-I has notoriously low adherence rates. Does sleep restriction simply coincide with natural 5-week insomnia resolution cycles?

**pedalpete, HN (paraphrased -- sleep tech insider)**
*What this tells us: Scientifically literate objectors will question whether results are from CBT-I or natural insomnia resolution. This is a real scientific debate. Ad copy cannot resolve it, but can lean into the trial + Harvard credentialing as credibility signals. Do not overclaim "CBT-I causes this" -- frame as "evidence-based program used by sleep therapists."*

---

> It's the app version of snake oil for positioning routine sleep hygiene advice as medical treatment.

**ThinkBeat, HN (paraphrased)**
*What this tells us: Brand positioning risk. If the program is perceived as dressing up sleep hygiene (no screens before bed, consistent schedule) as CBT-I, educated users will reject it. The Harvard collaborator relationship and the specific mechanism of SRT/sleep restriction are the best defenses against this objection. Make sure CBT-I is named specifically, not just implied.*

---

> The price is too high for the product it delivers.

**Knoji review (paraphrased -- exact text not accessible, 3.9/5 overall)**
*What this tells us: Price sensitivity is a real objection. At $63/month (or similar), this is one of the most expensive CBT-I apps on the market. Value justification must be explicit -- either through outcomes ("what is a full night of sleep worth to you") or through comparison to professional sleep therapy ($200+/session).*
