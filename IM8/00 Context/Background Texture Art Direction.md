# Background Texture Art Direction — IM8

## North Star: "The World Inside the Powder"

What you'd see if you could look inside IM8's powder at the biological world its ingredients come from — but the photographs were shot by a fashion photographer. The textures come from the same biological world as the product's ingredients and mechanism of action, viewed at tissue level or as liquid extracts.

This is NOT "beautiful nature photography." It's **scientific subjects, editorial eye.** Every texture must pass one test: could this image appear in a scientific paper about one of IM8's ingredient categories? If yes — and it's shot with luxury editorial quality — it belongs. If it's just beautiful but unconnected to the science (honey, jellyfish, dahlias), it fails.

IM8's palette names encode the principle: "molecular" + "organic." These textures are both simultaneously — real organic subjects that reveal molecular-level structure.

---

## The Relevance Test

Before generating any background texture, it must pass:

1. **Ingredient-family connection** — Does this organism/subject belong to the same biological family as an IM8 ingredient? (Fungi → ashwagandha/adaptogens. Coral → amino complex. Botanical tissue → saffron/plant extracts. Cell structures → CoQ10/B12 cellular mechanism. Extracts → the dissolved ingredient itself.)
2. **Tissue-level view OR extract specimen** — Is this a close-up of biological STRUCTURE (gills, membranes, tissue, vascular networks), or a liquid extract of an actual ingredient? Not a whole organism or decorative object.
3. **Science journal test** — Could this image appear in a biology research paper or pharmaceutical specimen catalog? Not a food magazine, not a nature calendar, not a home decor site.
4. **Brand palette fit** — Does the natural color of this subject work within IM8's approved spectrum? Warm (coral, burgundy, crimson, amber, blush, golden yellow), cool (cellular blue, cobalt, violet), or scientific (green cellular, emerald, sage).

All four must pass. If even one fails, the subject is off-topic.

### What Fails the Test
- **Honey** — food photography, not science. No ingredient connection.
- **Jellyfish** — whole animal, not tissue-level. Cool/decorative.
- **Dahlia petals** — just a flower. No connection to IM8's ingredient world.
- **Turkey tail** — right family but image reads as "forest floor," not biological structure.
- **Leaf veins** — interesting structure but too "green/nature," no specific ingredient tie.
- **Generic water/pool** — no biological substance.
- **CGI/3D renders** — neural networks, crystals, digital particles.
- **Flat-lay extract droplets** — passes the ingredient test but has no structural interest. Just colored blobs that all look identical regardless of ingredient. No visual differentiation.
- **"Interesting but not pretty"** — a subject can pass all 4 relevance tests and still fail if it's not visually beautiful. Lichen, spirulina chains, dried flower heads, and dark moody forest compositions passed the science test but weren't aesthetically sophisticated enough. This is editorial beauty photography — it must be gorgeous.

### What Passes
- **Mushroom gills** (any species in the adaptogen/medicinal family) — tissue-level view of IM8's ingredient kingdom
- **Coral tissue** — structural biology of marine amino compounds
- **Cell/bubble** — cellular membrane, the literal scale where IM8 works
- **Saffron threads** — an actual IM8 ingredient at macro scale
- **Botanical vascular tissue** — plant-derived ingredient structure (not whole flowers)
- **Sea anemone tissue** — marine biology, amino/mineral structure
- **Microscopy** (collagen fibers, nerve tissue, B12 crystals) — polarized/fluorescent views of structures IM8 affects
- **Plant cells** (chloroplasts, stomata, diatoms) — cellular biology in the green/scientific spectrum
- **Ingredient extracts** (liquid droplets on matte surface) — the dissolved form of IM8's actual ingredients
- **Subtle/airy compositions** — same subjects but with negative space, shallow DOF, bokeh (not just dense textures)

## Art Direction Rules

### Scale
Always macro or extreme close-up. Tissue-level. The viewer sees biological structure they wouldn't notice at normal scale.

### Subject
Tissue, structure, organisms, and extracts from IM8's ingredient families. Never whole animals, food styling, decorative nature, or inorganic subjects.

### Structure
Natural biological patterns must be visible — gill ridges, coral branches, cell membranes, vascular networks, tissue folds, crystalline formations, droplet surface tension. Structure = the body's systems working in order.

### Lighting
Soft, natural, editorial. Never flash, never harsh. Golden hour backlight, diffused natural light, ambient underwater light, or even studio product lighting (for extract/specimen compositions).

### Color Temperature
Full ingredient-natural spectrum: warm (coral, salmon, burgundy, amber, crimson, blush, golden yellow, orange), cool (cellular blue, cobalt, violet, indigo), scientific (emerald, sage green, amber-green). The color must be the natural color of the subject — never artificially tinted.

### Photography Quality
Must read as real editorial macro photography — shot on medium format (Hasselblad, Phase One) with macro lens. Never CGI, 3D renders, or digitally constructed imagery. The test: does this look like it was photographed, or generated?

### Composition Styles
Three approved composition approaches:

1. **Dense / Textured** — Subject fills the entire frame. Wall-to-wall biological structure. Maximum visual impact. Works as rich, immersive backgrounds.
2. **Subtle / Airy** — Subject occupies 30-40% of the frame, positioned to one side. Shallow DOF dissolves the rest into creamy bokeh or smooth gradient. Generous negative space for text overlay. Dreamy, ethereal.
3. **Extract / Specimen** — Flat-lay overhead of ingredient liquid droplets on a matte neutral surface. One large organic-shaped pool + smaller satellites. Shimmer particles visible in the liquid. Product-photography quality. The color of each extract is the natural color of the ingredient in solution.

## Animation Plan

Each approved background can be animated for use in video end cards. Three tiers based on subject type. The universal rule: **restraint over spectacle.** These are background textures — the animation supports the end card content, never competes with it. If it looks like a motion graphics demo reel, it's wrong. If it looks like a nature documentary shot by a fashion brand, it's right.

### Animation Principles
- **Motion from frame 1.** No static openings. The subject must be visibly moving from the very first frame. The first 5 seconds are the most important — that's what the viewer sees behind the end card.
- **Real physics only.** Water moves like water. Fungi sway like fungi. No bounce, no elastic snap, no particle bursts.
- **One axis of motion per composition.** Pan OR push OR rotate. Never combine. The eye should track one thing.
- **No easing curves that feel digital.** Organic deceleration — things slow down because of friction/resistance, not because of a bezier curve. Linear or very gentle ease-out.
- **Light shifts > object movement.** When in doubt, move the light, not the subject. A caustic ripple across coral is more elegant than the coral moving.
- **Depth of field is your friend.** Rack focus between planes is inherently cinematic and subtle.
- **Don't ask AI models for complex biological events.** Cell division, neural firing, or other multi-stage processes produce unrealistic results. Instead: use ambient motion the subject would have (floating, pulsing, drifting) rather than narrative events.
- **Front-load the prompt.** Describe what is ALREADY HAPPENING, not what should start happening. "A cell is floating and gently rotating" not "A cell begins to rotate."

### Prompting Lessons (Kling v3 Pro)
- Saying "extremely subtle" or "imperceptible" produces static-looking video. Use "visible," "continuous," "active."
- Describing a process that "completes" (like division) causes the model to delay the event and rush it at the end. Instead describe continuous ambient motion.
- 15 seconds at $1.68/video is the standard duration and budget.
- Morel-surface "dolly push" and turmeric-cross "rotation with light" worked perfectly — direct camera motions are Kling's strength.
- Clear-bubble "rotation with refraction shifts" also strong — describe the light effect, not just the object motion.
