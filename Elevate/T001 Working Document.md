## Raw Notes

We need to develop a batch of 15 statics. We can do a maximum of three times the same type of visual format but no more than that. 

### Strategic Foundation: WeightRx Playbook (Direct GLP-1 Competitor)

WeightRx (T001–T004) provides the winning framework for this category. Elevate will adopt these direct-response GLP-1 patterns as our strategic foundation.

### 1. The "Food Noise" Breakthrough
- **The Insight:** "Food noise" (the mental obsession with food) is the #1 pain point for first-timers. 
- **The Execution:** Use a two-door approach:
    - **Door A (The Problem):** Naming the "deafening noise" and normalizing it as a biological error, not a character flaw.
    - **Door B (The Revelation):** Describing the silence/shock of "is this how normal people feel?" when it stops.
- **For Elevate:** This should be the core of our "Trigger" stage ads.

### 2. The Willpower Reframe (Shame vs. Relief)
- **The Insight:** Most buyers have 10+ years of self-blame.
- **The Execution:** Stop trying to "motivate" them. Instead, validate that willpower was never the issue—biology was. 
- **The Angle:** "You're not weak. Your brain is sending the wrong signals. Semaglutide fixes the signal."

### 3. Micro-Moments > Broad Claims
- **The Insight:** Broad weight loss claims ("lose 40 lbs") are saturated and often trigger skepticism. Specific, lived-experience "micro-moments" bypass this by naming a private reality the audience hasn't heard a brand acknowledge before.
- **The Execution:** Focus on "Quiet Humiliation" — the private moments of physical friction in public spaces.
    - **The Seatbelt Click:** The anxiety of the "click" not happening on a plane or rollercoaster. The moment of "worst day" turning into the "best day" (the day they finally did something different).
    - **The Chair with Arms:** Dreading the dentist or a restaurant because the chair might be too tight. It's about present-tense, physical discomfort.
    - **The Shoe-Tying Stool:** The loss of basic mobility (needing a stool to reach your feet) and the triumph of the first time you don't need it.
- **Elevate Adaptation:** We will frame these as "Origin Stories of Transformation," not just shame. We name the "rock bottom" moment to bridge into the GLP-1 solution.

### 4. Format Winner: Creative + Reddit VoC
- **What worked:** NOT a native Reddit mimic (no upvotes/downvotes/usernames). It's a premium creative that incorporates a VoC quote styled as a social post element within a larger designed ad — like WeightRx AD368: dark background, big headline ("TURN OFF 30 YEARS OF FOOD OBSESSION"), a quote block that looks like it could be from Reddit, product shot, price, CTA. The quote is the scroll-stopper, the design around it does the selling.
- **Why:** It bypasses "ad blindness" by leading with a peer voice, but the surrounding creative does the brand/price work that a pure social screenshot can't.

Some examples of good ads of theirs: '/Users/marce/Downloads/00 WeightRX'

They have creative imagery and good messaging that we can learn from

## Decisions & Strategic Split

### 1. Product Distribution (Internal Targeting Only)

Per compliance, pricing and product are decoupled from the drug in the ad itself — every concept uses "Programs from $58/week" and the product (semaglutide vs tirzepatide) is determined by the licensed provider during medical review. The split below is **internal audience routing**, not on-ad messaging.

- **Semaglutide-leaning (7 concepts):** #1, 2, 6, 7, 9, 11, 12. First-timer and broad-reach angles — food noise, willpower reframe, insurance denial, postpartum, grounded "not a miracle drug" testimonial, no-insurance access, food noise in the widget.
- **Tirzepatide-leaning (7 concepts):** #3, 5, 8, 10, 13, 14, 15. Heavier-individual / longer-journey / premium-positioning angles — seatbelt micro-moment, vs. other providers, "money well spent," VoC experience headlines, shame-moment widget, city poster access, city poster experience VoC.
- **Universal (1 concept):** #4 "What's Included In Your Care Plan" — educational, applies to any care plan.

### 2. Messaging Hierarchy
- **Primary Hook:** VoC-based experience moments — food noise (#1, 10, 12), willpower reframe / biology (#2, 10), physical micro-moments (#3, 13), postpartum struggle (#7), "money well spent" (#8), "not a miracle drug" (#9).
- **Primary Mechanism:** "Programs from $58/week / $0 down / payment plans." Every ad carries Elevate's affordability and accessibility lever — payment plan logos (Affirm, Afterpay, Klarna, HSA/FSA) are a visual element, not fine print.
- **Secondary Proof:** Free bloodwork (included, not extra), 35,000+ patients, 4.6 Trustpilot, no insurance needed, ships in 7-10 days after approval. These counter the "med spa / insurance / fragmented care" objections.

### 3. Identity Framing (The Three Selves)
- **Actual Self (6):** Naming current friction in present tense — #1 Food Noise, #2 Willpower Reframe, #3 Seatbelt Click, #9 Not a Miracle, #12 Food Noise Widget, #13 Micro-moment Widget.
- **Ideal Self (4):** Experience-based transformation (NOT quantified results) — #7 Getting My Life Back, #8 Money Well Spent, #10 VoC experience headlines on Frosted Glass, #15 Experience VoC City Poster.
- **Ought Self (5):** Access, fairness, "you deserve" framing — #4 What's Included, #5 vs. Other Providers, #6 vs. Insurance, #11 No Insurance + Access Proof, #14 Stop Overpaying Your Med Spa.

## Script Criteria

### COMPLIANCE (Read First)

**Before writing anything for Elevate, read [[Compliance - Marketing Rules]].** Every brief, headline, primary text, and image direction must pass the compliance checklist. Compliance rules override every other criteria doc. Non-compliance is an automatic fail.

Summary of the hardest constraints:
- **No "GLP-1" as a product name.** Not in headlines, primary text, card copy, or CTAs. Can appear in biological/educational context only.
- **No brand names** (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound, Saxenda, Victoza) anywhere except the mandatory brand-ownership disclaimer.
- **No "same active ingredient as [brand]"** — FDA has warned companies for this exact phrasing.
- **No specific weight numbers, percentages, or timeframes** as results claims. No pounds lost, no "in 6 months," no "15% body weight."
- **No drug imagery.** No vials, syringes, pens, medication packaging. No before/after photos.
- **No "safe," "effective," "well-tolerated," "zero side effects," "clinically proven."**
- **Pricing decoupled from drug.** "Programs starting at $58/week" — never "GLP-1 for $58/week."
- **CTAs lead to medical review.** "See if you qualify" / "Get started" / "Check eligibility" / "Begin your medical review" / "Explore care options."
- **Testimonials describe experience, not results.** "I finally felt supported" ✓. "I lost 50 lbs" ✗.
- **Mandatory brand-ownership disclaimer** lives in the IMAGE fine print (small legal type at the bottom of the design), NOT in the primary text on the platform. Same placement as existing top spenders — designers add this. See [[Compliance - Marketing Rules]] §4 for the exact disclaimer text.

### Elevate-Specific Rules

- **First-party source of truth.** Every factual claim (pricing, shipping timelines, what's included, star ratings, patient counts) must be verified against Elevate's website — never copy facts from existing ads. Existing ads may contain stale or incorrect data. The canonical source is the **Verified First-Party Facts** section at the top of [[Product Context - GLP-1 Programs]], which captures pricing, the $58/week derivation, 7-10-day shipping, program inclusions, 35,000+ patient count, and ratings with source attribution. For anything not already captured there, re-run `scripts/scrape_elevate.py` against the live site.
- **Payment plan logos are a design element, not just information.** Affirm, Afterpay, Klarna, HSA/FSA logos should be visually prominent (green pill badges, tear-off tabs, or frosted glass cards) — not tucked into fine print. Payment logos are OVERLAYS — never on sticky notes. Sticky notes carry the price only.
- **Price framing.** "Programs from $58/week" is the default (verified floor: semaglutide 12-week bundle $699/12wk = $58/wk). Pricing language must not tie directly to a drug.
- **Shipping framing.** "Ships in 7-10 days after approval" is the verified timeline. Do NOT use "3-7 days" (that number came from existing ads and doesn't match the website). Where the 7-10 number isn't load-bearing for the concept, leave shipping out entirely — it's not a strong hook.
- **Real-life imagery only.** Women in gyms, bedrooms, bathrooms, kitchens. Mirror selfies, casual outfits. Lifestyle imagery (walking, movement, confidence). Never fitness models, never aspirational travel imagery, never before/after. The viewer should see themselves, not an aspiration they can't relate to.
- **VoC quotes must come from Elevate Trustpilot reviews or WeightRx research sprints** AND must describe experience, not results. Don't invent quotes — source from real material. Paraphrasing for clarity or brevity is acceptable when it reads better than verbatim. Attribute when using Elevate reviews (first name only). Never use VoC with weight numbers, percentages, or timeframes.
- **Elevate Health logo:** Present on most formats — tiny, bottom-left, doesn't count toward the composition element limit. First thing to drop if space is tight. **Exception: VoC Editorial format gets NO logo AND no mandatory brand-ownership disclaimer.** The editorial format works because it looks like content, not an ad. A brand logo or legal-type disclaimer breaks that illusion. Every other format carries the disclaimer in-image.
- **Tonal range:** Empathetic, grounded, validating. The audience skews female. Avoid humorous tone unless it comes directly from a VoC quote. Provocative is OK. Confrontational toward competitors (med spas, insurance companies) is OK. Absurdist or meme-based humor is not.
- **Compliance is a rulebook, not a vibe check.** If a headline doesn't break a specific rule in [[Compliance - Marketing Rules]], keep its edge. Don't pre-emptively soften punchy lines because they "feel" aggressive. Over-policing kills conversion. The test is literal: does this line name a drug, claim a weight number, promise a timeframe, claim safety/efficacy, or violate the Florida rule list? If no, it ships.

### Headline / Subheading Rules

- **One idea per headline.** If a headline contains a scroll-stopper AND supporting details (features, price, social proof), split them. The headline is the punch — one clear idea that makes someone stop scrolling. Everything else goes in the subheading.
- **Subheadings are optional.** A subheading is there to add information the headline can't carry on its own. If the hero element (chart, card, diagram, info block) already shows what the subheading would say, DROP the subheading. In-static redundancy (headline → subheading → chart all saying the same thing) makes the image feel AI-assembled. A strong headline can stand alone.
- **No "Elevate." prefix in subheadings.** The brand name already appears in the account username and logo on-platform — repeating it in the subheading is dead weight. Subheadings should carry mechanism, differentiator, or price only. (This overrides the "Elevate. [detail]." pattern in [[Creative Image Ad Criteria]] for this batch.) **Applies to flyer subs on the Grimy / City Poster format too,** since the Elevate logo is already on the flyer itself — don't double up.
- **Subheading format when used:** Mechanism or differentiator. Under 10 words. No brand prefix. No repetition of chart/card content.
- **Subheading treatment varies by layout:**
  - Creative Image / Comparison Table / Premium Frosted Glass: standard headline + short subheading below (drop if redundant with the hero)
  - Grimy / City Poster: headline on the flyer + smaller supporting text below on the flyer
  - VoC Editorial: the editorial headline IS the VoC element — subheading functions more like a byline or tagline
  - Instagram Stories Widget: headline-with-background (mimics native IG Stories caption-with-background text) + info card below — no separate subheading, the card structure carries the details
- **Instagram Stories Widget headlines must be first-person typographic.** The widget mimics the native IG Stories caption-with-background text that users apply over their own stories — a UI element users associate with first-person self-narration. Headlines must match that register: "I stopped flying because of the seatbelt," "I can eat dinner in peace," "I haven't looked in a full-body mirror in 3 years." Third-person headlines ("She did X," "Her doctor asked") break the native-feel illusion and make the ad read as a caption pretending to be one. This rule applies to the widget format only — other formats can use third person freely.
- **VoC Editorial headlines must be real review quotes, not constructed.** The format reads as a magazine pull-quote from an actual customer. If the quote is fabricated, viewers clock the difference. Pull headlines verbatim (or lightly tightened for punctuation / length) from `Elevate/00 Context/Review Samples - Elevate.md`. Subheading functions as the byline ("A member, one year into her medical weight loss journey.") — not as sales copy. Same applies to any future "fake testimonial" layout (Tweet, Reddit post, user-comment). If the format mimics a real user saying it, the words have to come from a real user saying it.
- **Ad-unit congruence.** Headline + hero image + VoC quote block (if present) + subheading must all tell the same story. Read them as one unit before shipping. If the elements jump between unrelated micro-moments (e.g., seatbelt headline + airplane hero + stool VoC quote), rewrite the weakest elements to conform to the strongest — usually the hero image, since it's the scroll-stop. Particular risk on Creative + Social VoC, where the quote block was often written before the hero was finalized.
- **Headline word count: 8-12 max, target ~8.** Exception: punchline structures where the rhythm justifies extra words (e.g., three-beat story structures — short clause + short clause + short clause — where the rhythm is the scroll-stop).
- **Primary text can repeat image content; static image cannot repeat itself.** Many viewers don't read the image — the primary text has to work as a standalone unit. So repetition between primary text and image is FINE. But inside the image, every element must add new information. That's the redundancy line.
- **Category clarity test.** Every ad must answer the question "what is being sold?" through the headline + sub + hero combo — before the viewer reads a single line of primary text. For branded formats (Frosted Glass, IG Stories Widget, Comparison Table, City Poster) where the card/chart/logo can carry the category, the headline can be abstract. For VoC Editorial (no logo, no card, no branded anchor), the subheading MUST name "medical weight loss" or an unmistakable weight-loss signal ("the scale," "diets," etc.). Before ship: imagine the viewer sees only the image, no caption. Can they answer "what is this ad selling"? If no, fix it.
- **Category signal on cards is a structural solve.** When a format uses an info card or a frosted card (Frosted Glass, IG Stories Widget), put "Medical weight loss" as the top line of the card (smaller weight, above the dominant number). This way every headline variation ships with category clarity intact, regardless of how abstract the headline itself gets.

### Composition Density System

**Element count: 6 max** (excluding logo and disclaimer).

**Weight budget: 12 points max.** Over 10 = review for reductions (shorten headline, reduce chart rows, shrink an image, or drop an element). Never sacrifice copy clarity or message coherence just to hit a number — the weight system is a guardrail, not a mandate.

| Weight | Points | What qualifies | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heavy | 3 | Takes 30%+ of canvas. Dense info or large visual. | Comparison chart (4+ rows), full photo (person/scene), creative hero image, Reddit VoC quote block |
| Medium | 2 | Takes 10-30% of canvas. Clear visual unit, not dominant. | Headline (8-12 words), info card (3 lines), payment logo cluster (3-4 badges), sticky note, lifestyle accent image |
| Light | 1 | Takes <10%. Glanceable in under a second. | Short headline (under 6 words), CTA button, price text line, small icon, subheading |

**Reduction options when over budget** (in order of preference):
1. Shrink a Heavy element to Medium (e.g., reduce chart from 5 rows to 3, or make a hero image smaller)
2. Shorten headline word count (drops from Medium to Light)
3. Reduce payment logos from 6 to 3-4
4. Drop the least essential element entirely

**Two-heavy rule:** A composition can carry at most ONE read-heavy hero (comparison chart, cycle diagram, dense info card). Pairing two read-heavy elements — e.g., a cycle diagram AND a Reddit-style VoC quote block — overloads the image. Viewers don't know where to look. Only single-glance heroes (photo, hero image, headline-dominant card) can safely pair with a VoC quote block.

**Weight points are internal strategist shorthand.** Never include element counts or weight totals in designer notes, brief references, or anything that ships outside the strategy team. Designers don't use this system and the numbers look like gibberish to them. Keep weight math inside the Batch Plan and Script Criteria only.

**After:** Rewrote #3, #8, #9, #13 (A and C only — B kept), and #15 so each variation carries at least one of: category ("medical weight loss," "care plan"), specific mechanism ("food noise," "diet cycle," "dieting"), or price anchor ("$58/week," "$0 down"). The evocative micro-moment is preserved where it fits inside 8-12 words next to the anchor. Where it doesn't fit, the moment is dropped in favor of the anchor.

**Rule:** The headline is the FIRST thing read and often the ONLY thing read — but whether it must self-anchor depends on the format. **Image-forward formats** (Creative + Social VoC with a strong hero like #3's seatbelt shot, VoC Editorial with real-person photography, Comparison Table with a dominant chart, Cycle Diagram) let the image carry the category; the headline can focus on an angle or moment. **Image-light formats** (IG Stories Widget — image sits behind the widget/card; Grimy / City Poster — typographic-only flyer; Premium Frosted Glass card-dominant) require the headline to independently signal at least one of: **category** (medical weight loss / care plan / med spa), **specific mechanism** (food noise, diet cycle, dieting), or **price anchor** ($58/week, $0 down). Evocative micro-moments (seatbelt, mirror, stool, "money well spent," "not a miracle") can pair with an anchor on image-light formats — they cannot substitute for one. Before ship on an image-light format: imagine the hero reduced to a blur at thumbnail. Does the headline alone sell the category? If no, rewrite.

See [[Headline & Text Hook Criteria]] → Headline-Specific Copy Rules → "First-read anchor test" and Recurring Mistake #13 ("Orphan evocative micro-moments on image-light formats").

### VoC Editorial Headlines Must Be Real Review Quotes

**Before:** First drafts of Ads #8 and #9 had constructed editorial headlines that SOUNDED like review quotes — "Money Well Spent. Worth Every Penny." / "Not Magic. Just Care That Works With You." — but weren't pulled from actual reviews. The format mimics a magazine pull-quote from a real customer. If the quote is fabricated, the format dies the moment the viewer clocks the difference.

**After:** Lifted headlines verbatim (or lightly tightened for punctuation) from `Elevate/00 Context/Review Samples - Elevate.md`:
- #8A `"Very Pleased With This Process. Money Well Spent."` — Sonia, Nov 2024
- #8B `"Worth It. I Owed This To Myself."` — composite from Janay + Vianey
- #9A `"Not A Miracle Drug. You Have To Put The Work In."` — Elaine, Nov 2024
- #9B `"A Forever Lifestyle Change."` — Elaine, Nov 2024
- #9C `"Worth It. But It's Hard Work."` — Vianey, Nov 2024

Subheadings function as the byline ("A member, one year into her medical weight loss journey.") — not as sales copy.

**Rule:** For any "fake testimonial" format (VoC Editorial, Tweet, Reddit, user-comment, IG comment), the quote has to come from a real review. Pull it from the brand's Review Samples doc. If you can't point to a source review, the format is broken. Paraphrasing for length or compliance is OK — constructing from scratch is not. See [[Headline & Text Hook Criteria]] → Recurring Mistake #14 and "Real-quote test" Quality Check.

### Ad-Unit Congruence

**Before:** Ad #3 (Seatbelt Click) had three incongruent moments stapled together — headline about a seatbelt, hero image of an airplane seat, VoC quote block about a stool-and-shoe-tying moment. Each element was strong in isolation. Read together they felt like someone had assembled a Creative + Social VoC template with whatever VoC block was nearest to hand.

**After:** Rewrote the VoC quote block to be airplane-congruent: "I stopped booking flights years ago — the seatbelt conversation was the worst part. Last time I flew, I didn't ask for the extender. Just clicked it and took off." (u/noextenderneeded, 4.7k upvotes). Rewrote headline option C to also reference the airplane. All three elements now name the same micro-moment.

**Rule:** Before shipping any static ad with 3+ narrative surfaces (headline, hero, VoC quote, subheading, card copy), read them all as one sentence. If they jump between unrelated moments, rewrite the weakest elements to conform to the strongest. The hero image is usually the scroll-stop and the right anchor. Most common failure mode: Creative + Social VoC templates where the VoC quote block was written before the hero image was finalized. When hero changes, the VoC quote and headline have to follow. See [[Creative Image Ad Criteria]] → Ad-Unit Congruence.

### Widget Headlines Must Be First-Person

**Before:** Rewriting #13 surfaced that calling the widget element a "bubble" (it sounds like a thought bubble) obscured what the format actually mimics: the native Instagram Stories caption-with-background text — the one users tap on when writing over their own stories. That UI element is a first-person register in the wild. But the widget briefs mixed first-person ("I stopped flying...") and third-person ("She did X," "Her doctor asked") freely, which breaks the native-feel illusion.

**After:** Rebranded "bubble" → "headline with background" across #12 and #13, with a designer note explaining the reference is IG Stories' native caption-with-background style. Added a rule to Script Criteria: widget headlines must be first-person typographic.

**Rule:** Any time the format mimics a native platform UI element (Stories caption, comment, DM, tweet, Reddit post), match the voice register people actually use on that surface. IG Stories caption-with-background = first person ("I...", "my..."). Break the voice register and the ad reads as an ad pretending to be native. This is format-specific, not brand-specific — log on any future Widget-style brief regardless of brand.

### Local Duplication of Global Rules

**Before:** In an earlier pass I added "No em-dashes in primary text" and an "AI-Chunky Copy" rule to Elevate's Script Criteria/Process Log. Both are already in [[Universal Copy Rules]] (em-dashes line 15, "VO must sound spoken, not written" line 17). The Elevate Working Document is supposed to hold OVERRIDES to the global baseline, not restatements.

**After:** Removed the em-dash rule from the Headline/Subheading section. Process Log's em-dash and AI-chunky examples stay (they're batch-specific execution notes), but the "rule" line of each points back to Universal Copy Rules rather than re-mandating.

**Rule:** Before adding a rule to the Elevate Script Criteria, check [[Universal Copy Rules]], [[Headline & Text Hook Criteria]], and the format-specific criteria doc. If the rule already exists globally, don't restate it — the "Baseline" pointer at the top of Script Criteria does that work. Local criteria is for Elevate-specific overrides only (compliance rules, brand tone, approved imagery, pricing, brand sourcing).

### References Database — Format-First, Not Brand-First

**Before:** When creating Notion References Database entries for the formats used in this batch (Creative + Social VoC, VoC Editorial, Grimy / City Poster, Premium Frosted Glass, Instagram Stories Widget, Cycle Diagram, Cartoon Head with Orbiting Food Icons), I wrote the descriptions brand-first — with Elevate's specific angle (food noise, diet cycle, insurance denial) woven into the format definition itself. That made the entries less transferable for strategists working on other brands.

**After:** Noted the existing rule in CLAUDE.md (References Database section, lines 236-258) covers this — the execution-focused / role-based structure and "Don't log brand-specific copy or claims. Only transferable patterns." The miss wasn't a missing rule. The miss was not reading CLAUDE.md before creating entries.

**Rule:** Before creating or editing any References Database entry, read the References Database section of CLAUDE.md. Write the entry format-first — describe the transferable pattern (visual structure, why it works, how to apply, role-based application) — and let brand examples live in the Link field and in the Examples section, not woven into the format definition. If swapping the originating brand out of the description makes the entry incoherent, the entry is brand-specific and needs a rewrite.

### Composition Density

**Key learning from the top spenders:** The best performers (Ads 10, 03, 02) have 5-7 visual elements. The worst performer (Ad 12, comparison chart) has 12+. Dense doesn't mean better — it means the viewer's eye has nowhere to land.

**System:** Max 6 elements + weight budget (12 pts max, review at 10). Comparison charts eat 3+ points by themselves — everything around them must be minimal. The logo and disclaimer don't count (always present, always tiny).

### Image Direction

**Problem:** First draft described abstract concepts ("a woman's silhouette with illustrated noise radiating from her head") instead of replicating what's proven. The WeightRx AD368 junk-food-smashing concept was right there.

**Fix:** When a reference ad exists, describe the actual visual approach from that reference and adapt it. Don't invent abstract concepts when concrete ones have already been tested. Be specific enough for designers to execute but not so prescriptive that the result looks unnatural.

### VoC Quote Block — Brief Format

**Before:** Quote text buried in Image Direction as a single bullet: "Reddit-style VoC quote block placed alongside or above the hero visual (designer decides). Quote text: [quote]"

**After:** Dedicated `### VoC Quote Block` section between Image Direction and Designer Note, containing the quote as a blockquote + a 3-row widget table (Username, Upvotes, Timestamp) + blur instruction. Image Direction references the section rather than embedding the quote.

**Rule:** For any brief using a Reddit-style VoC quote block, give it its own section. Fill in all widget fields with specific values — no placeholders. Don't include a subreddit field: subreddit names often contain competitor brand/drug names (r/WegovyWeightLoss, r/Semaglutide). Username should be a plausible placeholder blurred in the final design. Paraphrasing the source quote for clarity or brevity is acceptable when it reads better than verbatim.

### Format Naming

- "Creative + Social VoC" — NOT "Reddit-style" (it's not a native Reddit screenshot)
- "VoC Editorial" — NOT "VoC Magazine" (confuses designers)
- "Instagram Stories Widget" — NOT "IG Stories UI" (distinguishes from the existing full-screen IG Story format in the References database)
