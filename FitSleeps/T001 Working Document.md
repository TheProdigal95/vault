## Format Analysis

Before building the plan, here's the rationale for format choices based on FitSleeps' top spenders and the formats we want to test.

### What FitSleeps' Account Already Has (Top Spenders)

| Format | Count | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| Native image + long-form story (whitelisted) | 4 of 10 | The workhorse. Image stops scroll, story in primary text does the selling. 1,500-2,200 word narratives. |
| Humor skit → product showcase | 3 of 10 | Physical comedy hook (5s) earns attention for 15-25s of product/benefits. No VO needed. |
| Podcast-style UGC testimonial | 1 of 10 | Creator in interview setting, intercut with B-roll. Humor hook + social proof. |
| B-roll listicle (text overlay only) | 1 of 10 | Shortest format (13s). Benefits stack on screen. No VO. |
| BOF promo image | 1 of 10 | Conversion ad with discount, features, guarantee. |

### What's Missing (Formats to Test)

| Format | Why It Works | How It Translates |
|---|---|---|
| **Creator on green screen over B-roll** | Versatile format — creator educates while B-roll demonstrates. | FitSleeps has ONE clear mechanism to explain (sound habituation vs wrist vibration). A creator can educate on this while B-roll shows failed alternatives being dismissed and the product working. |
| **UGC talking-head direct to camera** | Different energy from podcast format — more personal, more intimate. | A creator telling their "I tried everything" story compressed into video. Borrows the story beats from the long-form text ads but delivers them as video. |
| **Overwhelm then simplify** | Show all the failed solutions, then collapse into one product. | FitSleeps version: "6 alarms + sunrise lamp + sleep app + dual-bell clock + partner shaking you... or one wristband." Show the pile, sweep it away. |

### This Batch: 4 Videos + 3 Images

**Videos give us format variety (4 distinct structures):**
1. UGC Podcast Testimonial — humor hook, proven FitSleeps format
2. Creator on Green Screen — mechanism education, new for FitSleeps
3. UGC Talking-Head — "I tried everything" story, new for FitSleeps
4. Narrated UGC Humor — relatable alarm scenarios, adapted from proven FitSleeps humor format

**Images follow the proven template (3 new stories):**
5. Native Image + Long-Form Story — shift worker/nurse (new stakes)
6. Native Image + Long-Form Story — considerate partner (new angle)
7. Native Image + Long-Form Story — parent for teen with ADHD (untested persona)

**Sleep Spindles (Harvard)**
"Research from Harvard shows heavy sleepers produce more of something called sleep spindles — bursts of brain activity that actively block sound from reaching your conscious brain. If you're a heavy sleeper, your brain is literally better at ignoring your alarm."
- *Source: Dang-Vu et al., 2010, Current Biology / Harvard*

**Sound Travels Through Air, Vibration Is Already On You**
"A sound alarm has to travel through the air, through your room, past your pillow, into your ear, and then your brain decides whether to let it through. A vibration alarm on your wrist is already touching you. There's no air gap, no pillow, no filter."
- *Source: Basic physics + somatosensory pathway*

**It's Not Laziness, It's Neuroscience (Validation)**
"Sleeping through your alarm isn't a discipline problem. Sleep scientists have known for decades that the brain habituates to repetitive sounds. Your brain is doing exactly what it's designed to do — the alarm is the problem, not you."
- *Source: General habituation science*

## Script Criteria

Permanent rules for writing scripts. These compound across batches. Derived from top spender analysis, creative direction, and mistakes corrected during T001.

### Copywriting Rules

**Voice and Flow:**
- No em-dashes in VO text. Scripts are spoken word. Use commas, periods, or natural sentence flow.
- Lines must flow like natural speech. Use connectors: "but," "then," "and finally," "because."
- Every mechanism claim needs a "so that" or "because" connector. "It vibrates on your wrist" means nothing alone. "...so it reaches your brain through a different pathway" gives the reason.
- VO must sound spoken, not written. If a line sounds like copy or a tagline, rewrite it as something a person would actually say. "Same problem, different packaging" is copy. "They're all just different versions of the same thing" is speech.
- Close sections must be conversational, not listing. "175,000 customers. 4.6 stars on Trustpilot." reads like a data dump. "It has 175,000 customers and a 4.6 on Trustpilot" sounds like a person talking.

**Product Introduction:**
- Must bridge from the problem/mechanism naturally. "That's why I switched to this" works because "that's why" references what was just explained. Never jump from education to product.
- Each script's product bridge must be unique to that concept. Do NOT reuse "It's called FitSleeps" across multiple scripts. V1 uses word-of-mouth ("my buddy showed me this band"), V2 uses personal experience ("that's why my partner and I switched"), V3 uses discovery-after-exhaustion ("that's when I found FitSleeps"), V4 goes straight to the name. Each earns the bridge differently.
- When a creator is speaking from personal experience, the product bridge must be congruent with their perspective. If the creator is a woman whose partner uses the product, she should say "that's why my partner and I switched to this" — not "that's why I switched."

**On-Screen Captions (Video Scripts):**
- Captions and VO are two separate hooks on two channels (sound on vs sound off). Caption creates its own curiosity gap, never repeats VO verbatim.
- Captions must work on mute as standalone headlines.
- Caption cells must contain ONLY the text that appears on screen. No formatting instructions, no markdown, no labels.
- Use dashes (-) not middle dots or special characters.
- Emoji in captions is acceptable (the brand uses them). Use sparingly — checkmarks and sleep emoji only. Don't overdo it.
- The close caption should stack proof points (175K+ customers, Trustpilot, guarantee) while the VO delivers the emotional beat. Two different messages on two channels, not the same message twice.

**Redundancy:**
- Never repeat the same phrasing between a hook variation and the body.
- Don't echo the hook's closing phrase at the body's opening.
- When a hook already names the product or explains the mechanism (as in humor hooks where the product IS the punchline), the body should skip the reintroduction and go straight to benefits.
- No two scripts in the same batch should have near-identical close sections. Each close must be structurally and verbally distinct.

**Qualitative Copy Standards:**
- Cleverness is fine when it's also clear. The test: would someone scrolling with zero context understand the problem AND feel curiosity? If the cleverness creates confusion, cut it. If it creates clarity faster than a literal statement would, keep it.
- Don't add attribution layers when you can state the thing directly. "People are saying this works" adds a hearsay layer. "This works" is direct. Same information, no attribution filter.
- Dollar amounts spoken aloud should sound like speech. "$89" sounds choppy. "Eighty-nine dollars" is how a person talks. This applies to casual numbers only — stats like "175,000 customers" and "4.6 stars" are fine as numbers because they're credibility signals.
- Hook captions must name something the viewer FEELS, not just something technically true. "YOUR BRAIN BLOCKS SOUND" is technically accurate but doesn't trigger identification. "HEAVY SLEEPER - EVERYTHING I TRIED WOKE HER UP" names an experience. The caption should make the viewer think "that's me."
- For persona-specific ads, establish the persona's condition somewhere in EVERY hook — either in the VO or in the caption. A heavy sleeper ad should mention "heavy sleeper." A partner ad should mention the partner. The viewer doesn't know which ad they're watching.

**Model Script Usage:**
- When writing a batch, write one model script first with the creative director. Iterate on it until the quality bar is set.
- Sub-agents then follow the model's FORMAT (section structure, table format, level of detail) but NOT its exact WORDING. Every product bridge, close, and body line must be unique to each concept.
- The model script teaches the structure. It does not provide copy to paste.

**Humor Register:**
- Humor is allowed and encouraged. FitSleeps' top-performing videos all use humor.
- Humor should be relatable exaggeration, not jokes. The comedy comes from recognizable situations pushed slightly past reality (sleeping through 8 alarms, roommate with a cooking pot).
- Self-deprecating humor builds trust. "I slept in my work clothes" is funny because it's embarrassingly real.
- The humor hook earns attention. The product pitch is straight.

**Partner Angle:**
- Even heavy sleeper ads should include a partner beat somewhere. "She was still asleep" is the emotional payoff in 7 of 10 FitSleeps top spenders.
- The partner doesn't need to be the focus — just present. A single line is enough.
- Exception: when the persona has no romantic partner (teenager in a dorm, single shift worker), a roommate fills the same role.

**Alternative Positioning:**
- Every script should mention at least 2 failed alternatives the viewer has tried. This is FitSleeps' most consistent persuasion technique.
- Name them specifically: sunrise lamp, Sleep Cycle app, phone under pillow, smartwatch alarm, dual-bell alarm clock.
- Include prices where known ($89 lamp, $12.99/mo app, $35 alarm clock).
- For videos, this can be visual (show the product, dismiss it) or spoken.
- When transitioning from the mechanism explanation to the failed alternatives, use a connector. Don't jump topics. "And that same filtering applies to most conventional alarm methods too" bridges from "your brain blocks sound" to "that's why a smartwatch is too weak."

**Mechanism Claims (Science):**
- All mechanism claims must be defensible. Reference `00 Context/Sleep Science Research - Why Vibration Works.md` for sourced claims.
- NEVER say "your brain can't ignore vibration" or "your brain can't habituate to vibration." This is scientifically false. Mechanoreceptors DO adapt to sustained vibration.
- CORRECT framing: "Vibration on your wrist uses a completely different sensory pathway than sound" / "It's much harder to sleep through" / "Touch and sound take different routes to your brain."
- NEVER say "vibration bypasses the thalamus." It doesn't. It uses different thalamic nuclei.
- When saying "regular alarms don't work," qualify it: "most conventional alarm methods" or "for heavy sleepers." Don't make absolute claims that would also apply to FitSleeps.

**Guarantee Phrasing:**
- Use "100 nights" consistently across all scripts and briefs. Not "100 days."

**Pricing:**
- Do not mention specific FitSleeps prices in scripts (pricing may be promotional and change).
- You CAN mention prices of failed alternatives ($89 lamp, $12.99/mo app, $35 alarm clock, $279 smartwatch).

### Structural Rules

**Hooks (0:00-0:05):**
- Must land in under 5 seconds.
- 3 variations per EVERY video brief:
  - **Variation A:** Primary hook type for the concept
  - **Variation B:** Untested type (humor, reframe, alternative callout, conditional — see Headline Types & Criteria doc for full reference)
  - **Variation C:** Must use a different visual style from A/B
- For narrated humor, the 3 variations can be different scenarios OR different text overlays on the same footage.
- Humor hooks (pattern interrupts, faux complaints, exaggerated scenarios) are the #1 proven hook type for FitSleeps video.
- Reference `00 Global/Headline Types & Criteria.md` for the full list of 18 core types and 3 registers (straight, humor, pattern interrupt).

**Time to Benefit:**
- Narrated humor: 5-8 seconds (problem shown, product by second 5-6)
- Podcast testimonial: Immediate (product named in first 2 seconds)
- Green screen educational: by 0:15 (mechanism first, then product)
- UGC talking-head: by 0:10 (faster than educational — the story carries the benefit)

**Body:**
- Specificity always. 100-night guarantee, 175,000 customers, 4.6 Trustpilot, 20-day battery, 2-minute escalating vibration.
- The mechanism explanation is ONE concept, not a multi-step system. "Your brain filters repetitive sounds. That's why you sleep through alarms. Vibration on your wrist uses a different pathway to your brain." That's the whole thing. Don't overcomplicate it.

**Close (final 5-8 seconds):**
Distribute across the batch:
1. **Emotional close:** "I finally feel in control of my mornings."
2. **Proof cascade:** Trustpilot + customer count + guarantee.
3. **Ease/simplicity close:** "One band. One purpose. And it actually works."
4. **Urgency/offer:** "100-night guarantee. If it's in stock, don't wait."
5. **Story close (image ads):** The narrative IS the close.

**Timing & Pacing:**
- No hard cap on duration. A good script should not be cut short just to hit a number. Suggested ranges for reference:
  - Narrated humor: ~20-30 seconds
  - Green screen educational: ~30-50 seconds
  - UGC talking-head: ~35-50 seconds
  - Podcast testimonial: ~30-50 seconds
- Image ad stories: ~1,200-1,500 words as a guide, but the story should be as long as it needs to be.
- Measure pacing in SYLLABLES PER MINUTE, not words per minute. Short words ("gut," "sleep," "one") take half the time of long words ("somatosensory," "bisglycinate"). Word count alone is misleading.
- **250 spm = comfortable conversational pace.** This is the target for body and close.
- **280 spm = brisk, energetic.** Acceptable for hooks and short punchy lists but not sustained body delivery.
- If a line feels rushed at its assigned timestamp, extend the timestamp or split the line into two rows. Do NOT cut good VO to hit a time target — extend the time instead.
- Timestamps in the script must reflect real delivery time. If a line has 40 syllables, it needs at least 10 seconds at 250 spm — don't assign it 5 seconds.
- When pacing is off, the fix is to adjust timestamps or split into more rows with more visual variety. The VO is written first; the time bends to fit it.

**Brief Delivery Format (Videos):**
Each video brief must include, in this order:
1. Brief name (Reach Digital_FitSleeps_Concept_##_T001)
2. Format one-liner (immediately tells the editor what type of video this is)
3. Editor Notes
4. Footage links (Google Drive folders for B-roll and content)
5. Footage needs (what needs to be filmed or generated)
6. Visual Direction (tone, edit pace, caption style, color)
7. References (links to FitSleeps top spender ads this borrows from)
8. Hook Variations (4-column tables: Time | Visual | Voiceover | On-Screen Captions)
9. Body (4-column table)
10. Close (4-column table)
- No quotation marks around VO text.
- Triple line dividers between briefs.

**Brief Delivery Format (Image Ads):**
Each image ad brief must include:
1. Brief name
2. References (links to FitSleeps top spender ads for style reference)
3. Designer Notes (own section — images are photo-only, no text/branding/overlays; headlines go in the platform not the image)
4. Headline Variations + Images (each headline paired with its own image direction)
5. Primary Text (the full long-form story, same for all headline variations)
6. Platform Headline (the one-liner below the image on Meta)
7. CTA Button text recommendation

**Image Direction Format:**
- One-liner scene description + key props. Not a long prompt-style paragraph.
- Must specify that the image looks native/organic — like someone shot it on an iPhone.
- Reference specific top spender images for style (e.g., "Reference Ad 01 image — coffee in car at warehouse — for the native style").

### Quality Checklist (run on every video brief)

1. Syllable count verified for every VO line. Body/close lines target 250 spm, hooks can be up to 280 spm. Timestamps match real delivery time.
2. Hook captions are different from VO (two separate hooks on two channels)
3. Hook captions work as standalone headlines on mute
4. Hook C uses a different visual style from A/B
5. No em-dashes in VO or caption text
6. No formatting labels in caption cells
7. No special characters in captions (use dashes, not middle dots)
8. No quotation marks wrapping VO text
9. Every mechanism claim has a "so that" or "because" connector
10. Mechanism claims are defensible — no "brain can't ignore vibration" or "bypasses the thalamus"
11. Product bridge is unique to this concept (not the same phrasing as other briefs in the batch)
12. Close type is assigned and varied across the batch — no two briefs share the same close structure
13. No redundancy between any hook variation and the body
14. All hook variations transition smoothly into the same body opening
15. At least 2 failed alternatives mentioned or shown
16. Partner angle present (even as a single line) in heavy sleeper briefs
17. Humor feels like relatable exaggeration, not forced comedy
18. VO sounds spoken, not written — no lines that read like taglines or copy
19. Close caption stacks proof points (customers, Trustpilot, guarantee) instead of repeating the VO
20. Creator's personal references are congruent with their perspective (a female creator says "my partner" not "my wife")
21. Guarantee phrasing is "100 nights" (not "100 days")
22. Brief includes a format one-liner, footage links, and reference links with actual URLs
23. Persona's condition appears in every hook (VO or caption) — "heavy sleeper," "partner," etc.
24. Hook captions name something the viewer FEELS, not just something technically true
25. Dollar amounts in VO sound like speech ("eighty-nine dollars" not "$89")

### Quality Checklist (run on every image ad brief)

1. Image looks native/organic — could be a real phone photo, not designed. No text, no branding, no overlays.
2. Image direction is a one-liner scene description + key props (not a long paragraph)
3. 3 headline variations, each paired with its own image
4. Headlines go in the platform (Meta headline field), NOT in the image

7. First line of primary text opens with high stakes — the reader must feel the consequence before they understand the context

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Issues documented from T001 batch work. These are the specific mistakes that were made and corrected. Read these before writing any new brief.

1. **Formatting labels in caption cells.** Never put "Milestone card:", "Keyword callout:", or any production note in the On-Screen Captions column. That column is ONLY what appears on screen.
2. **Middle dots in captions.** Use dashes (-) or line breaks. Nobody types the interpunct character on their phone.
3. **Product bridge copied across briefs.** "It's called FitSleeps" appeared in 3 of 4 video briefs in the first draft. Each brief needs its own unique product bridge.
4. **Hook/body redundancy.** If the hook already names the product or explains the mechanism, the body should skip the reintroduction. In T001, Hook B of the humor brief already revealed FitSleeps as "S tier," then the body said "It's called FitSleeps" again.
5. **Problem-first hooks without a bridge.** If a hook ends on the problem ("and your brain just tunes it out"), it needs to hint at a solution before the body starts ("but one device found a way around it").
6. **Stats without a human subject.** "99% wake on the first attempt" should be "99% of heavy sleepers woke on the first attempt." Add "of users" or "of people."
7. **Vague visual direction.** "Strong and competent, not intimidating" means nothing. "Creator speaks naturally, like telling a friend over coffee — not presenting" is actionable.
8. **All closes using the same structure.** In T001's first draft, V3's last body segment duplicated V4's close almost verbatim. Each close must be structurally and verbally distinct.
9. **"Brain can't ignore vibration" claim.** The Sleep Science Research doc explicitly flags this as false. Four briefs made this claim in the first draft. Use "different pathway" and "harder to sleep through" instead.
10. **Inconsistent guarantee phrasing.** V1 said "100 nights," V2 said "100 days," image ads said "100-day." Standardize to "100 nights" everywhere.
11. **Phonetically similar character names.** I5 used "Raquel" and I6 used "Rachel." Too close. Pick names that sound completely different across the batch.
12. **Fourth-wall breaks in story ads.** I7 said "That's not a sales pitch." A parent writing their own story would never say that. Never acknowledge that the story is an ad.
13. **Image ad openers without high stakes.** "I got the call at 10:15 on a Tuesday" doesn't hook. "My son was about to fail out of college" does. The first line must make the reader feel the stakes before they understand the context.
14. **Topic transitions without connectors.** In V2, the body jumped from "your brain filters sound" to "that's why a smartwatch is too weak" with no bridge. When switching topics in educational content, use a connector: "And that same filtering applies to most conventional alarm methods too."
15. **Pacing measured by vibes, not syllables.** The first draft assigned 5-second timestamps to 40-syllable lines. Always count syllables. At 250 spm, 40 syllables needs ~10 seconds. When pacing is off, extend the timestamp or split into more rows — don't cut the VO.
16. **Close sections that list instead of talking.** "175,000 customers. 4.6 stars on Trustpilot." reads like a bulleted list. "It has 175,000 customers and a 4.6 on Trustpilot" sounds like a person.
17. **Missing format one-liner.** Editors need to immediately understand what kind of video they're making. Every video brief needs a one-liner at the top: "Video — creator in podcast/interview setting, intercut with B-roll" or "Video — voiceover narration over B-roll and text overlays."
18. **Missing footage links and reference links.** Every brief needs Google Drive footage links and actual URLs to the top spender ads it references. "Link to be provided" is not a finished brief.

## Process Log — T001 Examples

The Script Criteria above contains the rules. This section shows what actually went wrong during T001 and how it was fixed. Read these to develop judgment about edge cases the rules don't cover.

**V2 body transition that jumped topics:**
- Before: "So your alarm goes off, and your brain just filters it out. That's why a smartwatch buzz is too weak, a sunrise lamp lights up the whole room..."
- Problem: The body jumps from explaining sound filtering to dismissing failed alternatives with no connector. The viewer goes from "your brain blocks sound" to "smartwatches are too weak" without understanding why one leads to the other.
- After: "So your alarm goes off, and your brain just filters it out. And that same filtering applies to most conventional alarm methods too. A smartwatch buzz is too weak to break through..."
- Why it works: "And that same filtering applies to most conventional alarm methods too" bridges the two ideas. The viewer understands that the sound-blocking principle is WHY all these other methods fail.

**V2 product bridge incongruent with creator's perspective:**
- Before: "That's why I switched to this. It's called FitSleeps." (Creator is a woman — but "I switched" implies she's the heavy sleeper, when the concept is that her partner is.)
- After: "That's why my partner and I switched to this. It's called FitSleeps. He gets up at five every morning and I used to wake up every single time."
- Why it works: The bridge is now congruent with who's speaking. The female creator is explaining the product from her perspective as the partner who used to get woken up.

**V2 close that read like a data dump:**
- Before: "175,000 customers. 4.6 stars on Trustpilot. And you get 100 days to try it risk-free."
- Problem: Sounds like someone reading bullet points, not a person talking.
- After: "It has 175,000 customers and a 4.6 on Trustpilot. You get 100 nights to try it risk-free. And honestly, she's going to notice the difference before you do."
- Why it works: Conversational phrasing ("It has..." instead of bare number) + the emotional kicker at the end makes it feel like a person giving genuine advice, not a sales pitch.

**"Brain can't ignore vibration" claim across 4 briefs:**
- Before: V2: "One your brain hasn't learned to block." V3: "so your brain can't learn to ignore it." V4: "so your brain can't ignore it like a phone alarm." I5: "Your nervous system can't ignore it."
- Problem: The Sleep Science Research doc explicitly flags "your brain can't habituate to vibration" as false. Mechanoreceptors DO adapt.
- After: V2: "One that's much harder to sleep through." V3: "because it uses touch instead of sound, it reaches your brain through a completely different pathway." V4: "so it reaches your brain through a different pathway than every alarm you've ever slept through." I5: "It reaches your brain through touch, not sound, so the filter that blocks your alarm doesn't apply."
- Why it works: Comparative language ("harder to sleep through," "different pathway") is defensible. Absolute language ("can't ignore," "can't habituate") is not.

**I7 fourth-wall break:**
- Before: "That's not a sales pitch. That's the neuroscience of why my son missed fourteen classes."
- Problem: A parent writing about their child's ADHD would never say "that's not a sales pitch." It acknowledges this is an ad, breaking the story format.
- After: "That's why my son missed fourteen classes. Not because he didn't care. Because his brain was built to block the one thing every alarm relies on: sound."
- Why it works: Same information, same emotional weight, but told from inside the story instead of stepping outside it.

**Image ad openers without high stakes:**
- Before (I5): "I showed up to my unit forty-three minutes late on a Tuesday night."
- Before (I7): "I got the call at 10:15 on a Tuesday."
- Problem: These are factual statements that don't create urgency. The reader doesn't know the stakes yet.
- After (I5): "The morning I almost lost my nursing license, I showed up to my unit forty-three minutes late."
- After (I7): "My son was about to fail out of college, and it had nothing to do with effort."
- Why it works: The consequence is front-loaded. The reader feels the stakes on line one, then reads to understand the context.

**V3/V4 close duplication:**
- Before: V3's last body segment said "They give you a hundred days to try it. 175,000 people already have. And it sells out, so if it's in stock, grab it." V4's close said "They give you a hundred days to try it. 175,000 people already have. If it's in stock right now, don't wait." Nearly identical.
- Problem: Same structure, same proof point, same scarcity language across two briefs. A viewer who sees both ads hears the same close twice.
- After: V3's last body segment changed to a personal beat: "I haven't set a phone alarm in three months. Not one. I just put this on and go to sleep." V3's close became: "One band. One purpose. 100-night money-back guarantee. FitSleeps." V4 kept the urgency/scarcity close.
- Why it works: V3 earns its ease/simplicity close type. V4 keeps urgency/offer. They no longer sound like the same ad.

**Product bridge copied from model across sub-agent scripts:**
- Before: V2, V3, and V4 all used "It's called FitSleeps" as the product bridge — the same phrasing the model script (Brief 1) used.
- Problem: Sub-agents copied the model's wording, not just its format. By the third brief, the batch feels template-driven.
- After: V2 uses personal experience ("That's why my partner and I switched to this"), V3 uses discovery-after-exhaustion ("That's when I found FitSleeps"), V4 drops the naming entirely since the hooks already introduce the product.
- Lesson: When briefing sub-agents, explicitly state: "Follow the model's FORMAT, not its WORDING. Every product bridge must be unique."

**Creator filming instructions that assumed equipment:**
- Before: "Film on green screen" implied Karra needs a green screen setup.
- After: Added note: "You don't need an actual green screen. Just find a background that's not too busy so we can key it out in post."
- Lesson: Never assume what equipment or setup a creator has. Describe the result you want, not the equipment you assume.

**Pacing that looked right by gut but was wrong by syllables:**
- Before: V2's body was assigned 8 seconds for a 47-syllable line (~352 spm — physically impossible to deliver naturally). The timestamps were estimated by feel, not counted.
- After: Syllable count verified per-line. The 47-syllable line was extended to 10 seconds (282 spm) and the body was split into more rows to match real delivery time.
- Lesson: Always count syllables before assigning timestamps. Estimate-by-feel consistently underestimates delivery time for educational or mechanism-heavy content.
