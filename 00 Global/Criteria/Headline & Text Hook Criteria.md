# Headline & Text Hook Criteria

Reference for writing ad headlines (static ads) and text hooks (the first on-screen caption in video ads) across any brand. These are the same skill — both must stop the scroll in under 2 seconds with zero context. Apply on top of [[Universal Copy Rules]].

Every headline/hook has two layers: a **core type** (the structural architecture) and a **register** (the delivery tone). The same core type can be delivered straight, through humor, or as a pattern interrupt. Pick the core type first, then decide the register.

**Hook definition (canonical for this vault):** The first stop-the-scroll element — a static ad headline, the first on-screen caption of a video, or the first line of a native post. Must communicate problem and/or curiosity in under 2 seconds with zero context. Video Script and Creative Image criteria reference this definition.

## Quick Reference: 18 Core Types

| # | Type | What It Does | Example | Best For |
|---|------|-------------|---------|----------|
| 1 | **Provocation** | Challenges the viewer's belief or behavior | "If Your Probiotic Worked, Why Are You Still Bloated?" | Cold traffic in high-awareness markets |
| 2 | **Mechanism Contrast** | Old way vs. new way in two clauses | "Nylon Seals Heat In. Alpaca Lets It Out." | Products with a genuinely different mechanism |
| 3 | **Conditional** | "If you X, then Y" — qualifies the reader | "If You Map Every Bathroom Before You Leave the House, Your Gut Lining Is the Problem." | Pre-selecting audience; works gentle or aggressive |
| 4 | **VoC Hook** | Customer quote as the lead, bridges to cause | "'My Cramps Used to Knock Me Out for a Day.' I Fixed My Leaky Gut." | When review language is more vivid than anything you'd write |
| 5 | **Mechanism Chain** | Step-by-step causation sequence | "Your Gut Is Inflamed. Inflamed Guts Speed Everything Up. That's the Urgency." | Educated or research-driven audiences |
| 6 | **Ideal Self** | Paints life after the fix — no problem language | "Eat Dinner. Feel Nothing. No Bloat. No Unbuttoning. That's Day 7." | Warm traffic that already understands the problem |
| 7 | **Credibility / Authority** | Leads with stats, data, or clinical proof | "67% of Accident Victims Accept Less Than Half of What They Could Get." | When a number creates a gap or surprises |
| 8 | **Alternative Callout** | Lists what they tried + why it failed | "L-Glutamine. Colostrum. Probiotics. None of Them Repair Your Gut Lining." | Solution-aware audiences who've tried and failed |
| 9 | **Problem-Only** | Pure pain, no solution — subheading carries the pivot | "Car Accident. Emergency Room. Physical Therapy. Bills. More Bills. No End in Sight." | When the pain is specific enough to be the hook |
| 10 | **Time Progression** | Mini-story with timestamps or sequence | "Week 1: The Emergency Room. Week 3: The Bills Start. Week 6: They're Still Coming." | Problems that escalate over time |
| 11 | **Validation → Pivot** | Empathizes first, then redirects to solution | "You Shouldn't Have to Choose Between Healing and Paying Bills." | Hesitant, self-doubting, or minimizing audiences |
| 12 | **Confrontational Command** | Imperative. Tells the viewer what to do. | "Stop Paying Medical Bills Alone. Talk to an Attorney for Free." | Purchase-stage or high-urgency situations |
| 13 | **Reframe** | Replaces a wrong belief with the right one | "Bathroom Anxiety Isn't a Personality Trait. It's a Damaged Gut Lining." | When a limiting belief prevents action |
| 14 | **Formula / Math** | Equation format — math tells the story | "$40 Once Or $400 in Dead Shirts. Your Call." | Cost/value propositions, financial comparison |
| 15 | **Conversational Question** | Low-pressure question mirroring their internal dialogue | "Still Paying Medical Bills From a Car Accident?" | Broad targeting; uncertain audiences |
| 16 | **Short / Direct** | Fewest words, maximum punch — image carries the rest | "$40 Now. $0 Forever." | Image-led ads, retargeting, Purchase stage |
| 17 | **Identity Callout** | Names who the viewer is; identity IS the hook | "Mechanics Don't Buy Shirts That Quit." | Lifestyle brands where "who uses this" > "what does this do" |
| 18 | **Curiosity Gap** | Opens a loop the viewer must click to close | "Here's Why Your Probiotics Never Worked." | Cold traffic; mechanism-education angles |

## Registers

A register is the delivery tone layered onto any core type. The same Reframe can be delivered straight ("It's not age. It's joint inflammation."), through humor ("Your dryer didn't shrink it. Your shirt was just weak."), or as a pattern interrupt ("DO NOT blame your age for joint pain."). Pick the register based on audience and category.

### Straight

Default register. No tonal wrapper. The structure and message do the work alone. Use when the information itself is compelling enough, or when the category demands professional/clinical tone.

Most headlines across health supplements, legal, financial, and medical devices use this register.

### Humor

The joke creates the stop; the value prop rides inside the humor. **Humor is a delivery vehicle, not an angle** — funny without the value prop doesn't convert. Funny AND specific does.

**The core rule:** Humor describes the PROBLEM, not the solution. The joke is about their suffering. The product is straight, clear relief. "Your closet is a shirt graveyard" = funny. "Hemp + bamboo. Guaranteed for life" = straight.

**Humor subtypes:**

| Subtype | What It Does | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Self-deprecating** | Viewer laughs at a shared experience, never laughed at | "Your collar looks like a wavy potato chip." |
| **Sarcastic observation** | Mock celebration of a problem they recognize | "Congratulations. Your shirt lasted 3 washes." |
| **Absurd comparison** | Unexpected frame that makes the problem vivid | "Your wife's yoga pants have better fabric than your shirt." |
| **Invented vocabulary** | Absurdly specific terms that create bonding | "Bacon neck," "man titties," "doughy gut," "shirt graveyard" |
| **Cost sarcasm** | Numbers + irony | "$40 once or $400 in dead shirts. Your call." |

**How humor layers onto core types:**

| Core Type | Straight | Humor Register |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Provocation | "Your probiotic can't colonize a damaged gut." | "Bacon neck isn't a style. It's a cry for help." |
| Reframe | "It's not age. It's joint inflammation." | "Your dryer didn't shrink it. Your shirt was just weak." |
| Formula / Math | "Emergency Room + PT + Missed Work = Claim." | "$40 once or $400 in dead shirts. Your call." |
| Alternative Callout | "Gabapentin. Lyrica. Ice packs. None worked." | "You've switched deodorants three times. It was never you." |
| Mechanism Contrast | "Nylon seals heat in. Alpaca lets it out." | "You're not sweaty. Your shirt just traps heat like a trash bag." |
| Identity Callout | "Built for men who work with their hands." | "Your nipples shouldn't be visible in a T-shirt." |

**When to use:** Male audiences (especially blue-collar or fitness-adjacent), products where earnest aspirational copy gets rejected, categories where the problem has shame (humor gives permission), when VoC data contains invented vocabulary or self-deprecation.

**When NOT to use:** Legal/compliance-heavy categories, severe-pain medical categories, when you don't have real VoC humor to draw from (forced humor reads as tone-deaf).

**Key insight:** "Men use humor as a defense mechanism. When your copy matches their humor register, they feel understood. Copy that's funny-then-serious builds instant trust." Open with their humor, close with the solution.

**What doesn't work:** Humor without a value prop, mean-spirited roasts that shame the viewer, earnest copy where humor was available (e.g., "Look like you lift" fell flat; "No man titties" performed).

### Pattern Interrupt

Breaks the pattern of what the viewer expects to see in the category. Earns attention that predictable formats can't. **Must deliver real substance after the stop** — interrupt without payoff = clickbait.

**Pattern Interrupt subtypes:**

| Subtype | What It Does | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **WARNING / DO NOT** | Alarm-format opener | "DO NOT Pay Medical Bills Without Talking to an Attorney First." |
| **Contrarian / "Don't Buy"** | Says the opposite of what an ad should say | "Do Not Buy This Board." |
| **Contradiction** | Two similar inputs, different outcomes | "Both Ate the Same Food. Only One Got Lumps. Here's Why." |
| **Format break** | Uses a visual/structural format the viewer doesn't expect in an ad | "BREAKING:" or numbered list or fake screenshot |

**How pattern interrupt layers onto core types:**

| Core Type | Straight | Pattern Interrupt Register |
|-----------|----------|--------------------------|
| Confrontational Command | "Stop paying medical bills alone." | "WARNING: Medical bills can follow you for years." |
| Provocation | "Your probiotic can't colonize a damaged gut." | "DO NOT take another probiotic until you read this." |
| Credibility / Authority | "67% accept less than half." | "EXPOSED: What insurance companies don't tell you." |
| Problem-Only | "Bills. More bills. No end in sight." | "Both ate the same food. Only one got lumps." |

**When to use:** Oversaturated categories, cold traffic needing a pattern break, products with a genuinely contrarian position.

**When NOT to use:** No real substance behind the interrupt (empty shock erodes trust), professional-tone categories, too frequently within the same brand (loses power when every ad is an interrupt).

## Core Type Details

Each entry adds signal beyond the Quick Reference table: one canonical example plus any risk, variant, or distinction worth preserving. For the baseline definition and "best for" column, use the table.

### 1. Provocation
"You're Not Fat. You're Inflamed."
**Risk:** Crosses into shaming if it targets choices rather than situation.
**Variant — Peer-behavior provocation:** describes other people's inaction to trigger "I'm not like most people." "Most Flock Owners Don't Act Until Unwanted Guests Have Already Settled In."

### 2. Mechanism Contrast
"Probiotics Add Bacteria. Tributyrin Repairs the Lining. Guess Which Fixes Bloating."
The contrast must be structural (how the mechanism differs), not just "better." This type can carry an entire brand when the mechanism IS the differentiator.

### 3. Conditional
"If You Map Every Bathroom Before You Leave the House, Your Gut Lining Is the Problem."
Variation: gentle qualification ("If you've been thinking about…") vs. imperative ("If you X, then Y — do this now").

### 4. VoC Hook
"'My Cramps Used to Knock Me Out for a Day. Now They're Background Noise.' I Fixed My Leaky Gut."
Quote must be authentic (from reviews). Self-deprecating humor is the strongest variant. Bridge back to second person after the quote. Short enough for a static ad.
**Variant — First-person raw (emotional plea):** not a customer quote, but a raw first-person line from the writer or brand. Use sparingly — overuse sounds desperate. "I'm Begging You to Try Tributyrin."

### 5. Mechanism Chain
"Your Gut Is Inflamed. Inflamed Guts Speed Everything Up. That's the Urgency."
**Risk:** reads educational rather than emotional if the chain is too clinical. The last step must connect to felt experience.

### 6. Ideal Self (The After)
"Eat Dinner. Feel Nothing. No Bloat. No Unbuttoning. That's Day 7."
**Critical rule:** must tap base human desires (looking good, control, freedom, energy) — NOT product-centric aspiration ("confidence from a scoop" fails).

### 7. Credibility / Authority
"The Only Gut Supplement With 55% Bioavailability to the Colon."
All stats require source documentation and compliance approval. Numbers exact — never round, never extrapolate. The number itself must be surprising or gap-creating.

### 8. Alternative Callout
"L-Glutamine. Colostrum. Probiotics. None of Them Repair Your Gut Lining."
Name solution categories, never competitor brand names. 3–4 alternatives is the sweet spot. Must explain WHY they failed (mechanism), not just that they did.

### 9. Problem-Only
"Car Accident. Emergency Room. Physical Therapy. Bills. More Bills. No End in Sight."
Must pair with a strong subheading. Problem-only without a solution pivot = frustration, not conversion.

### 10. Time Progression
"Week 1: The Emergency Room. Week 3: The Bills Start. Week 6: They're Still Coming."
Underused in DTC. Works for product results too: "Day 1: You take it. Day 2: Inflammation stops. Day 3: Your gut starts rebuilding."

### 11. Validation → Pivot
"Those Medical Bills Aren't Your Problem to Carry Alone."
**Distinct from Reframe:** Validation → Pivot empathizes then redirects. Reframe challenges the belief directly without empathy preamble.
**Variant — Permission-giving:** explicitly removes "I'm not allowed to" or "I'm overreacting" barriers. "You Don't Need to Be Sure You Have a Case. Just Ask."

### 12. Confrontational Command
"Stop Paying Medical Bills Alone. Talk to an Attorney for Free."
Variation: aggressive ("STOP doing X") vs. gentle caring push ("Don't talk yourself out of…").
**Distinct from Provocation:** Provocation challenges beliefs. Confrontational Command demands action.

### 13. Reframe
"Bathroom Anxiety Isn't a Personality Trait. It's a Damaged Gut Lining."
The most powerful cold-traffic pattern. Makes the viewer feel seen AND gives them new information in one sentence.
**Distinct from Provocation:** Provocation challenges. Reframe replaces one understanding with a better one.

### 14. Formula / Math
"Emergency Room + Physical Therapy + Missed Work = Car Accident Claim."
The math tells the story faster than prose.

### 15. Conversational Question
"Still Paying Medical Bills From a Car Accident? Have You Talked to an Attorney?"
**Distinct from Provocation:** Provocation challenges. Conversational Question empathizes and gently prompts. Should sound like something they asked themselves last night.

### 16. Short / Direct
"$40 Now. $0 Forever."
Best for image-led, retargeting, and Purchase-stage ads where the viewer already has context.
**Variant — CTA-forward:** the headline IS the call to action. Works for high-hesitation audiences. "Just Ask. Free. No Obligation."

### 17. Identity Callout
"Mechanics Don't Buy Shirts That Quit."
Doesn't require mechanism in the headline because identity carries the stop.
**Key insight:** ICP aspiration > ICP identification. "30 Years In Construction" works not because the buyer IS a construction worker, but because he WANTS to be that tough.

### 18. Curiosity Gap
"Here's Why Your Probiotics Never Worked."
The payoff must be real — the answer lives in the ad body, subheading, or landing page. Works best paired with a genuine mechanism reveal, not bait-and-switch. The gap must be specific ("here's why your probiotics failed") not vague ("this one trick").
**Variant — Story lead:** third-person mini-narrative. "She Wore a Fitted Dress to Her Sister's Wedding. Two Months Ago She Wouldn't Have."

## Content-Based Hook Categories

Core types describe the headline's *architecture* — how it works. Content-based categories describe the headline's *subject* — what it talks about. These are angle-specific and vary by brand.

The best headlines combine a core type + a register + a content category. Example: a Pattern Contradiction (content) delivered as a Provocation (type) in Pattern Interrupt register = "Both Ate the Same Food. Only One Got Lumps. Here's Why."

Common content categories seen across brands:

| Content Category | What It Talks About | Example |
|-----------------|--------------------| --------|
| Fear to Relief | Worst-case fear diffused by information | "Weird bumps on your dog? Vet explains why it's probably not cancer" |
| Recurrence / Escalation | The problem gets worse if you wait | "The root stays behind. That's why lipomas keep returning" |
| Cost / Risk of Inaction | Financial or health cost of not acting | "$3,000 per lump or $40 for prevention?" |
| Authority / Expert | Professional credibility as the hook | "My vet found three fatty lumps. I found this" |
| Transformation | Before → after as social proof | "My 12-year-old acts like a puppy again" |
| Daily Impact | Specific moment the problem controls | "Hip bumps making stairs a struggle?" |
| Simplicity / Ease | The solution is easier than expected | "Fatty lumps multiplying? Sprinkle this on food daily" |
| Pattern Contradiction | Two similar inputs, different outcomes | "Both ate the same food. Only one got lumps. Here's why" |
| Breakthrough / Victory | The problem is solved — celebrate | "His Lumps Are GONE Now" |

These are not exhaustive. New content categories emerge from VoC data for each brand.

## Tactics Layer

33 tactical frames for hook writing and classification, organized by psychological function. Parallel to the 18 core types — core types describe structural architecture (how the hook works), tactics describe strategic frame (what psychological job the hook does).

**How to use:** Start from the core type when you know the structural pattern. Start from the tactic when you know the psychological job ("I need to trigger reactance" → Reverse Psychology; "I need social belonging anxiety" → FOMO). The cross-walk table at the end maps between systems.

### Identity & Audience Tactics

| Tactic | Frame | Triggers | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aspirational | Frames identity/lifestyle/status the viewer wants | Aspiration, Identity Call-Out | "What your morning routine looks like when you actually sleep 8 hours" |
| Demographic Callout | Names a specific segment to qualify relevance | Identity Call-Out | "Remote workers who can't focus past 2pm... this is for you" |
| Direct Address | Speaks directly and personally to the viewer | Identity Call-Out, Pain Agitation | "You've been washing your face wrong your entire life" |
| If-Then | Qualifies with a condition, then promises an outcome | Identity Call-Out, Pain Agitation | "If you've tried every mattress and still wake up stiff... read this" |
| Relatability | Anchors in a shared, everyday scenario for empathy or humor | Pain Agitation, Identity Call-Out | "That moment when you check your bank account after a 'quick Target run'" |

### Credibility & Proof Tactics

| Tactic | Frame | Triggers | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Authority | Credibility via expertise or credentials (not popularity) | Social Proof | "I'm a dermatologist and I'd never use the products I prescribe on my own skin" |
| Confession | Candid reveal to build trust through vulnerability | Pattern Interrupt, Social Proof | "I spent $4,000 on skincare last year and my skin got worse" |
| Reasons Why | Explicit "reasons why" phrasing (not generic list) | Curiosity Gap, Social Proof | "3 reasons your protein powder is making you bloated" |
| Social Proof | Leads with evidence from other people's experiences | Social Proof, Urgency | "47,000 people switched to this in January alone" |
| Statistic | Opens with a surprising or compelling number | Pattern Interrupt, Social Proof | "93% of people are using the wrong pillow height for their sleep position" |

### Belief & Positioning Tactics

| Tactic | Frame | Triggers | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Belief | Brand POV or values-led opener (the brand's mission, not customer identity) | Pattern Interrupt, Identity Call-Out | "I genuinely believe this is the most important thing you can do for your gut health" |
| Contrarian | Goes against conventional wisdom to create surprise | Pattern Interrupt, Contrarian/Myth-Busting | "Sunscreen is actually making your skin worse. Here's why." |
| Myth Busting | Debunks a widely held misconception with corrective facts | Contrarian/Myth-Busting, Curiosity Gap | "The '8 glasses of water' rule is completely made up" |

### Emotion & Desire Tactics

| Tactic | Frame | Triggers | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Challenge | Competitive framing that invites a test or attempt | Pattern Interrupt, Identity Call-Out | "Try this for 7 days and tell me your sleep didn't change" |
| Curiosity | Creates an open loop or tease (the loop must feel worth closing) | Curiosity Gap | "There's one ingredient in your shampoo that's causing all the damage" |
| Storytelling | Drops mid-moment into a personal or brand story | Curiosity Gap, Pain Agitation | "Last Tuesday I almost threw my laptop out the window. Here's what happened." |
| FOMO | Anxiety about missing a trend or social movement (belonging, not time) | Social Proof, Urgency | "Everyone in my friend group uses this except me. I finally tried it." |
| Exclusivity | Selective access or "not for everyone" (gated access, not belonging) | Urgency, Identity Call-Out | "This was only available to dermatology patients until last month" |
| Urgency | Time or supply pressure to act now (scarcity, not belonging or access) | Urgency/Stakes | "They're pulling this offer at midnight. I'm not even joking." |

### Educational & Informational Tactics

| Tactic | Frame | Triggers | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Directive | Imperative that reframes thinking or behavior (not immediate shopping) | Pattern Interrupt, Identity Call-Out | "Stop comparing your morning to everyone else's highlight reel" |
| Explainer | Explains causes or rationale using "why" phrasing | Curiosity Gap, Pattern Interrupt | "The reason your coffee tastes bitter has nothing to do with the beans" |
| How To | Instructional promise that teaches a task or fix | Curiosity Gap | "How to fall asleep in under 5 minutes without melatonin" |
| Listicle | Numbered or list-based framing without explicit "why" rhetoric | Curiosity Gap | "5 things I stopped buying that saved me $400/month" |
| Question | Opens with a direct question that triggers self-reflection | Pain Agitation, Curiosity Gap | "Why does your face look tired even when you slept 9 hours?" |

### Action & Conversion Tactics

| Tactic | Frame | Triggers | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bold Claim | Outsized or extreme promise for impact | Pattern Interrupt, Contrarian | "This $12 serum replaced my entire skincare routine" |
| Call To Action First | Opens with explicit action instruction (shopping, not mindset) | Urgency, Pattern Interrupt | "Add this to cart before you forget. Your future self will thank you." |
| Contrast | Juxtaposes two things to highlight a mismatch or imbalance | Pattern Interrupt, Pain Agitation | "What I was spending on coffee vs. what I spend now" |
| Offer Only | Discount or monetary incentive as the sole hook (no urgency/comparison) | Urgency | "40% off everything. No code needed." |
| Price Anchor | Frames cost against a familiar benchmark | Pattern Interrupt, Social Proof | "People assume this costs $200. It's $29." |
| Risk Reversal | Reduces perceived risk with guarantees or assurances | Social Proof, Urgency | "If it doesn't work in 30 days, keep it and get your money back" |
| Shocking Statement | Surprising or provocative claim that challenges assumptions | Pattern Interrupt | "I threw away every product my dermatologist recommended" |
| Warning | Sincere caution that halts default behavior until you explain | Pain Agitation, Urgency | "Stop using this ingredient immediately if you have sensitive skin" |
| Reverse Psychology | Tells the viewer NOT to do something, triggering reactance | Pattern Interrupt, Curiosity Gap | "Don't buy this if you're not serious about fixing your sleep" |

## Distinction Tables

11 tactic pairs that look similar but serve different psychological functions. Confusing them produces hooks that feel derivative or misfire. Check when two hooks in a batch feel too similar.

| Tactic A | Tactic B | The Difference |
|---|---|---|
| Contrast | Contrarian | Contrast highlights a mismatch or imbalance between two things. Contrarian breaks the rule of what logic and expertise says should be true. |
| Confession | Storytelling | Confession is a candid reveal — one vulnerable admission. Storytelling drops into a narrative scene mid-moment. |
| Exclusivity | FOMO | Exclusivity gates access ("only available to X"). FOMO drives social belonging anxiety ("everyone is doing this except you"). |
| Urgency | FOMO | Urgency applies time/supply pressure ("ends tonight"). FOMO centers on missing a trend ("you're the last person to try this"). |
| Directive | Call To Action First | Directive reframes thinking or behavior ("stop comparing yourself"). CTA First instructs immediate shopping/action ("add to cart now"). |
| Offer Only | Price Anchor | Offer Only leads with the discount alone ("40% off"). Price Anchor frames cost against a benchmark ("you'd think $200, it's $29"). |
| Question | Curiosity | Question is direct and triggers self-reflection. Curiosity creates an information gap without directly asking. |
| Bold Claim | Shocking Statement | Bold Claim asserts a strong positive ("replaced my entire routine"). Shocking Statement provokes with something unexpected or alarming. |
| Explainer | How To | Explainer explains why something happens. How To teaches the steps to do something. |
| Reasons Why | Listicle | Reasons Why uses explicit "reasons why" phrasing. Listicle uses numbered format without "why" rhetoric. |
| Belief | Aspirational | Belief expresses the brand's POV or mission. Aspirational speaks to the viewer's desired identity or lifestyle. |

## Hook Quality Bar

Every hook must pass all 5 tests before shipping. Approximately 35% of generated hooks typically clear this bar.

### 5-Test Gate

1. **Genuine cognitive friction** — would a stranger scrolling at full speed involuntarily stop? The hook must break autopilot.
2. **Strong psychological trigger** — you must name the specific trigger, and it must feel powerful, not just technically present. Valid triggers: pattern interrupt, identity call-out, pain agitation, curiosity gap, social proof/validation, contrarian/myth-busting, loss aversion, visceral/taboo, relatable experience, brand vulnerability.
3. **Complete thought** — the hook gives the viewer enough to feel the friction. No half-formed ideas, no setups without payoffs.
4. **Authentic voice** — sounds like something a real person would say on camera, not polished ad copy. If a skeptical viewer would roll their eyes, it fails.
5. **Personal stakes** — answers "why should I care?" There must be a personal emotion, consequence, or desire at play. Naming a tactic without connecting to a feeling is not enough.

### What ALWAYS Fails

- Product/brand before tension — lead with friction, not the brand name
- Benefit-first without pain — the viewer must feel the problem before you offer anything
- Content format announcements without tension — "5 signs of...", "3 reasons why...", "Here's how to..." as openers *when benefit-first or brand-first.* Pain-agitated versions pass (e.g., "3 reasons your protein powder is making you bloated" works because pain leads)
- Vlog openers with no hook mechanism — "Hey guys, so today..."
- Unfinished hooks — trigger set up but the thought goes nowhere
- Commands without the why — "Stop buying X" without making the viewer FEEL the cost
- Broad accusations — "You're doing X wrong" must specify HOW or WHAT is at stake
- Overly dramatic claims — "I threw out every pair of shoes I owned" sounds manufactured
- Demographic callouts without emotion — "I'm 43, a mum of three" is a census entry, not a hook
- Rhetorical questions with obvious answers — if the viewer isn't personally implicated, it doesn't work
- Generic testimonials — "I love this product" without prior skepticism or surprise
- In-group language for cold traffic — don't assume the viewer knows your world

### Format Constraints

- Hooks average 75 characters and never exceed 100.
- Conversational, authentic, specific to the brand's product and audience.
- Vary tactics across hooks — use a different tactic for each. Don't repeat the same tactic or trigger pattern.

### Calibrated Examples

**PASS:**
- "So [celebrity] came out with a protein and I do not think I have ever been more offended." — Celebrity pattern interrupt with humor and curiosity gap.
- "We apologize to anyone who purchased our starter kit bundle because we lied." — Brand vulnerability. An apology from a brand forces attention.
- "You know, normally I'd be like, wow, really shiny hair. No, girl, this is oil." — Relatable experience with visceral honesty. The pivot from compliment to confession is authentic.
- "This one easy mistake will cost you thousands when buying a used car." — Loss aversion with specific stakes.
- "Why did no one tell me these foods were hurting me?" — Pain agitation with betrayal. The viewer feels implicated.
- "This is my 48 year old chest, redness, rosacea, broken capillaries, stretch marks." — Visceral vulnerability with demographic specificity.

**FAIL:**
- "[Brand] bikes are lightweight, kid-friendly, and thoughtfully designed." — Benefit-first, no tension. Just a product description.
- "These [brand] meals really made my life so much easier." — Generic testimonial. No friction.
- "Three reasons why men love [brand]. Number one, self-locking design." — Content format announcement. Instant scroll-past.
- "Well, top of the morning, friends. Hope you're having a good one." — Vlog opener with zero hook mechanism.
- "Did you know 65 to 90% of cancers are preventable based off of lifestyle factors?" — Stat-lead with no personal stakes. Facts don't stop scrolls — feelings do.
- "I told y'all we were doing a restock restock." — In-group language for cold traffic. Only makes sense if you already follow this brand.

## Layering Frameworks

Beyond core type and register, every headline operates on two strategic dimensions: **Awareness Stages** and **Three Selves**. Full framework detail in [[Creative Strategy Matrix]]. Headline-specific application notes below.

- **Awareness Stage application:** Each ad serves ONE stage. At Unaware, headlines educate — no CTA, no discount. At Problem-Aware, agitate and validate. At Solution-Aware, teach WHY your approach works. At Product-Aware, proof/comparison/before-after. At Most-Aware, offer/guarantee/commitment. See Tactics Layer → Deployment by Awareness Stage for which tactics fit each stage.
- **Three Selves application:** Organize headline options by Self within each angle to force variety. Actual Self is overused (~60% of most ad libraries); Ought Self is massively underused (~5%) and produces high-intent, high-LTV buyers; Ideal Self must tap base human desires, not product-centric aspiration ("confidence from a scoop" fails the test).

## Universal Headline Rules

Shared rules (no em-dashes, no false dichotomies, no "It's not X. It's not Y." lists, mechanism required, specificity always, no fourth-wall breaks, clarity over cleverness) live in [[Universal Copy Rules]]. The items below are headline-specific extensions.

### Headline-Specific Copy Rules
- No character limits on static ad headlines (unless brand-specific).
- **2-second zero-context test** — a person scrolling with zero context must understand the problem AND the solution (or the subheading completes it).
- **First-read anchor test.** The headline is the first thing read and often the only thing read. Whether it must self-anchor depends on the format:
  - **Image-forward formats** — creative hero image, real-person photography, comparison chart, cycle diagram. Anything where the visual names the category on its own. The headline CAN lean on the image for category and focus on a specific angle, moment, or mechanism.
  - **Image-light formats** — IG Stories Widget (image sits behind a widget/card), Flyer / City Poster (typographic-only), other text-card-dominant layouts. The headline MUST independently carry at least one of: **category** (the thing being sold — "medical weight loss," "attorney," "probiotic"), **specific mechanism** (the named problem or solution — "food noise," "gut lining," "diet cycle"), or **price anchor** ("$58/week," "$0 down," "free").
  - Evocative micro-moments (a seatbelt, a mirror, "money well spent") can pair with an anchor — they cannot substitute for one on image-light formats. Sub-as-crutch is not an acceptable pattern when the headline is the load-bearing element.
  - Before ship on an image-light format: imagine the hero reduced to a blur at thumbnail. Does the headline alone sell the category? If no, rewrite.
- **Second person** — "You bloat after every meal." Exception: VoC quotes can be first person but must bridge back.
- **Customer language only** — match the PDP, match the reviews. If a term isn't on the landing page, don't use it in the ad.
- **Name solution categories, never competitor brand names** (probiotics, fish oil — not Seed, Align).
- **No shaming** — strong words fine in VoC, not in headlines directed at the viewer.
- **Vary the closers** — if 5 headlines end the same way, they all sound the same.

### Subheading Rules
- Ultra-short: under 15 words.
- **2-3 per angle** that rotate: one mechanism-forward, one credibility-forward, one outcome-forward.
- The subheading completes headlines that are problem-only.

### Proof Point Rules
- 1-3 words each, not sentences.
- Don't cite review counts under 2000.
- Include mechanism proof, clinical proof, and guarantee.

### VoC Quote Rules (for creative, alongside headlines)
- 3 per angle.
- At least one with self-deprecating humor.
- Short enough to fit on a static ad.

### Math & Claims Rules
- Only use client-approved figures — never extrapolate or scale.
- Never round down time claims (24-48hrs is not "24 hours").
- Never invent speed claims from feature descriptions.
- All stats require source documentation and compliance approval.
- If the math doesn't work backwards, remove the claim.

## Recurring Mistakes

Unique mistakes observed across real work. Run through before finalizing.

1. **All headlines the same type.** All Solution-Aware, all Actual Self, all ending the same way. Use the type index to check variety.
2. **Product-first instead of person-first.** "Brand X supports gut health with ingredient Y" vs. "You love your girls. But something might be living inside them." Headline = their life. Body = product.
3. **Softening the problem along with the solution.** Compliance applies to product claims only — the problem is unregulated. Keep aggressive energy for the problem, soften only the solution half.
4. **Hooks that swap between angles.** If a headline works for any angle, it's not specific enough to that angle's mechanism.
5. **Proof points as sentences.** "CoreBiome tributyrin: 55% bioavailable, reaches the colon" is too long. "55% bioavailable to colon" is a proof point.
6. **One subheading per angle.** Need 2-3 that rotate based on headline type.
7. **Same CTA repeated across concepts.** CTAs vary by concept and complete the argument the headline started.
8. **Risk reversal buried as fine print.** For new products, the guarantee can be headline-level prominent.
9. **Starting with mechanism instead of customer pain.** Research the pain first, then map the mechanism to their suffering.
10. **Fake persona names.** "The Exhausted Supplement Stacker" works. "Alex, 42" is invented and useless.
11. **Assuming viewer context.** "Everything helped a little." Everything WHAT? Helped with WHAT? Always specify.
12. **Earnest copy where humor was available.** If the VoC data is funny, the headlines should be too.
13. **Orphan evocative micro-moments on image-light formats.** "I stopped flying because of the seatbelt." "Money well spent." "Not a miracle." "I finally felt supported." These are emotionally loaded but only land if the reader already knows what the ad is about. They read as captions that need the image. On **image-forward** formats (real-person photography, hero images, comparison charts) the image can carry the category and the moment can hold the headline. On **image-light** formats (IG Stories Widget, flyer / typographic) they fail the first-read anchor test — pair the moment with a category/mechanism/price anchor, or replace it with a direct statement.
14. **VoC Editorial headlines written instead of lifted.** The VoC Editorial format sells because it reads as a real testimonial quote, not an ad headline. If you *write* the headline, the format dies — viewers clock the difference between "I liked the support" (real) and "Support that actually shows up for you" (written). Pull the headline verbatim (or lightly tightened for punctuation / length) from an actual review in the brand's review samples. Subheading functions as the byline ("A member, one year into her medical weight loss journey."). Same applies to any "fake testimonial" layout — Tweet format, Reddit post, user-comment. If the format mimics a real user saying it, the words have to come from a real user saying it.
15. **Native-UI format voice-register mismatch.** When a layout mimics a native platform surface — IG Stories caption-with-background, tweet, Reddit post, iMessage DM, YouTube comment — the headline must match the voice register people actually use on that surface. Stories captions and DMs are first-person ("I can eat dinner in peace"). Tweets and Reddit posts range but tend first-person or observational. Breaking the voice register (third-person "She stopped flying" inside a native IG Stories widget) makes the ad read as an ad pretending to be native, which kills the format's scroll-stop power.
16. **Ad-unit incongruence.** The headline, the VoC quote block (if present), and the hero image must all tell the same story. A seatbelt-click headline + an airplane hero + a stool-and-shoe-tying VoC quote is three different moments stapled together. Viewers feel it even if they can't name it. Before ship, read the three elements as one unit: are they all naming the same micro-moment or the same mechanism? If the quote is about the stool and the image is the seatbelt, rewrite the quote — don't hope it reads as "related."

(For generic mistakes — clever-over-clear, made-up math, false dichotomies, AI tells — see [[Universal Copy Rules]].)

## Quality Checks

Run these before shipping any set of headlines.

- **Type variety:** Does this set include at least 5 different core types? Use a type index to verify.
- **Register variety:** Is every headline straight? Consider where humor or pattern interrupt would earn more attention.
- **Self variety:** Are all headlines Actual Self? Add at least one Ought and one Ideal.
- **Stage check:** Are all headlines targeting the same awareness stage? Ensure coverage across at least 2 stages in a multi-headline set. Over-indexing on Product-Aware (Stage 4) is the most common imbalance.
- **Swap test:** Can any headline move to a different angle without anyone noticing? If yes, it needs more angle-specific mechanism or pain language.
- **2-second test:** Cover the brand name and subheading. Does the headline alone communicate what this is about?
- **First-read anchor test (image-light formats only):** On IG Stories Widgets, flyers, and other typographic-dominant formats, cover everything except the headline. Does it alone signal category, specific mechanism, or price? On image-forward formats, the hero can carry category — skip this check.
- **Real-quote test (VoC Editorial / fake-testimonial formats only):** Is the headline a verbatim or lightly-tightened pull from a real review in the brand's review samples? If you can't point to the source review, the format is broken.
- **Voice-register test (native-UI formats only):** On IG Stories Widget, tweet, Reddit, iMessage, or any layout that mimics a native platform surface, does the headline's voice match how real users write on that surface? Stories captions = first person. Break register, break the format.
- **Ad-unit congruence test (any format with multiple narrative elements):** Headline + VoC quote + hero image must all tell the same story. Read them as one unit. If one element is about a stool and another is about a seatbelt, rewrite until they cohere.
- **Language match:** Does every term in the headlines also appear on the PDP/landing page?
- **Closer variety:** Do more than 2 headlines end the same way?
