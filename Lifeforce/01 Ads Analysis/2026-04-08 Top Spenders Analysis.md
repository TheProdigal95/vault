### Ad 01 — BFCM Core Product Aware

**Format:** Image
**Spend:** $18,933 | **ROAS:** 0.158 | **CTR:** 1.23% | **CPM:** $94.63 | **Freq:** 3.89

**Visual Layout:** Vertical image with a warm, lifestyle photo of a man sitting comfortably at home, smiling, with his arm extended as though mid-blood draw. He appears mid-30s to 40s, fit, relaxed — the vibe is "this is easy and pleasant." Dark overlay gradient on the right side holds the text and feature list. Bottom-right corner features the Lifeforce logo in white.

**On-Screen Text (Verbatim):**
> THE MOST ADVANCED HEALTH TEST FOR
> ~~$599~~ **$199**
> **ENDS TONIGHT**
>
> - At-home blood test 4x/per year
> - Results interpreted by a real doctor
> - 45-minute doctor visit 4x/per year
> - Personalized health & longevity plan
>
> LIFEFORCE

**Style Notes:** Clean, premium healthcare aesthetic. Dark gradient overlay on warm photography. Sans-serif typography. The struck-through price in smaller text with the $199 in large bold creates a strong visual anchor. "ENDS TONIGHT" in all caps, large, bright — creates urgency. Bullet points use small icons (test tube, stethoscope, calendar, clipboard) next to each feature.

**Key Message:** Get concierge-level healthcare at a massive discount before the deal expires tonight.

**Persuasion Techniques:**
- **Price anchoring** — $599 struck through next to $199 creates a perception of 67% savings. The original price makes the offer feel like a steal.
- **Scarcity / urgency** — "ENDS TONIGHT" in bold, all-caps creates time pressure
- **Value stacking** — four distinct features listed as bullet points make the $199 feel like it covers a lot
- **Specificity** — "4x/per year," "45-minute," "50+ biomarkers" — concrete numbers build credibility
- **Authority** — "a real doctor" and "personalized health & longevity plan" signal medical legitimacy
- **Convenience framing** — "At-home" is the first word, addressing the main friction point

**What Makes It Work:** This is a straightforward discount-driven acquisition ad, and it's the top spender for a reason — it stacks value (four features), anchors price ($599 to $199), and creates urgency ("ENDS TONIGHT") all in a single glance. The warm lifestyle photo of a relaxed man makes the blood draw feel approachable rather than clinical. However, the 3.89 frequency is a red flag — audiences are seeing this nearly four times, which means creative fatigue is likely setting in. The 0.158 ROAS at $18.9K spend suggests the account is burning budget on this format without strong conversion returns.

## Patterns

### 1. Format Distribution

**8 images, 4 videos.** Images dominate the top spender list at a 2:1 ratio.

However, the story is nuanced. The top two spenders by far are an image (#1, $18.9K) and a video (#2, $16.8K), and together they account for more than the other 10 ads combined. Videos carry the highest CPMs ($85-88), likely because they're being used for broad awareness and retargeting with premium placements. Images show a wider CPM range ($21-95), with the native-format images (tweets, comparisons) running at dramatically lower CPMs ($21-67) than the polished sale images ($67-95).

The best-performing formats by ROAS are both images: Comparison-Price (0.321) and Comparison-Clinician (0.156). The best CTR performers are a video (Ashley T at 15.76%) and a video (Supplements Alone at 11.31%), but the tweet images also punch well above average (5.53% and 2.91%). The takeaway: images convert better, but the best scroll-stoppers are split between video UGC and native-disguised image formats.

### 2. Offer/Discount Dependency

**7 of 12 ads lead with or prominently feature price.** Ads 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 10 all include specific dollar amounts ($199, $49, $150 off). This account is heavily discount-dependent.

The three highest-spend ads (1, 2, 3) account for $49K in spend, and two of them are nearly identical discount ads. This is a problem. When you look at the best-converting ads by ROAS, neither of the top two (Comparison-Price at 0.321, Ashley T at 0.290) leads with a discount. The Comparison ad mentions price as context ($599 vs. $499) rather than as a deal, and Ashley T doesn't mention price at all. The ads that lead hardest with discounts (Ad 01 at 0.158, Ad 03 at 0.138, Ad 04 at 0.075) have mediocre-to-poor ROAS.

The account is spending the most money on the format (sale ads) that converts the worst. The formats that convert best (comparison, UGC testimonial) are getting the least spend.

### 3. Tony Robbins Dependency

**8 of 12 ads feature or mention Tony Robbins.** Ads 2, 3, 4, 5, 7 (by brand association), 9, 10, and the brand itself is "Created with Tony Robbins." Only Ads 1, 6, 8, 11, and 12 don't feature him directly.

Tony is the gravitational center of this account's creative strategy. He appears on camera in the two video ads (2, 5), is name-dropped in the tweet ads (4, 10), and is credited as co-creator in the sale ad (3). His presence correlates with higher spend — the algorithm is spending more on Tony-featuring ads. But his presence does NOT correlate with higher ROAS. The two best ROAS ads (Comparison-Price at 0.321 and Ashley T at 0.290) don't feature Tony at all.

This suggests the account is over-indexed on Tony as a creative crutch. His name and face drive attention (useful for prospecting), but the ads that actually convert buyers lean on product comparison and relatable testimonials rather than celebrity authority. There's a ceiling to what Tony alone can close.

### 4. TRT vs. Core Split

The account divides into two distinct product lines:

| **Core (general health/longevity)** | 1, 3, 7, 8, 9, 11, 12 | $43,371 | 0.175 |

Core ads are outperforming TRT ads on ROAS by nearly 3x. This is a significant finding. The TRT ads are getting heavy spend (especially Ad 2 at $16.8K), but their conversion rates are consistently poor. The best TRT ad by ROAS is Ad 05 at 0.098 — still below every single Core ad except Ad 09.

This doesn't mean TRT is a bad product or audience. It likely means the TRT creative isn't doing its job at the bottom of the funnel. The TRT ads are strong at generating interest (Ad 04 has 2.91% CTR, Ad 10 has 5.53% CTR) but weak at closing. The creative may need more specific objection-handling, more social proof from real TRT users, or a stronger conversion mechanism beyond "Start for $49."

### 7. What's Missing

**Creative gaps based on what's NOT in the top 12:**

1. **Women's hormones** — Every TRT ad targets men. There is zero creative addressing women's hormonal health (perimenopause, menopause, estrogen, progesterone). The only woman in the entire set is Ashley T, and she's talking about prediabetes, not hormones. This is a massive untapped audience.

2. **Prediabetes/metabolic health depth** — Ashley T's ad proves this angle works (15.76% CTR, 0.290 ROAS), but there's only ONE ad exploring it. There should be an entire creative suite around metabolic health: pre-diabetic detection, insulin resistance, blood sugar optimization. The creative director notes Ashley's ad lacks specificity — imagine a version with real biomarker numbers like the TRT tweet ads use.

3. **Supplement-specific creative** — Lifeforce sells supplements in addition to testing/TRT, but not a single top spender focuses on a specific supplement product. Every ad is about the testing/clinician service. There's no creative for individual supplement positioning.

4. **UGC testimonials beyond Ashley T** — Ashley's ad is the best performer in the set, but she's the only testimonial creator. Where are the male UGC testimonials? Where are the couples? Where are the older adults? One UGC voice is not a strategy — it's a lucky hit.

5. **Doctor/clinician-led content** — Despite "see a real doctor" being a key selling point, there's no ad featuring an actual Lifeforce clinician speaking about the service. A whitelisted doctor ad (similar to IM8's Dr. James DiNicolantonio whiteboard format) could be extremely effective.

6. **Specific biomarker education** — The phone mockup in Ad 07 shows biomarker charts, but no ad actually explains what biomarkers are being tested and why they matter. Educational content about Vitamin D, testosterone, insulin, cortisol — and what your numbers should be — could be a strong awareness play.

7. **Long-form transformation stories** — There's no "90-day journey" video following a real customer through the Lifeforce process. IM8's top performer is literally this format (90-day progression). A real customer's bloodwork results at Day 1 vs. Day 90 would be incredibly compelling.

### 8. Tweet Format Performance

The two tweet-format ads represent the highest-CTR image ads in the account:

| Ad | CTR | CPM | ROAS | Hook |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ad 10 — Stress/Low T | 5.53% | $21.94 | 0.035 | Symptom list |
| Ad 04 — 1 in 3 Men | 2.91% | $29.14 | 0.075 | Statistical claim |

Both use an identical template: Twitter/X dark-mode UI, fitness profile picture, text-only post with a before/after transformation photo below. Both mention Tony Robbins and close with "Start for $49" / "Get started for $49."

The tweet format is the most cost-efficient reach play in the account. Both ads run at CPMs under $30 — roughly 3x cheaper than the polished sale ads. They work because they bypass ad detection entirely: users process them as organic social content, which means they read the full text before realizing it's an ad.

**However, the ROAS is terrible on both.** The symptom version (0.035) and statistical version (0.075) are among the worst converters. This suggests the tweet format is an excellent awareness/consideration driver but a poor closer. The people who click may not be ready to buy — they're curious about low T but not in active purchase mode.

**The statistical version (Ad 04) outperforms the symptom version (Ad 10) on ROAS by 2x** despite having lower CTR. This confirms the creative director's observation: specific numbers (300-500 ng/dL, 600-900+ ng/dL) attract more qualified buyers than generic symptom lists. The people who recognize testosterone numbers are further along their research journey and closer to a purchase decision.

The opportunity: pair the tweet format's scroll-stopping power with a stronger mid-funnel element. A tweet that leads with specific data AND includes a compelling reason to act now (not just "$49") could bridge the gap between attention and conversion.

### 9. Comparison Ads

The two comparison ads are the most interesting format in the account:

| Ad | ROAS | CTR | CPM | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ad 08 — Comparison Price | 0.321 | 2.49% | $66.60 | Feature + price side-by-side |
| Ad 12 — Comparison Clinician | 0.156 | 3.59% | $51.92 | Doctor access checklist |

**Both outperform the account average on ROAS and CTR.** The Comparison-Price ad is the #1 converter in the entire set.

What makes the comparison format work for Lifeforce specifically:

1. **It addresses the "why not just go to my doctor?" objection.** The biggest competitor for Lifeforce isn't another health startup — it's inertia and existing healthcare. The comparison format directly answers "what does this give me that I don't already have?"

2. **It positions Lifeforce as premium, not cheap.** Both ads show Lifeforce at a higher price point ($599 vs. $499). This is counterintuitive for direct response, but it works because it signals quality and attracts buyers who are willing to invest in their health. The marginal price difference ($100/year) makes the feature gap feel absurd.

3. **The clinician version has higher CTR (3.59% vs. 2.49%) but lower ROAS (0.156 vs. 0.321).** This suggests the doctor/credibility angle scroll-stops effectively but the price/value angle closes better. The ideal comparison ad might combine both: show the doctor AND the price.

4. **Neither ad has been given significant budget.** Combined spend is $3,756 — less than what Ad 06 (ROAS 0.049) gets alone. This format should be the centerpiece of the account's creative strategy, not an afterthought.

### 10. The Ashley T Signal

Ad 11 (Ashley T Prediabetic) has the best CTR (15.76%) and second-best ROAS (0.290) in the entire account, but it's only getting $1,373 in spend. This is the single biggest strategic question in the analysis.

**Why is the best-performing creative getting the least budget?**

Possible explanations:
1. **Audience size** — The ad may be targeting a narrow audience that's close to saturation at low spend. The 1.68 frequency is still healthy, so this isn't the issue yet.
2. **Algorithmic learning phase** — At $1,373, the ad may not have exited the learning phase with enough conversion data for the algorithm to scale confidently.
3. **Account structure** — The ad may be in a campaign or ad set with budget caps that prevent scaling, or competing against higher-spend ads for budget allocation.
4. **Recency** — The ad may have been launched recently and hasn't had time to scale.

Regardless of the cause, the signal is clear: **UGC testimonial + hidden health problem + relatable female voice = the highest-performing creative combination in the account.** The 15.76% CTR is not a fluke — it's 4x higher than the next-best image ad. And the 0.290 ROAS at a $25.81 CPM means it's converting cheaply.

**What the account should do:**
1. Remove budget constraints on this ad and let it scale
2. Create 3-5 more UGC testimonials using the same structure (relatable person + "everything looked normal" hook + specific health discovery)
3. Add the specificity that the creative director notes is missing — biomarker numbers, before/after bloodwork, named conditions — to make the next versions even more compelling
4. Test male voices using the same format for the TRT audience, where UGC is currently nonexistent
5. Test the prediabetes/metabolic health angle as a full creative suite, not a single ad
