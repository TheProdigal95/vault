# Creative Diversity Audit

Portfolio-level audit for creative balance. Run at batch planning (before script/brief assignments) and between batches (retros on what winners tell us about the next batch's focus). Uses the [[Creative Strategy Matrix]] for shared vocabulary — awareness stages, matrix cells, pain × persona intersections.

**Companion docs:** [[Creative Strategy Matrix]] · [[Batch Template]] (Concept Validation) · [[Headline & Text Hook Criteria]] (Tactics Layer)

---

## Why Creative Diversity Matters

- **Scale requires new reach.** Every angle and format reaches a subset of potential audience. Growing beyond existing demand requires creative that speaks to different pain/desire buckets, different personas, and different awareness stages.
- **Protection against fatigue.** Any creative wears out. A diverse portfolio means no single piece carries the whole account. When one angle softens, others absorb the load.
- **Learning velocity.** Testing different matrix cells generates more signal faster. When everything is a variation of the same concept, you learn one thing slowly.

---

## Five-Dimension Audit

### 1. Coverage

How many distinct pain/desire buckets are represented in active creative? How many distinct personas? How many awareness stages? How many distinct messaging angles? Broader coverage = more surface area for finding winners.

### 2. Concentration

What percentage of spend or top performers fall into a single matrix cell? A single pain/desire bucket? A single awareness stage? High concentration in any one dimension is the primary risk signal.

**Concentration signals:**
- Multiple winners in the same pain × persona cell — proven one thing but haven't expanded
- All top performers at the same awareness stage (usually Stage 4-5) — harvesting existing intent but not building new demand
- All winning formats are the same — format bet hasn't been tested
- All hooks draw on the same source (all pain agitation, all social proof) — one-dimensional persuasion

**Gap signals:**
- Pain/desire buckets with no representation in top performers — untested demand
- Personas that appear only in losing or untested creative — haven't found the right angle/format
- Missing awareness stages, especially Stage 1 (Unaware) and Stage 3 (Solution-Aware) — commonly skipped
- No variation in hook tactics across winners

### 3. Format Spread

How many distinct asset types and visual formats are in the portfolio? Is one format carrying disproportionate weight? Format concentration limits the audience segments you can reach effectively.

**Concentration signals:**
- One format (e.g., UGC talking head) represents >50% of active creative — format bet is untested
- All winners are the same aspect ratio or placement type — reach is artificially narrowed
- Static and video never tested against each other for the same angle

**Gap signals:**
- Entire format categories untested (no statics, no long-form, no native screenshot)
- Winning angles only exist in one format — unknown whether the angle or the format drove performance

### 4. Hook Diversity

Across top performers, how many distinct hook tactics are represented (see Tactics Layer in [[Headline & Text Hook Criteria]])? How many distinct psychological triggers? If all winners use the same tactic, investigate whether others were properly tested.

### 5. Stage Balance

Is there top-of-funnel coverage (Stage 1-2)? Middle-funnel (Stage 3)? Bottom-funnel (Stage 4-5)? Accounts that are all bottom-funnel harvest existing intent but cannot scale.

## Exploitation vs Exploration

The tension in any creative portfolio is between deepening what works and expanding into new territory.

**Exploitation** — Iterating on proven angles. Deepening coverage of winning cells. Lower risk, more predictable returns, limited growth ceiling.

**Exploration** — Testing new matrix cells. New pain/desire buckets, new personas, new awareness stages, new formats. Higher variance, required for growth.

A healthy portfolio does both. Most volume runs on proven angles while a consistent portion tests new territory. The ratio shifts by account stage: earlier accounts need more exploration; mature accounts need more exploitation balanced with exploration to avoid stagnation.

### Warning Signs of Too Much Exploitation
- All new creative is a variation of the same concept
- The winning angle hasn't changed in months
- Top performers are all in the same matrix cell
- Account looks efficient but isn't growing

### Warning Signs of Too Much Exploration
- No pattern has emerged across tests
- Many one-off concepts, no consistent winners
- The account is learning but not compounding

## What to Build Next

After auditing, prioritize:

1. **Proven cells with no recent creative** — you know it works; demand is uncaptured. Build more here first.
2. **Adjacent cells to winners** — similar persona or pain/desire bucket to a winner. Likely responsive, lower risk.
3. **Completely untested cells** — highest uncertainty, required for expansion. Smaller tests to qualify before scaling.
4. **Missing funnel stages** — if Stage 1-2 is absent and budget allows, this is a structural gap worth closing. Top-of-funnel coverage is what makes scaling possible.

Prioritize: known signal first, adjacent hypotheses second, net-new exploration third.

## Integration with Batch Planning

- **Pre-batch:** Run the 5-dimension audit on prior batches before drafting the new Batch Plan. The audit surfaces concentration risk and gap opportunities that feed the Concept Validation gate.
- **Between-batch retros:** "What does T001 winner data tell us about concentration risk going into T002?"
- **Relationship to Concept Validation:** The diversity audit is the batch-level portfolio view; the Concept Validation's differentiation rule ([[Batch Template]]) is the concept-level view.
